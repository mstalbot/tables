id=\"apjlaad6des1\" name=\"apjlaad6des1\">1.\u00a0Introduction</h2><div class=\"article-text\" data-mobile-collapse=\"\"><p>The flat rotation curves in local spiral galaxies (Sofue &amp; Rubin <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjlaad6debib23\" id=\"fnref-apjlaad6debib23\">2001</a>) are a basic piece of evidence for the existence of dark matter in the universe. At high redshift, studies of rotation curves are limited by the low surface brightnesses of the outskirts of galaxies. The recent report of decreasing (as a function of galactocentric radius) rotation curves in massive galaxies at redshift <em>z</em>\u00a0=\u00a00.9\u20132.4 has suggested that dark matter does not dominate the dynamical mass at\u00a0 distances larger than 1.3\u20131.5 times the galaxy effective radius (Genzel et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjlaad6debib12\" id=\"fnref-apjlaad6debib12\">2017</a>). These findings are influenced by the effect of pressure support in turbulent disks, which is more commonly seen in high-<em>z</em> gas-rich galaxies. Probing rotation curves is difficult at high <em>z</em>; however, this can be aided by strong gravitational lensing (Stark et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjlaad6debib25\" id=\"fnref-apjlaad6debib25\">2008</a>).</p><p>Strong gravitational lensing by massive clusters can boost the signal of background galaxies, offering the opportunity to probe in great detail their internal structures (Swinbank et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjlaad6debib27\" id=\"fnref-apjlaad6debib27\">2010</a>). The Bullet Cluster (1ES 0657-558) consists of two merging galaxy clusters at <em>z</em>\u00a0=\u00a00.296 (Tucker et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjlaad6debib29\" id=\"fnref-apjlaad6debib29\">1998</a>), and its high mass (Brada\u010d et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjlaad6debib4\" id=\"fnref-apjlaad6debib4\">2006</a>; Paraficz et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjlaad6debib21\" id=\"fnref-apjlaad6debib21\">2016</a>) causes the field to display an exceptionally high number of lensed submillimeter galaxies (Johansson et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjlaad6debib15\" id=\"fnref-apjlaad6debib15\">2010</a>). Here we present the physical properties of galaxy SMM\u00a0J0658 at <em>z</em>\u00a0=\u00a02.7793 (Johansson et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjlaad6debib14\" id=\"fnref-apjlaad6debib14\">2012</a>), hereafter called the \"Cosmic Seagull\" (Figure <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" href=\"#apjlaad6def1\">1</a>). This galaxy lies close to a lensing critical line, resulting in one of the largest magnifications reported to date (<em>\u03bc</em> up to ~50), which facilitates precise measurements of the kinematics at the outskirts of this dusty, star-forming galaxy. Throughout the text we assume a \u039bCDM cosmology with \u03a9<sub><em>m</em></sub>\u00a0=\u00a00.3, \u03a9<sub>\u039b</sub>\u00a0=\u00a00.7 and <em>h</em>\u00a0=\u00a00.7.</p><figure xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" id=\"apjlaad6def1\" tabindex=\"-1\" role=\"group\" class=\"boxout boxout-bdr-grey keyboard-focus-only\" data-toolbar-type=\"figure\" data-toolbar-link=\"apjlaad6def1\" data-toolbar-img=\"https://cdn.iopscience.com/images/2041-8205/863/2/L16/Full/apjlaad6def1_lr.jpg\" data-toolbar-title=\"Figure 1.\"><figure><div class=\"panzoom-container\"><div class=\"panzoom-parent\" style=\"overflow: hidden; position: relative;\"><img class=\"panzoom\" alt=\"Figure 1.\" src=\"https://static.iopscience.com/2.44.0/img/lazy-loading-placeholder.gif\" data-src=\"https://cdn.iopscience.com/images/2041-8205/863/2/L16/Full/apjlaad6def1_lr.jpg\" style=\"transform: matrix(1, 0, 0, 1, 0, 0); backface-visibility: hidden; transform-origin: 50% 50% 0px; cursor: move; transition: transform 200ms ease-in-out 0s;\"></img></div><div class=\"buttons zoom-tools\"><button class=\"zoom-in\"><span class=\"icon-zoomin\"></span>\n\t                 Zoom In\n\t\t\t\t</button><button class=\"zoom-out\"><span class=\"icon-zoomout\"></span>\n\t                 Zoom Out\n\t\t\t\t</button><span class=\"mobile-block\"><button class=\"reset\"><span class=\"icon-loop\"></span>\n\t                     Reset image size\n\t\t\t\t\t</button></span></div></div><figcaption><div class=\"article-text figure-caption\"><p><strong>Figure 1.</strong>\u00a0<em>Hubble Space Telescope</em> (<em>HST</em>) composite\u00a0(WFPC2/F814W, WFPC3/F110W and F160W) image and contours (intervals from 20% to 80% of 1.16 mJy beam<sup>\u22121</sup> peak intensity) from Atacama Large Millimeter/submillimeter Array (ALMA) band-6 continuum centered at 231 GHz showing the structure of the \"Cosmic Seagull\" near a bright galaxy\u00a0(GalB). behind the Bullet Cluster (the \"Cosmic Seagull\"). The kinematic information at the outskirts of a galaxy at <span xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"inline-eqn\"><span class=\"tex\"><span class=\"texImage\"><img src=\"https://static.iopscience.com/2.44.0/img/lazy-loading-placeholder.gif\" data-src=\"https://cdn.iopscience.com/images/2041-8205/863/2/L16/Full/apjlaad6deieqn95.gif\" alt=\"$z\\simeq 2.8$\" align=\"top\"></img></span><script type=\"math/tex\">z\\simeq 2.8</script></span></span> is facilitated by one of the largest gravitational magnifications ever recorded (<span xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"inline-eqn\"><span class=\"tex\"><span class=\"texImage\"><img src=\"https://static.iopscience.com/2.44.0/img/lazy-loading-placeholder.gif\" data-src=\"https://cdn.iopscience.com/images/2041-8205/863/2/L16/Full/apjlaad6deieqn96.gif\" alt=\"$\\mu \\lesssim 50$\" align=\"top\"></img></span><script type=\"math/tex\">\\mu \\lesssim 50</script></span></span>). The strong lensing of this system reveals its internal dynamics, stellar mass, and gas distribution, providing an excellent opportunity to explore the internal structure of a normal (following the \"main sequence\") high-<em>z</em> galaxy that would otherwise be undetectably faint. This exceptionally magnified object observed at sub-arcsecond resolution provides us with a detailed rotational curve near the peak of the galaxy mass assembly epoch. Our ALMA data have shown that SMM\u00a0J0658 is a disk-like galaxy, with a dynamical mass of\u00a0 <span xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"inline-eqn\"><span class=\"tex\"><span class=\"texImage\"><img src=\"https://static.iopscience.com/2.44.0/img/lazy-loading-placeholder.gif\" data-src=\"https://cdn.iopscience.com/images/2041-8205/863/2/L16/Full/apjlaad6deieqn97.gif\" alt=\"$(6.3\\pm 0.7)\\times {10}^{10}{M}_{\\odot }$\" align=\"top\"></img></span><script type=\"math/tex\">(6.3\\pm 0.7)\\times {10}^{10}{M}_{\\odot }</script></span></span> inside <em>r</em>\u00a0=\u00a02.6\u00a0\u00b1\u00a00.1 kpc, where most of its baryonic mass is\u00a0probably dominated by the molecular gas content (<span xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"inline-eqn\"><span class=\"tex\"><span class=\"texImage\"><img src=\"https://static.iopscience.com/2.44.0/img/lazy-loading-placeholder.gif\" data-src=\"https://cdn.iopscience.com/images/2041-8205/863/2/L16/Full/apjlaad6deieqn98.gif\" alt=\"${f}_{\\mathrm{gas}}\\leqslant 80\\pm 20$\" align=\"top\"></img></span><script type=\"math/tex\">{f}_{\\mathrm{gas}}\\leqslant 80\\pm 20</script></span></span>%).
