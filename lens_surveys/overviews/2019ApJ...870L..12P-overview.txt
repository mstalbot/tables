itemprop=\"description\">\n             <P>The discovery of the first strongly lensed (<I>\u03bc</I>\u00a0\u2248\u00a050) quasar at <I>z</I>\u00a0&gt;\u00a06 (J0439+1634) represents a breakthrough in our understanding of the early universe. We derive the theoretical consequences of the new discovery. We predict that the observed population of <I>z</I>\u00a0&gt;\u00a06 quasars should contain many sources with magnifications <I>\u03bc</I> <img src=\"https://cdn.iopscience.com/icons/Entities/lesssim.gif\" alt=\"lesssim\" align=\"baseline\" /> 10 and with image separations below the resolution threshold. Additionally, current selection criteria could have missed a substantial population of lensed <I>z</I>\u00a0&gt;\u00a06 quasars, due to the contamination of the drop-out photometric bands by lens galaxies. We argue that this predicted population of lensed <I>z</I>\u00a0&gt;\u00a06 quasars would be misclassified and mixed up with low-<I>z</I> galaxies. We quantify the fraction of undetected quasars as a function of the slope of the bright end of the quasar luminosity function, <I>\u03b2</I>. For <I>\u03b2</I> <img src=\"https://cdn.iopscience.com/icons/Entities/lesssim.gif\" alt=\"lesssim\" align=\"baseline\" /> 3.6, we predict that the undetected lensed quasars could reach half of the population, whereas for <I>\u03b2</I> <img src=\"https://cdn.iopscience.com/icons/Entities/gsim.gif\" alt=\"gsim\" align=\"baseline\" /> 4.5 the vast majority of the <I>z</I>\u00a0&gt;\u00a06 quasar population is lensed and still undetected. This would significantly affect the <I>z</I>\u00a0&gt;\u00a06 quasar luminosity function and inferred black hole mass distributions, with profound implications for the ultraviolet, X-ray, and infrared cosmic backgrounds and the growth of early quasars.</P> </div>\n    <!-- End abstract -->\n<!-- Start Export citation and abstract tools -->\n\t<p>\n\t\t<small>Export citation and itemprop=\"articleBody\" class=\"wd-jnl-art-full-text article-text\">\n<h2 class=\"header-anchor\" id=\"apjlaaf86as1\" name=\"apjlaaf86as1\">1.\u00a0Introduction</h2><div class=\"article-text\" data-mobile-collapse=\"\"><p>The discovery of the first strongly lensed quasar at <em>z</em>\u00a0&gt;\u00a06 (Fan et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjlaaf86abib15\" id=\"fnref-apjlaaf86abib15\">2018</a>) marks a breakthrough nearly two decades after the first theoretical prediction of their existence (Wyithe &amp; Loeb <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjlaaf86abib40\" id=\"fnref-apjlaaf86abib40\">2002</a>). Fan et al. (<a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjlaaf86abib15\" id=\"fnref-apjlaaf86abib15\">2018</a>) report the detection of J0439+1634, a <em>z</em>\u00a0=\u00a06.51 quasar whose best-fit model predicts three images and a total magnification <em>\u03bc</em>\u00a0=\u00a051.3\u00a0\u00b1\u00a01.4. This lensed quasar is the first example of this class detected during the reionization epoch (Barkana &amp; Loeb <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjlaaf86abib3\" id=\"fnref-apjlaaf86abib3\">2001</a>; Fan et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjlaaf86abib14\" id=\"fnref-apjlaaf86abib14\">2006</a>).</p><p>Wyithe &amp; Loeb (<a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjlaaf86abib40\" id=\"fnref-apjlaaf86abib40\">2002</a>) suggested the possibility of a high fraction (up to ~1/3) of lensed sources among high-<em>z</em> quasars, due to the large lensing optical depths reached at <em>z</em> <img src=\"https://cdn.iopscience.com/icons/Entities/gsim.gif\" alt=\"gsim\" align=\"baseline\" /> 6. This fraction of the then-known population of high-<em>z</em> quasars would have had their observed flux magnified by factors <em>\u03bc</em> <img src=\"https://cdn.iopscience.com/icons/Entities/gsim.gif\" alt=\"gsim\" align=\"baseline\" /> 10. Thus far, none of the ~150 quasars observed at <em>z</em> <img src=\"https://cdn.iopscience.com/icons/Entities/gsim.gif\" alt=\"gsim\" align=\"baseline\" /> 6 (Ba\u00f1ados et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjlaaf86abib1\" id=\"fnref-apjlaaf86abib1\">2018</a>) have shown evidence for lensing.</p><p>As suggested by Fan et al. (<a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjlaaf86abib15\" id=\"fnref-apjlaaf86abib15\">2018</a>), this lack of lensed sources could be accounted for by a selection bias. The selection of <em>z</em> <img src=\"https://cdn.iopscience.com/icons/Entities/gsim.gif\" alt=\"gsim\" align=\"baseline\" /> 6 quasars requires a non-detection (or at least a strong drop) in the drop-out band at shorter wavelengths than the Lyman break (912  \u00c5). In their detection paper, Fan et al. (<a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjlaaf86abib15\" id=\"fnref-apjlaaf86abib15\">2018</a>) point out that the presence of a lens galaxy along the line of sight of the lensed quasar injects flux into the drop-out bands. This effect is very relevant for partly resolved images, a likely occurrence for high-<em>z</em> sources. This can thus constitute a relevant issue in the selection of <em>z</em> <img src=\"https://cdn.iopscience.com/icons/Entities/gsim.gif\" alt=\"gsim\" align=\"baseline\" /> 6 lensed quasars. As the drop-out bands also contain transmission windows of the intergalactic medium (IGM), Fan et al. (<a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjlaaf86abib15\" id=\"fnref-apjlaaf86abib15\">2018</a>) caution that great care is needed in separating the continuum of the lens galaxy from the transmission spikes of the IGM.</p><p>This new discovery has several key implications. First, it suggests the likely existence of a population of more modestly lensed quasars that are thus far undetected (Fan et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjlaaf86abib15\" id=\"fnref-apjlaaf86abib15\">2018</a>). Second, if this population of undetected quasars does exist, it could potentially impact in a very significant way the luminosity and mass functions of the earliest populations of supermassive black holes (BHs). In this regard, Wyithe &amp; Loeb (<a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjlaaf86abib40\" id=\"fnref-apjlaaf86abib40\">2002</a>) pointed out that the inclusion of a magnification bias in the high-<em>z</em> quasar surveys could have a major effect on the abundance of massive halos (<em>M</em><sub><em>h</em></sub> <img src=\"https://cdn.iopscience.com/icons/Entities/gsim.gif\" alt=\"gsim\" align=\"baseline\" /> 10<sup>13</sup> <span xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"inline-eqn\"><span class=\"tex\"><span class=\"texImage\"><img src=\"https://static.iopscience.com/2.44.0/img/lazy-loading-placeholder.gif\" data-src=\"https://cdn.iopscience.com/images/2041-8205/870/2/L12/Full/apjlaaf86aieqn1.gif\" alt=\"${M}_{\\odot }$\" align=\"top\"></img></span><script type=\"math/tex\">{M}_{\\odot }</script></span></span>), for which the mass function is very steep.</p><p>In this Letter we derive the theoretical consequences of the discovery of the first strongly lensed quasar in the epoch of reionization (Fan et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjlaaf86abib15\" id=\"fnref-apjlaaf86abib15\">2018</a>). Throughout the Letter we use the latest values of the cosmological parameters from the Planck Collaboration et al. (<a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjlaaf86abib33\" id=\"fnref-apjlaaf86abib33\">2018</a>).</p></div>\n<h2 class=\"header-anchor\" id=\"apjlaaf86as2\" name=\"apjlaaf86as2\">2.\u00a0Theoretical Framework</h2><div class=\"article-text\" data-mobile-collapse=\"\"><p>We employ the formalism developed in Pei (<a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjlaaf86abib32\" id=\"fnref-apjlaaf86abib32\">1995</a>) to analytically compute the magnification probability distribution, <em>P</em>(<em>\u03bc</em>), due to cosmologically distributed galaxies. We define <em>\u03bc</em> as the total magnification of a source at a redshift <em>z</em><sub><em>s</em></sub>, due to lenses at redshift <em>z</em>'. The probability distribution of magnifications larger than <em>\u03bc</em> is</p><div xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"display-eqn\" id=\"apjlaaf86aeqn1\"><span class=\"tex\"><span class=\"texImage\"><img src=\"https://static.iopscience.com/2.44.0/img/lazy-loading-placeholder.gif\" data-src=\"https://cdn.iopscience.com/images/2041-8205/870/2/L12/Full/apjlaaf86aeqn1.gif\" alt=\"Equation (1)\"></img></span><script type=\"math/tex; mode=display\">\\begin{eqnarray}&&P(\\gt \\mu )={\\int }_{\\mu }^{\\infty }P(\\mu ^{\\prime} )d\\mu ^{\\prime} .\\end{eqnarray}\n\t\t\t\t\\tag{\n\t\t\t\t1\n\t\t\t\t}\n\t\t\t</script></span></div><p>\n</p><p>Following Pei (<a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjlaaf86abib31\" id=\"fnref-apjlaaf86abib31\">1993</a>, <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjlaaf86abib32\" id=\"fnref-apjlaaf86abib32\">1995</a>), we distinguish between the magnification <em>\u03bc</em>, relative to a filled beam in a smooth universe, and the magnification <em>A</em>, relative to an empty beam in a clumpy universe. Calling <span xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"inline-eqn\"><span class=\"tex\"><span class=\"texImage\"><img src=\"https://static.iopscience.com/2.44.0/img/lazy-loading-placeholder.gif\" data-src=\"https://cdn.iopscience.com/images/2041-8205/870/2/L12/Full/apjlaaf86aieqn2.gif\" alt=\"$\\bar{A}$\" align=\"top\"></img></span><script type=\"math/tex\">\\bar{A}</script></span></span> the mean magnification, the two variables are related by <span xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"inline-eqn\"><span class=\"tex\"><span class=\"texImage\"><img src=\"https://static.iopscience.com/2.44.0/img/lazy-loading-placeholder.gif\" data-src=\"https://cdn.iopscience.com/images/2041-8205/870/2/L12/Full/apjlaaf86aieqn3.gif\" alt=\"$\\mu \\equiv A/\\bar{A}$\" align=\"top\"></img></span><script type=\"math/tex\">\\mu \\equiv A/\\bar{A}</script></span></span>. We define the moment function <span xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"inline-eqn\"><span class=\"tex\"><span class=\"texImage\"><img src=\"https://static.iopscience.com/2.44.0/img/lazy-loading-placeholder.gif\" data-src=\"https://cdn.iopscience.com/images/2041-8205/870/2/L12/Full/apjlaaf86aieqn4.gif\" alt=\"$Z(s| {z}_{s})$\" align=\"top\"></img></span><script type=\"math/tex\">Z(s| {z}_{s})</script></span></span> as</p><div xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"display-eqn\" id=\"apjlaaf86aeqn2\"><span class=\"tex\"><span class=\"texImage\"><img src=\"https://static.iopscience.com/2.44.0/img/lazy-loading-placeholder.gif\" data-src=\"https://cdn.iopscience.com/images/2041-8205/870/2/L12/Full/apjlaaf86aeqn2.gif\" alt=\"Equation (2)\"></img></span><script type=\"math/tex; mode=display\">\\begin{eqnarray}\\begin{array}{rcl}Z(s| {z}_{s}) & = & {\\displaystyle \\int }_{0}^{{z}_{s}}{dz}^{\\prime} {\\displaystyle \\int }_{1}^{\\infty }{dA}\\rho (A,z^{\\prime} | {z}_{s})\\\\ & & \\times \\,({A}^{s}-1-0.4\\mathrm{ln}(10)A+s).\\end{array}\\end{eqnarray}\n\t\t\t\t\\tag{\n\t\t\t\t2\n\t\t\t\t}\n\t\t\t</script></span></div><p>The quantity <span xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"inline-eqn\"><span class=\"tex\"><span class=\"texImage\"><img src=\"https://static.iopscience.com/2.44.0/img/lazy-loading-placeholder.gif\" data-src=\"https://cdn.iopscience.com/images/2041-8205/870/2/L12/Full/apjlaaf86aieqn5.gif\" alt=\"$\\rho (A,z^{\\prime} | {z}_{s})$\" align=\"top\"></img></span><script type=\"math/tex\">\\rho (A,z^{\\prime} | {z}_{s})</script></span></span> is the mean number of lenses in the range (<span xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"inline-eqn\"><span class=\"tex\"><span class=\"texImage\"><img src=\"https://static.iopscience.com/2.44.0/img/lazy-loading-placeholder.gif\" data-src=\"https://cdn.iopscience.com/images/2041-8205/870/2/L12/Full/apjlaaf86aieqn6.gif\" alt=\"$z^{\\prime} $\" align=\"top\"></img></span><script type=\"math/tex\">z^{\\prime}</script></span></span>, <span xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"inline-eqn\"><span class=\"tex\"><span class=\"texImage\"><img src=\"https://static.iopscience.com/2.44.0/img/lazy-loading-placeholder.gif\" data-src=\"https://cdn.iopscience.com/images/2041-8205/870/2/L12/Full/apjlaaf86aieqn7.gif\" alt=\"$z^{\\prime} +{dz}^{\\prime} $\" align=\"top\"></img></span><script type=\"math/tex\">z^{\\prime} +{dz}^{\\prime}</script></span></span>) with magnifications in the range (<em>A</em>, <em>A</em> + <em>dA</em>) for a given source at a redshift <em>z</em><sub><em>s</em></sub>. The probability distribution function for <em>\u03bc</em> reads</p><div xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"display-eqn\" id=\"apjlaaf86aeqn3\"><span class=\"tex\"><span class=\"texImage\"><img src=\"https://static.iopscience.com/2.44.0/img/lazy-loading-placeholder.gif\" data-src=\"https://cdn.iopscience.com/images/2041-8205/870/2/L12/Full/apjlaaf86aeqn3.gif\" alt=\"Equation (3)\"></img></span><script type=\"math/tex; mode=display\">\\begin{eqnarray}&&P(\\mu )={\\mu }^{-1}{\\int }_{-\\infty }^{+\\infty }{ds}\\exp [-2\\pi {is}\\mathrm{ln}\\mu +Z(2\\pi {is}| {z}_{s})].\\end{eqnarray}\n\t\t\t\t\\tag{\n\t\t\t\t3\n\t\t\t\t}\n\t\t\t</script></span></div><p>With some analytical adjustments, this integral can be efficiently computed via a fast Fourier transform algorithm.</p><p>Assuming that the population of lenses is composed of galaxies with flat rotation curves modeled as a truncated singular isothermal sphere, their surface mass density profile can be written as</p><div xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"display-eqn\" id=\"apjlaaf86aeqn4\"><span class=\"tex\"><span class=\"texImage\"><img src=\"https://static.iopscience.com/2.44.0/img/lazy-loading-placeholder.gif\" data-src=\"https://cdn.iopscience.com/images/2041-8205/870/2/L12/Full/apjlaaf86aeqn4.gif\" alt=\"Equation (4)\"></img></span><script type=\"math/tex; mode=display\">\\begin{eqnarray}&&{\\rm{\\Sigma }}(R)=\\displaystyle \\frac{{\\sigma }_{v}^{2}}{2{GR}}{\\left(1+\\displaystyle \\frac{R}{{R}_{T}}\\right)}^{-2},\\end{eqnarray}\n\t\t\t\t\\tag{\n\t\t\t\t4\n\t\t\t\t}\n\t\t\t</script></span></div><p>where <em>\u03c3</em><sub><em>v</em></sub> is the line-of-sight velocity dispersion, <em>R</em> is the projected radius, <em>R</em><sub><em>T</em></sub> is the radius containing half of the projected mass, and <em>G</em> is the gravitational constant. The total mass is finite and equal to <span xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"inline-eqn\"><span class=\"tex\"><span class=\"texImage\"><img src=\"https://static.iopscience.com/2.44.0/img/lazy-loading-placeholder.gif\" data-src=\"https://cdn.iopscience.com/images/2041-8205/870/2/L12/Full/apjlaaf86aieqn8.gif\" alt=\"${M}_{T}=\\pi {\\sigma }_{v}^{2}{R}_{T}/G$\" align=\"top\"></img></span><script type=\"math/tex\">{M}_{T}=\\pi {\\sigma }_{v}^{2}{R}_{T}/G</script></span></span>. For such a population of lenses, calling <em>\u03b4</em>(<em>x</em>) the Dirac delta function</p><div xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"display-eqn\" id=\"apjlaaf86aeqn5\"><span class=\"tex\"><span class=\"texImage\"><img src=\"https://static.iopscience.com/2.44.0/img/lazy-loading-placeholder.gif\" data-src=\"https://cdn.iopscience.com/images/2041-8205/870/2/L12/Full/apjlaaf86aeqn5.gif\" alt=\"Equation (5)\"></img></span><script type=\"math/tex; mode=display\">\\begin{eqnarray}&&\\rho (A,z^{\\prime} | {z}_{s})=2\\tau (z^{\\prime} | {z}_{s}){\\int }_{0}^{\\infty }{dff}\\delta [A-A(f)].\\end{eqnarray}\n\t\t\t\t\\tag{\n\t\t\t\t5\n\t\t\t\t}\n\t\t\t</script></span></div><p>The coefficient <span xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"inline-eqn\"><span class=\"tex\"><span class=\"texImage\"><img src=\"https://static.iopscience.com/2.44.0/img/lazy-loading-placeholder.gif\" data-src=\"https://cdn.iopscience.com/images/2041-8205/870/2/L12/Full/apjlaaf86aieqn9.gif\" alt=\"$\\tau (z^{\\prime} | {z}_{s})$\" align=\"top\"></img></span><script type=\"math/tex\">\\tau (z^{\\prime} | {z}_{s})</script></span></span> is the strong lensing optical depth between source and lens galaxy at <span xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"inline-eqn\"><span class=\"tex\"><span class=\"texImage\"><img src=\"https://static.iopscience.com/2.44.0/img/lazy-loading-placeholder.gif\" data-src=\"https://cdn.iopscience.com/images/2041-8205/870/2/L12/Full/apjlaaf86aieqn10.gif\" alt=\"$z^{\\prime} $\" align=\"top\"></img></span><script type=\"math/tex\">z^{\\prime}</script></span></span> (see Pei <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjlaaf86abib32\" id=\"fnref-apjlaaf86abib32\">1995</a>). The factor <em>f</em> is defined as <span xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"inline-eqn\"><span class=\"tex\"><span class=\"texImage\"><img src=\"https://static.iopscience.com/2.44.0/img/lazy-loading-placeholder.gif\" data-src=\"https://cdn.iopscience.com/images/2041-8205/870/2/L12/Full/apjlaaf86aieqn11.gif\" alt=\"$f=l/{({a}_{\\mathrm{cr}}{R}_{T})}^{0.5}$\" align=\"top\"></img></span><script type=\"math/tex\">f=l/{({a}_{\\mathrm{cr}}{R}_{T})}^{0.5}</script></span></span>, where <em>l</em> is the unperturbed impact parameter, and <em>a</em><sub>cr</sub> is the critical impact parameter for double images. In Equation (<a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"eqnref\" href=\"#apjlaaf86aeqn5\">5</a>), <span xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"inline-eqn\"><span class=\"tex\"><span class=\"texImage\"><img src=\"https://static.iopscience.com/2.44.0/img/lazy-loading-placeholder.gif\" data-src=\"https://cdn.iopscience.com/images/2041-8205/870/2/L12/Full/apjlaaf86aieqn12.gif\" alt=\"$A(f)={A}_{+}(f)$\" align=\"top\"></img></span><script type=\"math/tex\">A(f)={A}_{+}(f)</script></span></span> for <span xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"inline-eqn\"><span class=\"tex\"><span class=\"texImage\"><img src=\"https://static.iopscience.com/2.44.0/img/lazy-loading-placeholder.gif\" data-src=\"https://cdn.iopscience.com/images/2041-8205/870/2/L12/Full/apjlaaf86aieqn13.gif\" alt=\"$f\\gt 1/r$\" align=\"top\"></img></span><script type=\"math/tex\">f\\gt 1/r</script></span></span> and <span xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"inline-eqn\"><span class=\"tex\"><span class=\"texImage\"><img src=\"https://static.iopscience.com/2.44.0/img/lazy-loading-placeholder.gif\" data-src=\"https://cdn.iopscience.com/images/2041-8205/870/2/L12/Full/apjlaaf86aieqn14.gif\" alt=\"$A(f)={A}_{+}(f)+{A}_{-}(f)$\" align=\"top\"></img></span><script type=\"math/tex\">A(f)={A}_{+}(f)+{A}_{-}(f)</script></span></span> for <em>f</em>\u00a0\u2264\u00a01/<em>r</em>, with</p><div xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"display-eqn\" id=\"apjlaaf86aeqn6\"><span class=\"tex\"><span class=\"texImage\"><img src=\"https://static.iopscience.com/2.44.0/img/lazy-loading-placeholder.gif\" data-src=\"https://cdn.iopscience.com/images/2041-8205/870/2/L12/Full/apjlaaf86aeqn6.gif\" alt=\"Equation (6)\"></img></span><script type=\"math/tex; mode=display\">\\begin{eqnarray}&&{A}_{\\pm }(f)=\\displaystyle \\frac{1}{2}\\left(\\displaystyle \\frac{{f}^{2}\\pm {fr}+2}{f{\\left[{\\left(f\\pm r\\right)}^{2}+4\\right]}^{1/2}}\\pm 1\\right),\\end{eqnarray}\n\t\t\t\t\\tag{\n\t\t\t\t6\n\t\t\t\t}\n\t\t\t</script></span></div><p>\n</p><div xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"display-eqn\" id=\"apjlaaf86aeqn7\"><span class=\"tex\"><span class=\"texImage\"><img src=\"https://static.iopscience.com/2.44.0/img/lazy-loading-placeholder.gif\" data-src=\"https://cdn.iopscience.com/images/2041-8205/870/2/L12/Full/apjlaaf86aeqn7.gif\" alt=\"Equation (7)\"></img></span><script type=\"math/tex; Following Pei (<a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjlaaf86abib32\" id=\"fnref-apjlaaf86abib32\">1995</a>), we assume <em>F</em>\u00a0~\u00a00.05 and we check that our results are unchanged throughout the range 0.01\u00a0&lt;\u00a0<em>F</em>\u00a0&lt;\u00a00.1, which covers the full domain of interest.</p><h3 id=\"apjlaaf86as2-1\" name=\"apjlaaf86as2-1\">2.1.\u00a0Distribution of Lens Galaxies</h3><div class=\"article-text\"><p>The theoretical framework described thus far to make predictions for the lensing probability <em>P</em>(&gt;<em>\u03bc</em>) has to be supplemented with a realistic cosmological distribution of foreground (<em>z</em> <img src=\"https://cdn.iopscience.com/icons/Entities/lesssim.gif\" alt=\"lesssim\" align=\"baseline\" /> 6) galaxies, assumed to be early type (E/S0) and distributed uniformly in space. The ultraviolet (UV) luminosity function for galaxies is modeled as a simple Schechter function (Schechter <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjlaaf86abib34\" id=\"fnref-apjlaaf86abib34\">1976</a>):</p><div xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"display-eqn\" id=\"apjlaaf86aeqn8\"><span class=\"tex\"><span class=\"texImage\"><img src=\"https://static.iopscience.com/2.44.0/img/lazy-loading-placeholder.gif\" data-src=\"https://cdn.iopscience.com/images/2041-8205/870/2/L12/Full/apjlaaf86aeqn8.gif\" alt=\"Equation (8)\"></img></span><script type=\"math/tex; mode=display\">\\begin{eqnarray}&&{\\rm{\\Phi }}(L)=\\displaystyle \\frac{{{\\rm{\\Phi }}}_{\\star }}{{L}_{\\star }}{\\left(\\displaystyle \\frac{L}{{L}_{\\star }}\\right)}^{{\\alpha }_{g}}\\exp \\left(-\\displaystyle \\frac{L}{{L}_{\\star }}\\right),\\end{eqnarray}\n\t\t\t\t\\tag{\n\t\t\t\t8\n\t\t\t\t}\n\t\t\t</script></span></div><p>where <em>L</em><sub><img src=\"https://cdn.iopscience.com/icons/Entities/sstarf.gif\" alt=\"sstarf\" align=\"baseline\" /></sub> is the break luminosity, \u03a6<sub><img src=\"https://cdn.iopscience.com/icons/Entities/sstarf.gif\" alt=\"sstarf\" align=\"baseline\" /></sub> is the number density of galaxies of luminosity <em>L</em><sub><img src=\"https://cdn.iopscience.com/icons/Entities/sstarf.gif\" alt=\"sstarf\" align=\"baseline\" /></sub>, and <em>\u03b1</em><sub><em>g</em></sub> is the slope of the faint end. Several surveys (e.g., Schmidt et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjlaaf86abib35\" id=\"fnref-apjlaaf86abib35\">2014</a>; Coe et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjlaaf86abib8\" id=\"fnref-apjlaaf86abib8\">2015</a>) suggest that the Schechter function correctly models the distribution of galaxies up to <em>z</em>\u00a0~\u00a06, and possibly up to <em>z</em>\u00a0~\u00a08 (Bouwens et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjlaaf86abib6\" id=\"fnref-apjlaaf86abib6\">2014</a>). We employ previous results on the UV luminosity function for galaxies, which are customarily divided into two large redshift ranges. For <em>z</em>\u00a0&lt;\u00a01 we follow Beifiori et al. (<a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjlaaf86abib4\" id=\"fnref-apjlaaf86abib4\">2014</a>), while for <em>z</em> <img src=\"https://cdn.iopscience.com/icons/Entities/gsim.gif\" alt=\"gsim\" align=\"baseline\" /> 1 we follow Bernardi et al. (<a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjlaaf86abib5\" id=\"fnref-apjlaaf86abib5\">2010</a>) and Mason et al. (<a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjlaaf86abib27\" id=\"fnref-apjlaaf86abib27\">2015</a>). As shown by previous studies (e.g., Wyithe et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjlaaf86abib41\" id=\"fnref-apjlaaf86abib41\">2011</a>; Mason et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjlaaf86abib27\" id=\"fnref-apjlaaf86abib27\">2015</a>) most of the lensing optical depth for sources at <em>z</em> <img src=\"https://cdn.iopscience.com/icons/Entities/gsim.gif\" alt=\"gsim\" align=\"baseline\" /> 6 originates from lens galaxies at <em>z</em> <img src=\"https://cdn.iopscience.com/icons/Entities/lesssim.gif\" alt=\"lesssim\" align=\"baseline\" /> 1.5. The dependence of the velocity dispersion <em>\u03c3</em><sub><em>v</em></sub> on the magnitude of the galaxy is modeled assuming the Faber\u2013Jackson relation (Faber &amp; Jackson <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjlaaf86abib13\" id=\"fnref-apjlaaf86abib13\">1976</a>), i.e., <span xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"inline-eqn\"><span class=\"tex\"><span class=\"texImage\"><img src=\"https://static.iopscience.com/2.44.0/img/lazy-loading-placeholder.gif\" data-src=\"https://cdn.iopscience.com/images/2041-8205/870/2/L12/Full/apjlaaf86aieqn18.gif\" alt=\"$L\\propto {\\sigma }_{v}^{4}$\" align=\"top\"></img></span><script type=\"math/tex\">L\\propto {\\sigma }_{v}^{4}</script></span></span>. As the peak of star formation activity is reached at <em>z</em>\u00a0~\u00a02 (Madau &amp; Dickinson <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjlaaf86abib24\" id=\"fnref-apjlaaf86abib24\">2014</a>), the stellar velocity dispersion <em>\u03c3</em><sub><em>v</em></sub> is expected to increase with redshift, at least for <em>z</em> <img src=\"https://cdn.iopscience.com/icons/Entities/lesssim.gif\" alt=\"lesssim\" align=\"baseline\" /> 3. This redshift range includes the population of lens galaxies that contribute the most to the lensing optical depth. Following Beifiori et al. Following Barkana &amp; Loeb (<a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjlaaf86abib2\" id=\"fnref-apjlaaf86abib2\">2000</a>), in Figure <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" href=\"#apjlaaf86af2\">2</a> we show the distribution in angular separations of lensing images for <em>z</em>\u00a0=\u00a06.51, <span xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"inline-eqn\"><span class=\"tex\"><span class=\"texImage\"><img src=\"https://static.iopscience.com/2.44.0/img/lazy-loading-placeholder.gif\" data-src=\"https://cdn.iopscience.com/images/2041-8205/870/2/L12/Full/apjlaaf86aieqn29.gif\" alt=\"${\\rm{\\Delta }}\\theta =8\\pi {({\\sigma }_{v}/c)}^{2}D({z}_{s},z^{\\prime} )/D({z}_{s},0)$\" align=\"top\"></img></span><script type=\"math/tex\">{\\rm{\\Delta }}\\theta =8\\pi {({\\sigma }_{v}/c)}^{2}D({z}_{s},z^{\\prime} )/D({z}_{s},0)</script></span></span>, where <span xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"inline-eqn\"><span class=\"tex\"><span class=\"texImage\"><img src=\"https://static.iopscience.com/2.44.0/img/lazy-loading-placeholder.gif\" data-src=\"https://cdn.iopscience.com/images/2041-8205/870/2/L12/Full/apjlaaf86aieqn30.gif\" alt=\"$D({z}_{s},0)$\" align=\"top\"></img></span><script type=\"math/tex\">D({z}_{s},0)</script></span></span> is the angular diameter distance between source and observer. Our results represent an advance relative to previous calculations (such as Wyithe &amp; Loeb <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjlaaf86abib40\" id=\"fnref-apjlaaf86abib40\">2002</a>) in several respects: (i) we employ an updated, observationally motivated cosmological distribution of lenses and sources, and (ii) we now have at our disposal an observational point to calibrate our calculations.</p><p>The probability of a source at <em>z</em>\u00a0=\u00a06.51 being lensed with magnification <em>\u03bc</em>\u00a0&gt;\u00a050 depends on the slope of the bright end of the quasar luminosity function, and we obtain <em>P</em>(<em>\u03bc</em>\u00a0&gt;\u00a050)\u00a0=\u00a0<em>P</em><sub>obs</sub> for <em>\u03b2</em>\u00a0\u2248\u00a03.2. A discrepancy between the frequentist approximation of the probability and the theoretical expectation can be explained by taking into account the magnification bias.</p><p>Depending on the slope <em>\u03b2</em>, current predictions for the lensing probability range within 0.04\u00a0&lt;\u00a0<em>P</em><sub>0</sub>\u00a0&lt;\u00a00.92, in striking contrast with the observed value of <em>P</em><sub>obs</sub>\u00a0\u2248\u00a07\u00a0<b>&#x00d7;</b>\u00a010<sup>\u22123</sup>. It is thus likely that the observed population of <em>z</em>\u00a0&gt;\u00a06 quasars contains cases with <em>\u03bc</em> <img src=\"https://cdn.iopscience.com/icons/Entities/lesssim.gif\" alt=\"lesssim\" align=\"baseline\" /> 10.</p><p>Additionally, the current selection criteria might have missed a significant fraction of the <em>z</em>\u00a0&gt;\u00a06 quasars, due to the contamination of their light by lens galaxies. Assuming that this undetected population coincides with the set of magnified objects at <em>z</em>\u00a0&gt;\u00a06, we predict that the unknown lensed quasars could account for up to about half of the currently detected population for <em>\u03b2</em> <img src=\"https://cdn.iopscience.com/icons/Entities/lesssim.gif\" alt=\"lesssim\" align=\"baseline\" /> 3.6. Remarkably, for the most recently suggested (Kulkarni et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjlaaf86abib21\" id=\"fnref-apjlaaf86abib21\">2018</a>) value of <em>\u03b2</em>\u00a0~\u00a05, the vast majority of the <em>z</em>\u00a0&gt;\u00a06 lensed cases of quasars is still undetected. It is important to note that our formalism predicts the fraction of <em>z</em>\u00a0&gt;\u00a06 that are undetected only because the magnifying effect of a lens galaxy introduces a contamination in their drop-out band. The presence of additional populations of quasars that are undetected for other reasons (e.g., obscuration, Comastri et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjlaaf86abib9\" id=\"fnref-apjlaaf86abib9\">2015</a>, or inefficient accretion, Pacucci et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjlaaf86abib30\" id=\"fnref-apjlaaf86abib30\">2017</a>) is not taken into account here.</p><p>We have introduced the lensing boost factor <em>b</em><sub><em>L</em></sub> to model in a compact way the effect of an undetected population of <em>z</em>\u00a0&gt;\u00a06 quasars on the corresponding BH mass functions: <span xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"inline-eqn\"><span class=\"tex\"><span class=\"texImage\"><img src=\"https://static.iopscience.com/2.44.0/img/lazy-loading-placeholder.gif\" data-src=\"https://cdn.iopscience.com/images/2041-8205/870/2/L12/Full/apjlaaf86aieqn42.gif\" alt=\"${\\varphi }_{\\mathrm{real}}({M}_{\\bullet },z)=(1+{b}_{L}){\\varphi }_{\\mathrm{obs}}({M}_{\\bullet },z)$\" align=\"top\"></img></span><script type=\"math/tex\">{\\varphi }_{\\mathrm{real}}({M}_{\\bullet },z)=(1+{b}_{L}){\\varphi }_{\\mathrm{obs}}({M}_{\\bullet },z)</script></span></span>. Future high-<em>z</em> quasar surveys, employing improved selection criteria, will be instrumental to setting constraints on <em>b</em><sub><em>L</em></sub> and, consequently, determining the corrected quasar luminosity functions.</p><p>Some discussion is due on how these predicted lensed <em>z</em>\u00a0&gt;\u00a06 quasars are classified. It is reasonable to argue that they are misclassified as low-<em>z</em> galaxies. In fact, a contamination of the drop-out bands of <em>z</em>\u00a0&gt;\u00a06 quasars (outside the IGM transmission windows) would lead to a failure of the photo-<em>z</em> determination, thus artificially decreasing the redshift and misclassifying the quasar as a galaxy. This is the case as long as the following two conditions are met: (i) the flux from the lens galaxy is comparable to or larger than that of the quasars, and (ii) no X-ray emission is detected from the quasar. The first condition is likely met as the lens is usually a massive elliptical galaxy at <em>z</em> <img src=\"https://cdn.iopscience.com/icons/Entities/lesssim.gif\" alt=\"lesssim\" align=\"baseline\" /> 1.5 (Mason et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjlaaf86abib27\" id=\"fnref-apjlaaf86abib27\">2015</a>) while the quasar is at <em>z</em>\u00a0&gt;\u00a06. The difference in luminosity distance would cause the flux from the quasar to be comparable to the flux of the galaxy, even if the former is a hundred times more intrinsically luminous than the latter. Regarding the second condition, it is worth noting that the presence of the lens galaxy in the same line of sight would artificially increase the total gas column density, thus decreasing the probability of an X-ray detection.</p><p>In this Letter we considered two possible solutions to the problem of the lack of detection of lensed <em>z</em>\u00a0&gt;\u00a06 quasars: (i) a fraction of the currently detected <em>z</em>\u00a0&gt;\u00a06 quasars is lensed, but their images are unresolved; (ii) the lensed <em>z</em>\u00a0&gt;\u00a06 quasars are misclassified into low-<em>z</em> galaxies, due to contamination effects in their photometry. These two solutions are complementary and not in conflict with each other. Understanding their feasibility requires advanced modeling of the lens population and, possibly, ad hoc modeling of some specific lensing configurations. In fact, the detectability of the lens galaxy ultimately depends on the ratio between its flux and the magnified flux of the quasar. More advanced models are required to provide a final answer to the question: how many lensed <em>z</em>\u00a0&gt;\u00a06 quasars might we be missing?</p><p>An additional population of undetected <em>z</em>\u00a0&gt;\u00a06 quasars could have implications to our understanding of the epoch of reionization. For instance, it could change our view of the contribution of quasars to the reionization of the universe (e.g., Madau <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjlaaf86abib23\" id=\"fnref-apjlaaf86abib23\">2017</a>). Furthermore, there is a long-standing search for the origin of the infrared (e.g., Kashlinsky et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjlaaf86abib18\" id=\"fnref-apjlaaf86abib18\">2007</a>; Yue et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjlaaf86abib43\" id=\"fnref-apjlaaf86abib43\">2013</a>) and X-ray backgrounds (e.g., Gilli et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjlaaf86abib16\" id=\"fnref-apjlaaf86abib16\">2007</a>) and their cross correlation (Cappelluti et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjlaaf86abib7\" id=\"fnref-apjlaaf86abib7\">2013</a>). Our predicted population of undetected <em>z</em>\u00a0&gt;\u00a06 quasars, with their spectra extended from the infrared to the X-ray, could make an important contributions to these backgrounds. Moreover, if a fraction of the currently detected population of <em>z</em>\u00a0&gt;\u00a06 quasars is found to be magnified, the value of their BH mass would be consequently decreased, easing the problem of early supermassive BH growth (e.g., Volonteri &amp; Rees <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjlaaf86abib38\" id=\"fnref-apjlaaf86abib38\">2005</a>; Pacucci et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjlaaf86abib30\" id=\"fnref-apjlaaf86abib30\">2017</a>, <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjlaaf86abib29\" id=\"fnref-apjlaaf86abib29\">2018</a>). For these reasons, employing new selection criteria to better understand the population of high-<em>z</em> quasars will be of fundamental importance in the near future.
