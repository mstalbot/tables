at <I>z</I>             <SUB>lens</SUB> &gt; 1. The lens has a secure photometric redshift of <I>z</I> = 1.53  \u00b1  0.09 and the source is spectroscopically confirmed at <I>z</I> = 3.417. The Einstein radius (0<img src=\"https://cdn.iopscience.com/icons/Entities/farcs.gif\" alt=\"farcs\" align=\"bottom\" />35; 3.0\u00a0kpc) encloses 7.6 <b>&#x00d7;</b> 10<SUP>10</SUP> <I>M</I>             <SUB>\u2609</SUB>, with an upper limit on the dark matter fraction of 60%. The highly magnified (40<b>&#x00d7;</b>) source galaxy has a very small stellar mass (~10<SUP>8</SUP> <I>M</I>             <SUB>\u2609</SUB>) and shows an extremely strong [O <span class=\"small-caps\">III</span>]<SUB>5007 \u00c5</SUB> emission line (EW<SUB>0</SUB> ~ 1000 \u00c5) bolstering the evidence that intense starbursts among very low-mass galaxies are common at high class=\"wd-jnl-art-full-text article-text\">\n<h2 class=\"header-anchor\" id=\"apjl485414s1\" name=\"apjl485414s1\">1.\u00a0INTRODUCTION</h2><div class=\"article-text\" data-mobile-collapse=\"\"><p>Strongly lensing galaxies provide a range of important applications, from the measurement of cosmological parameters through time delays between lensed images (Refsdal <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjl485414r26\" id=\"fnref-apjl485414r26\">1964</a>), to the direct measurement of total galaxy masses (e.g., Kochanek <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjl485414r17\" id=\"fnref-apjl485414r17\">1995</a>), the evolution and ages of elliptical galaxies (e.g., Rusin et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjl485414r27\" id=\"fnref-apjl485414r27\">2003</a>; van de Ven et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjl485414r34\" id=\"fnref-apjl485414r34\">2003</a>), and the radial profile of the dark matter distribution (e.g., Treu &amp; Koopmans <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjl485414r31\" id=\"fnref-apjl485414r31\">2004</a>).</p><p>Since the discovery of the first <em>z</em> = 1 strong lens almost 30\u00a0yr ago (Lawrence et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjl485414r20\" id=\"fnref-apjl485414r20\">1984</a>; Schneider et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjl485414r28\" id=\"fnref-apjl485414r28\">1986</a>), no strong lenses at higher redshifts have been found, despite the large number of <em>z</em> <img src=\"https://cdn.iopscience.com/icons/Entities/lesssim.gif\" alt=\"lesssim\" align=\"baseline\" /> 1 lenses discovered since then (e.g., Bolton et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjl485414r2\" id=\"fnref-apjl485414r2\">2006</a>; Faure et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjl485414r10\" id=\"fnref-apjl485414r10\">2008</a>; More et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjl485414r22\" id=\"fnref-apjl485414r22\">2012</a>), and a handful of tentative <em>z</em> ~ 1.2 candidates (More et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjl485414r22\" id=\"fnref-apjl485414r22\">2012</a>) for which the lensing interpretation is unfortunately doubtful.</p><p>The paucity of <em>z</em> &gt; 1 lenses has four causes: first, lensing probabilities decrease for lenses at higher redshifts due to the small relative distances between lenses and sources\u2014for example, the Einstein radius for a <span xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"inline-eqn\"><span class=\"tex\"><span class=\"texImage\"><img src=\"https://static.iopscience.com/2.44.0/img/lazy-loading-placeholder.gif\" data-src=\"https://cdn.iopscience.com/images/2041-8205/777/1/L17/Full/apjl485414ieqn1.gif\" alt=\"$\\sigma = 200 \\,\\rm {km} \\rm {s}^{-1}$\" align=\"top\"></img></span><script type=\"math/tex\">\\sigma = 200 \\,\\rm {km} \\rm {s}^{-1}</script></span></span> galaxy at <em>z</em> = 1.5 is only ~0<img src=\"https://cdn.iopscience.com/icons/Entities/farcs.gif\" alt=\"farcs\" align=\"bottom\" />4 for a <em>z</em> &gt; 2.5 source. Second, the reduced volume and number density of sources behind potential lenses is greatly reduced. Third, massive galaxies are increasingly rare at higher redshifts, and those with the highest lensing probability\u2014the most concentrated, bulge-dominated galaxies\u2014are usually red and faint in the rest-frame ultraviolet and hence in optical <em>z</em> &gt; 1 surveys. Fourth, the measurement of lens and source redshifts and the confirmation of the lensing interpretation require near-infrared spectroscopy, which has proven much more challenging than optical spectroscopy.</p><p>Based on the number counts of potential high-redshift (<em>z</em> &gt; 2.5) sources (e.g., Bouwens et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjl485414r3\" id=\"fnref-apjl485414r3\">2007</a>) and the <em>z</em> = 1\u20132 mass function of potential lenses (e.g., Ilbert et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjl485414r14\" id=\"fnref-apjl485414r14\">2010</a>; Brammer et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjl485414r7\" id=\"fnref-apjl485414r7\">2011</a>) we estimate one <em>z</em> &gt; 1 galaxy\u2013galaxy lens to occur per ~200 square arcminutes. Thus, large-area, near-infrared surveys are needed to have any chance of finding <em>z</em> &gt; 1 lenses. The largest current survey of this kind, CANDELS (Grogin et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjl485414r13\" id=\"fnref-apjl485414r13\">2011</a>; Koekemoer et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjl485414r18\" id=\"fnref-apjl485414r18\">2011</a>), covers 800 square arcminutes with <em>Hubble Space Telescope</em> (<em>HST</em>) near-infrared imaging, thus it should contain a handful of <em>z</em> &gt; 1 lenses with background sources intrinsically brighter than 28th magnitude (AB) in the near-infrared.</p><p>Here we present the discovery of the quadruple galaxy\u2013galaxy lens J100018.47+022138.74 (J1000+0221) using imaging data from CANDELS and near-infrared spectroscopy from the Large Binocular Telescope (LBT). We report a spectroscopically confirmed source redshift of <em>z<sub>S</sub></em> = 3.417 \u00b1 0.001 and a record lens galaxy redshift of <em>z<sub>L</sub></em> = 1.53 \u00b1 0.09.</p><p>The <em>z</em> = 1.53 lens is a flattened, quiescent galaxy with a stellar mass of ~6 <b>&#x00d7;</b> 10<sup>10</sup>\u2009<em>M</em><sub>\u2609</sub> and appears to be located in a previously unknown overdense region populated with at least half a dozen <em>L</em>* galaxies at the same redshift. Its Einstein radius of 0<img src=\"https://cdn.iopscience.com/icons/Entities/farcs.gif\" alt=\"farcs\" align=\"bottom\" />35 (3.0\u00a0kpc at <em>z</em> = 1.53) presents us with the first opportunity for a direct mass measurement in this redshift range through lensing, providing an important confirmation of previous results based on stellar absorption line kinematics (see, e.g., van de Sande et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjl485414r32\" id=\"fnref-apjl485414r32\">2013</a>, and references therein), which has, thus far, been technically challenging.</p></div>\n<h2 class=\"header-anchor\" id=\"apjl485414s2\" name=\"apjl485414s2\">2.\u00a0DATA</h2><div class=\"article-text\" data-mobile-collapse=\"\"><p>CANDELS <em>HST</em>/Wide Field Camera 3 (WFC3; Kimble et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjl485414r15\" id=\"fnref-apjl485414r15\">2008</a>) (F125W+F160W) and <em>HST</em>/Advanced Camera for Surveys (ACS; Ford et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjl485414r12\" id=\"fnref-apjl485414r12\">2003</a>) (F606W+F814W) imaging of the lens\u2013source system is shown Figure <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" href=\"#apjl485414f1\">1</a>. name=\"apjl485414s4\">4.\u00a0LENS REDSHIFT AND STELLAR MASS</h2><div class=\"article-text\" data-mobile-collapse=\"\"><p>Spectroscopic confirmation of the lens redshift has remained elusive due to its faintness (<em>V</em> ~ 26; <em>H</em><sub>F160W</sub> ~ 22) and apparent lack of emission lines. However, the red <em>I</em><sub>F814W</sub> \u2212 <em>J</em><sub>F125W</sub> color implies the presence of the Balmer and/or 4000\u2009\u00c5 break straddled by these two filters, suggesting a redshift in the range 1.2 &lt; <em>z</em> &lt; 2.0. Using <tt>EAZY</tt> (Brammer et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjl485414r5\" id=\"fnref-apjl485414r5\">2008</a>) we construct the redshift probability distribution based on the photometry given in Table <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"tabref\" href=\"#apjl485414t1\">1</a>, and find that the peak lies at <em>z</em> = 1.53. We find the same result if we include the LBC <em>U</em>-band photometry.</p><p>The lens\u2013source system is unresolved in the ground-based imaging, which thus produces the sum of the fluxes of the source and the lens. The source has an accurately known redshift and a very simple SED, given that it is young and dust-poor as indicated by the blue colors (see Figure <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" href=\"#apjl485414f3\">3</a> and Section <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"secref\" href=\"#apjl485414s6\">6</a>). The <tt>EAZY</tt> redshift probability distribution for the lens based on the NMBS photometry after subtracting the source SED peaks at <em>z</em> = 1.50. The combined probability distribution from the two independent redshift estimates give 1.44 &lt; <em>z</em> &lt; 1.62 (1.34 &lt; <em>z</em> &lt; 1.74) as the 68% (95%) confidence interval, with no secondary redshift solutions.</p><p>Further credence to our redshift estimate of the lens, and the precise value of <em>z</em> = 1.53 in particular, is lent by the four ~10<sup>11</sup>\u2009<em>M</em><sub>\u2609</sub> galaxies with <em>z</em><sub>phot</sub> ~ 1.5 in the NMBS catalog within a projected distance of 250\u00a0kpc, one of which could be spectroscopically confirmed at <em>z</em> = 1.525 with our LUCI spectroscopy. This is the only such overdensity in the COSMOS field, and suggests that the lens is a member.</p><p>We use <tt>FAST</tt> (Kriek et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjl485414r16\" id=\"fnref-apjl485414r16\">2009</a>) to estimate the lens stellar mass, star formation history and attenuation based on the ACS+WFC3+IRAC photometry given in Table <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"tabref\" href=\"#apjl485414t1\">1</a>. Given our stellar mass estimate, the roundness of the critical curve, and the extremely steep mass profile slope inferred under the assumption that mass follows light, we conclude that there likely is a significant contribution from dark matter within <em>R<sub>E</sub></em> of the lens, with an 1\u03c3 upper limit of 60%.</p><p>As described above, the lens is situated in an overdense environment, which likely contributes to the projected, enclosed mass. A crude estimate of this effect can be inferred by assuming that the most massive galaxy in the overdensity, which lies at a projected distance of 110\u00a0kpc, is situated at the center of a spherical (Navarro et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjl485414r24\" id=\"fnref-apjl485414r24\">1996</a>) dark matter halo with concentration parameter <em>c</em> = 6. Based on the stellar mass of this central galaxy (<em>M</em><sub>*</sub> = 3 <b>&#x00d7;</b> 10<sup>11</sup>\u2009<em>M</em><sub>\u2609</sub>) we estimate the halo to have a total mass of 10<sup>13.5\u201314</sup>\u2009<em>M</em><sub>\u2609</sub> (Moster et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjl485414r23\" id=\"fnref-apjl485414r23\">2013</a>), such that the projected mass within the Einstein radius of the lens is ~10<sup>10</sup>\u2009<em>M</em><sub>\u2609</sub>. This does not significantly affect our conclusions regarding the dark matter and stellar mass fractions.</p><p>Finally, we note that the velocity dispersion implied by the lens model (<span xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"inline-eqn\"><span class=\"tex\"><span class=\"texImage\"><img src=\"https://static.iopscience.com/2.44.0/img/lazy-loading-placeholder.gif\" data-src=\"https://cdn.iopscience.com/images/2041-8205/777/1/L17/Full/apjl485414ieqn4.gif\" alt=\"$\\sqrt{M_E/R_E} \\equiv \\sigma _E=182\\pm 10$\" align=\"top\"></img></span><script type=\"math/tex\">\\sqrt{M_E/R_E} \\equiv \\sigma _E=182\\pm 10</script></span></span>\u00a0km\u00a0s<sup>\u22121</sup>) is consistent with stellar velocity dispersions of galaxies at similar redshifts as measured from absorption line spectra (e.g., van de Sande et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjl485414r32\" id=\"fnref-apjl485414r32\">2013</a>, and references therein): the velocity dispersion is higher by ~20% compared to present-day galaxies with a similar mass. Our accurate and precise measurement of the velocity dispersion provides important support to the results based on stellar kinematics given the practical difficulties associated with continuum spectroscopy for <em>z</em> <img src=\"https://cdn.iopscience.com/icons/Entities/gsim.gif\" alt=\"gsim\" align=\"baseline\" /> 1.5 galaxies.</p></div>\n<h2 class=\"header-anchor\" id=\"apjl485414s6\" name=\"apjl485414s6\">6.\u00a0THE NATURE OF THE SOURCE</h2><div class=\"article-text\" data-mobile-collapse=\"\"><p>The <em>HST</em> photometry of the source (blue points in Figure <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" href=\"#apjl485414f3\">3</a>) shows a very blue continuum, indicative of a young, dust-free galaxy. Using the method described by Finkelstein et al. (<a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjl485414r11\" id=\"fnref-apjl485414r11\">2012</a>) we fit the SED given by the four photometric data points (F606W, F814W, F125W, and F160W) given in Table <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"tabref\" href=\"#apjl485414t1\">1</a>. We confirm the young age (50 \u00b1 30 Myr), dust-free nature (<span xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"inline-eqn\"><span class=\"tex\"><span class=\"texImage\"><img src=\"https://static.iopscience.com/2.44.0/img/lazy-loading-placeholder.gif\" data-src=\"https://cdn.iopscience.com/images/2041-8205/777/1/L17/Full/apjl485414ieqn5.gif\" alt=\"$E(B-V)=0.00^{+0.16}_{-0.00}$\" align=\"top\"></img></span><script type=\"math/tex\">E(B-V)=0.00^{+0.16}_{-0.00}</script></span></span>), and blue continuum slope (\u03b2 = \u22122.0 \u00b1 0.3) of the source. The (demagnified) stellar mass of the galaxy is <span xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"inline-eqn\"><span class=\"tex\"><span class=\"texImage\"><img src=\"https://static.iopscience.com/2.44.0/img/lazy-loading-placeholder.gif\" data-src=\"https://cdn.iopscience.com/images/2041-8205/777/1/L17/Full/apjl485414ieqn6.gif\" alt=\"$M_*=2.6^{+2.0}_{-1.3}\\times 10^8 \\,M_{\\odot }$\" align=\"top\"></img></span><script type=\"math/tex\">M_*=2.6^{+2.0}_{-1.3}\\times 10^8 \\,M_{\\odot }</script></span></span>. The best-fitting model SED is shown in Figure <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" href=\"#apjl485414f3\">3</a>.</p><p>As we mentioned above and show in Figure <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" href=\"#apjl485414f2\">2</a>, three emission lines are detected with high significance: H\u03b2 and [O\u2009<span class=\"small-caps\">iii</span>] at 4959 \u00c5 and 5007 \u00c5. We detect a low-level (1\u03c3\u20132\u03c3 per pixel) continuum in the spectrum, and we use the average continuum flux over the entire <em>K</em>-band wavelength range to estimate the equivalent (<a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjl485414r37\" id=\"fnref-apjl485414r37\">2011</a>), and the properties of those extreme emission line galaxies are very similar to those of the source described here (also see Atek et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjl485414r1\" id=\"fnref-apjl485414r1\">2011</a>). Remarkably, this is the second such emission-line-dominated object that is found to be strongly lensed in CANDELS: using data from the 3D-<em>HST</em> survey (Brammer et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjl485414r6\" id=\"fnref-apjl485414r6\">2012b</a>), Brammer et al. (<a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjl485414r4\" id=\"fnref-apjl485414r4\">2012a</a>) found a galaxy at <em>z</em><sub>S</sub> = 1.847 with very similar mass and age. Given the abundance of unlensed emission-line galaxies it was seemingly unlikely that even one strongly lensed version exists within a CANDELS-sized survey. The existence of two strong lensed galaxies of this kind suggests that such galaxies are likely even more common than is currently assumed, and that objects near or below the detection limits of current surveys\u2014the unlensed magnitude of the <em>z</em> = 3.417 source is <em>H</em><sub>F160W</sub> ~ 28\u2014very commonly display such strong emission lines (see Smit et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjl485414r30\" id=\"fnref-apjl485414r30\">2013</a>). This would have profound consequences for our understanding of galaxy formation: a presumably brief burst of intense star formation may signal the initial formation stage of any galaxy.</p><p>Euclid will reveal tens of thousands <em>z</em> &gt; 1 lens candidates, which will give us unprecedented insight into the variety in properties among faint, high-redshift galaxies, as well as the galaxy mass function at all redshifts 0 &lt; <em>z</em> &lt; 2 with perfectly calibrated mass measurements in an absolute sense.</p></div><div class=\"article-text\" data-mobile-collapse=\"\"><p>The authors thank Ricardo Amorin, Marcello Cacciato, Nimish Hathi, David Koo, Ray Lucas, Sharon Meidt, Mara Salvato, Benjamin Weiner, and Steven Willner for helpful comments and suggestions. class=\"wd-jnl-art-full-text article-text\">\n<h2 class=\"header-anchor\" id=\"apjl485414s1\" name=\"apjl485414s1\">1.\u00a0INTRODUCTION</h2><div class=\"article-text\" data-mobile-collapse=\"\"><p>Strongly lensing galaxies provide a range of important applications, from the measurement of cosmological parameters through time delays between lensed images (Refsdal <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjl485414r26\" id=\"fnref-apjl485414r26\">1964</a>), to the direct measurement of total galaxy masses (e.g., Kochanek <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjl485414r17\" id=\"fnref-apjl485414r17\">1995</a>), the evolution and ages of elliptical galaxies (e.g., Rusin et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjl485414r27\" id=\"fnref-apjl485414r27\">2003</a>; van de Ven et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjl485414r34\" id=\"fnref-apjl485414r34\">2003</a>), and the radial profile of the dark matter distribution (e.g., Treu &amp; Koopmans <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjl485414r31\" id=\"fnref-apjl485414r31\">2004</a>).</p><p>Since the discovery of the first <em>z</em> = 1 strong lens almost 30\u00a0yr ago (Lawrence et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjl485414r20\" id=\"fnref-apjl485414r20\">1984</a>; Schneider et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjl485414r28\" id=\"fnref-apjl485414r28\">1986</a>), no strong lenses at higher redshifts have been found, despite the large number of <em>z</em> <img src=\"https://cdn.iopscience.com/icons/Entities/lesssim.gif\" alt=\"lesssim\" align=\"baseline\" /> 1 lenses discovered since then (e.g., Bolton et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjl485414r2\" id=\"fnref-apjl485414r2\">2006</a>; Faure et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjl485414r10\" id=\"fnref-apjl485414r10\">2008</a>; More et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjl485414r22\" id=\"fnref-apjl485414r22\">2012</a>), and a handful of tentative <em>z</em> ~ 1.2 candidates (More et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjl485414r22\" id=\"fnref-apjl485414r22\">2012</a>) for which the lensing interpretation is unfortunately doubtful.</p><p>The paucity of <em>z</em> &gt; 1 lenses has four causes: first, lensing probabilities decrease for lenses at higher redshifts due to the small relative distances between lenses and sources\u2014for example, the Einstein radius for a <span xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"inline-eqn\"><span class=\"tex\"><span class=\"texImage\"><img src=\"https://static.iopscience.com/2.44.0/img/lazy-loading-placeholder.gif\" data-src=\"https://cdn.iopscience.com/images/2041-8205/777/1/L17/Full/apjl485414ieqn1.gif\" alt=\"$\\sigma = 200 \\,\\rm {km} \\rm {s}^{-1}$\" align=\"top\"></img></span><script type=\"math/tex\">\\sigma = 200 \\,\\rm {km} \\rm {s}^{-1}</script></span></span> galaxy at <em>z</em> = 1.5 is only ~0<img src=\"https://cdn.iopscience.com/icons/Entities/farcs.gif\" alt=\"farcs\" align=\"bottom\" />4 for a <em>z</em> &gt; 2.5 source. Second, the reduced volume and number density of sources behind potential lenses is greatly reduced. Third, massive galaxies are increasingly rare at higher redshifts, and those with the highest lensing probability\u2014the most concentrated, bulge-dominated galaxies\u2014are usually red and faint in the rest-frame ultraviolet and hence in optical <em>z</em> &gt; 1 surveys. Fourth, the measurement of lens and source redshifts and the confirmation of the lensing interpretation require near-infrared spectroscopy, which has proven much more challenging than optical spectroscopy.</p><p>Based on the number counts of potential high-redshift (<em>z</em> &gt; 2.5) sources (e.g., Bouwens et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjl485414r3\" id=\"fnref-apjl485414r3\">2007</a>) and the <em>z</em> = 1\u20132 mass function of potential lenses (e.g., Ilbert et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjl485414r14\" id=\"fnref-apjl485414r14\">2010</a>; Brammer et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjl485414r7\" id=\"fnref-apjl485414r7\">2011</a>) we estimate one <em>z</em> &gt; 1 galaxy\u2013galaxy lens to occur per ~200 square arcminutes. Thus, large-area, near-infrared surveys are needed to have any chance of finding <em>z</em> &gt; 1 lenses. The largest current survey of this kind, CANDELS (Grogin et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjl485414r13\" id=\"fnref-apjl485414r13\">2011</a>; Koekemoer et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjl485414r18\" id=\"fnref-apjl485414r18\">2011</a>), covers 800 square arcminutes with <em>Hubble Space Telescope</em> (<em>HST</em>) near-infrared imaging, thus it should contain a handful of <em>z</em> &gt; 1 lenses with background sources intrinsically brighter than 28th magnitude (AB) in the near-infrared.</p><p>Here we present the discovery of the quadruple galaxy\u2013galaxy lens J100018.47+022138.74 (J1000+0221) using imaging data from CANDELS and near-infrared spectroscopy from the Large Binocular Telescope (LBT). We report a spectroscopically confirmed source redshift of <em>z<sub>S</sub></em> = 3.417 \u00b1 0.001 and a record lens galaxy redshift of <em>z<sub>L</sub></em> = 1.53 \u00b1 0.09.</p><p>The <em>z</em> = 1.53 lens is a flattened, quiescent galaxy with a stellar mass of ~6 <b>&#x00d7;</b> 10<sup>10</sup>\u2009<em>M</em><sub>\u2609</sub> and appears to be located in a previously unknown overdense region populated with at least half a dozen <em>L</em>* galaxies at the same redshift. Its Einstein radius of 0<img src=\"https://cdn.iopscience.com/icons/Entities/farcs.gif\" alt=\"farcs\" align=\"bottom\" />35 (3.0\u00a0kpc at <em>z</em> = 1.53) presents us with the first opportunity for a direct mass measurement in this redshift range through lensing, providing an important confirmation of previous results based on stellar absorption line kinematics (see, e.g., van de Sande et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjl485414r32\" id=\"fnref-apjl485414r32\">2013</a>, and references therein), which has, thus far, been technically challenging.</p></div>\n<h2 class=\"header-anchor\" id=\"apjl485414s2\" name=\"apjl485414s2\">2.\u00a0DATA</h2><div class=\"article-text\" data-mobile-collapse=\"\"><p>CANDELS <em>HST</em>/Wide Field Camera 3 (WFC3; Kimble et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjl485414r15\" id=\"fnref-apjl485414r15\">2008</a>) (F125W+F160W) and <em>HST</em>/Advanced Camera for Surveys (ACS; Ford et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjl485414r12\" id=\"fnref-apjl485414r12\">2003</a>) (F606W+F814W) imaging of the lens\u2013source system is shown Figure <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" href=\"#apjl485414f1\">1</a>. An annulus with an inner radius of 0<img src=\"https://cdn.iopscience.com/icons/Entities/farcs.gif\" alt=\"farcs\" align=\"bottom\" />18 and an outer radius of 0<img src=\"https://cdn.iopscience.com/icons/Entities/farcs.gif\" alt=\"farcs\" align=\"bottom\" />66 containing the source images is masked in the fitting process. The half-light radius measured along the major axis is <em>R</em><sub>eff</sub> = 0.29 \u00b1 0<img src=\"https://cdn.iopscience.com/icons/Entities/farcs.gif\" alt=\"farcs\" align=\"bottom\" />01 or <em>R</em><sub>eff</sub> = 2.5 \u00b1 0.1 kpc. The S\u00e9rsic index is <em>n</em> = 2.2 \u00b1 0.2, the projected axis ratio <em>q</em> = 0.52 \u00b1 0.01, and the position angle (P.A. = 27\u00b0 \u00b1 1\u00b0 from north to east). The results do not significantly change if the masked region is made narrower. This size and shape are quite typical for early-type galaxies at <em>z</em> ~ 1.5 (e.g., van der Wel et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjl485414r36\" id=\"fnref-apjl485414r36\">2011</a>; Chang et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjl485414r9\" id=\"fnref-apjl485414r9\">2013</a>).</p><p>Then, for each of the ACS and WFC3 images, we convolve this S\u00e9rsic model with the appropriate PSF model<sup xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"fnref\"><a class=\"fnref\" href=\"#apjl485414fn1\">8</a></sup> and scale the total flux to minimize the residual flux within a radius of 0<img src=\"https://cdn.iopscience.com/icons/Entities/farcs.gif\" alt=\"farcs\" align=\"bottom\" />18. name=\"apjl485414s4\">4.\u00a0LENS REDSHIFT AND STELLAR MASS</h2><div class=\"article-text\" data-mobile-collapse=\"\"><p>Spectroscopic confirmation of the lens redshift has remained elusive due to its faintness (<em>V</em> ~ 26; <em>H</em><sub>F160W</sub> ~ 22) and apparent lack of emission lines. However, the red <em>I</em><sub>F814W</sub> \u2212 <em>J</em><sub>F125W</sub> color implies the presence of the Balmer and/or 4000\u2009\u00c5 break straddled by these two filters, suggesting a redshift in the range 1.2 &lt; <em>z</em> &lt; 2.0. Using <tt>EAZY</tt> (Brammer et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjl485414r5\" id=\"fnref-apjl485414r5\">2008</a>) we construct the redshift probability distribution based on the photometry given in Table <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"tabref\" href=\"#apjl485414t1\">1</a>, and find that the peak lies at <em>z</em> = 1.53. We find the same result if we include the LBC <em>U</em>-band photometry.</p><p>The lens\u2013source system is unresolved in the ground-based imaging, which thus produces the sum of the fluxes of the source and the lens. The source has an accurately known redshift and a very simple SED, given that it is young and dust-poor as indicated by the blue colors (see Figure <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" href=\"#apjl485414f3\">3</a> and Section <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"secref\" href=\"#apjl485414s6\">6</a>). The <tt>EAZY</tt> redshift probability distribution for the lens based on the NMBS photometry after subtracting the source SED peaks at <em>z</em> = 1.50. The combined probability distribution from the two independent redshift estimates give 1.44 &lt; <em>z</em> &lt; 1.62 (1.34 &lt; <em>z</em> &lt; 1.74) as the 68% (95%) confidence interval, with no secondary redshift solutions.</p><p>Further credence to our redshift estimate of the lens, and the precise value of <em>z</em> = 1.53 in particular, is lent by the four ~10<sup>11</sup>\u2009<em>M</em><sub>\u2609</sub> galaxies with <em>z</em><sub>phot</sub> ~ 1.5 in the NMBS catalog within a projected distance of 250\u00a0kpc, one of which could be spectroscopically confirmed at <em>z</em> = 1.525 with our LUCI spectroscopy. This is the only such overdensity in the COSMOS field, and suggests that the lens is a member.</p><p>We use <tt>FAST</tt> (Kriek et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjl485414r16\" id=\"fnref-apjl485414r16\">2009</a>) to estimate the lens stellar mass, star formation history and attenuation based on the ACS+WFC3+IRAC photometry given in Table <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"tabref\" href=\"#apjl485414t1\">1</a>. The best-fitting model SED is shown in Figure <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" href=\"#apjl485414f3\">3</a>.</p><p>As we mentioned above and show in Figure <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" href=\"#apjl485414f2\">2</a>, three emission lines are detected with high significance: H\u03b2 and [O\u2009<span class=\"small-caps\">iii</span>] at 4959 \u00c5 and 5007 \u00c5. width (EW) of the brightest [O\u2009<span class=\"small-caps\">iii</span>] line at <span xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"inline-eqn\"><span class=\"tex\"><span EW}([O\\,{\\scriptsize{III}}]_{5007\\,\\mathring{\\rm{A}}})=340\\pm 50\\,\\mathring{\\rm{A}}</script></span></span> in the observed frame, or <span xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"inline-eqn\"><span class=\"tex\"><span class=\"texImage\"><img src=\"https://static.iopscience.com/2.44.0/img/lazy-loading-placeholder.gif\" source.<sup xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"fnref\"><a class=\"fnref\" href=\"#apjl485414fn3\">9</a></sup> Adopting the <em>K</em>-band flux ratio predicted by the SED models for the source and the lens, we arrive at an estimated rest-frame <span xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"inline-eqn\"><span class=\"tex\"><span class=\"texImage\"><img EW}_0([O\\,{\\scriptsize{III}}]_{5007\\,\\mathring{\\rm{A}}})=1200\\pm 300\\,\\mathring{\\rm{A}}</script></span></span> for the source alone.</p><p>This high EW is consistent with the stellar population property estimates above, although we note that this is not necessarily the EW of the unlensed source, as a spatially varying EW could lead to a boosted, lensed EW. The fact that the brightest image is also the bluest may indicate that this may be the case here, but it is beyond the scope of our analysis to attempt to correct for this effect.</p><p>The [O\u2009<span class=\"small-caps\">iii</span>]/H\u03b2 line ratio from the spectrum shown in Figure <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" href=\"#apjl485414f2\">2</a> is <span xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"inline-eqn\"><span class=\"tex\"><span class=\"texImage\"><img src=\"https://static.iopscience.com/2.44.0/img/lazy-loading-placeholder.gif\" data-src=\"https://cdn.iopscience.com/images/2041-8205/777/1/L17/Full/apjl485414ieqn10.gif\" alt=\"$5^{+2}_{-1}$\" align=\"top\"></img></span><script type=\"math/tex\">5^{+2}_{-1}</script></span></span>, indicative of a high excitation, and likely a low metallicity. Additional emission lines such as [O\u2009<span class=\"small-caps\">ii</span>] or [Ne\u2009<span class=\"small-caps\">iii</span>], needed to confirm the low metallicity, are not detected.</p><p>An abundant population of objects at <em>z</em> ~ 2 with very high [O\u2009<span class=\"small-caps\">iii</span>] EW<sub>0</sub> was identified by van der Wel et al. (<a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjl485414r37\" id=\"fnref-apjl485414r37\">2011</a>), and the properties of those extreme emission line galaxies are very similar to those of the source described here (also see Atek et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjl485414r1\" id=\"fnref-apjl485414r1\">2011</a>). Remarkably, this is the second such emission-line-dominated object that is found to be strongly lensed in CANDELS: using data from the 3D-<em>HST</em> survey (Brammer et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjl485414r6\" id=\"fnref-apjl485414r6\">2012b</a>), Brammer et al. (<a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjl485414r4\" id=\"fnref-apjl485414r4\">2012a</a>) found a galaxy at <em>z</em><sub>S</sub> = 1.847 with very similar mass and age. Given the abundance of unlensed emission-line galaxies it was seemingly unlikely that even one strongly lensed version exists within a CANDELS-sized survey. The existence of two strong lensed galaxies of this kind suggests that such galaxies are likely even more common than is currently assumed, and that objects near or below the detection limits of current surveys\u2014the unlensed magnitude of the <em>z</em> = 3.417 source is <em>H</em><sub>F160W</sub> ~ 28\u2014very commonly display such strong emission lines (see Smit et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjl485414r30\" id=\"fnref-apjl485414r30\">2013</a>). This would have profound consequences for our understanding of galaxy formation: a presumably brief burst of intense star formation may signal the initial formation stage of any galaxy.</p><p>Euclid will reveal tens of thousands <em>z</em> &gt; 1 lens candidates, which will give us unprecedented insight into the variety in properties among faint, high-redshift galaxies, as well as the galaxy mass function at all redshifts 0 &lt; <em>z</em> &lt; 2 with perfectly calibrated mass measurements in an absolute sense.</p></div><div class=\"article-text\" data-mobile-collapse=\"\"><p>The authors thank Ricardo Amorin, Marcello Cacciato, Nimish Hathi, David Koo, Ray Lucas, Sharon Meidt, Mara Salvato, Benjamin Weiner, and Steven Willner for helpful comments and suggestions.
