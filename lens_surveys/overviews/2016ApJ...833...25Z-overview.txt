Multiply imaged background galaxies allow us to map in detail the otherwise-invisible dark matter (DM) distribution of the cluster, as well as to detect faint background objects that are highly magnified by the foreground cluster lens (see reviews by Bartelmann <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib2\">2010</xref>; Kneib &amp; Natarajan <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib26\">2011</xref>).</p>\n<p>The past decade, in particular, has seen a dramatic increase in SL-related science, thanks mainly to the continued impressive performance of the <italic>Hubble Space Telescope</italic>, from the combination of deep high-resolution optical and NIR imaging, and because of the development of improved lens modeling techniques (e.g., Broadhurst et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib7\">2005</xref>; Diego et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib10\">2005</xref>; Liesenborgs et al. <xref rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib28\" ref-type=\"bibr\">2006</xref>; Jullo et al. <xref rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib22\" ref-type=\"bibr\">2007</xref>; Zitrin et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib54\">2009b</xref>). Cluster lensing programs such as the Cluster Lensing and Supernova with Hubble (CLASH; PI: Postman, Postman et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib39\">2012</xref>), and the ongoing Hubble Frontier Fields (HFF; PI: Mountain &amp; Lotz; see Lotz et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib31\">2016</xref>) with <italic>HST</italic>, have proven to be&nbsp;extremely successful for SL, including the detection of hundreds of multiply lensed (e.g., Jauzac et al. <xref rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib19\" ref-type=\"bibr\">2014</xref>; Monna et al. <xref rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib35\" ref-type=\"bibr\">2014</xref>; Zitrin et al. <xref rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib55\" ref-type=\"bibr\">2015</xref>, as a&nbsp;few examples) and high-redshift, magnified background objects extending into the reionization era above <inline-formula>\n<tex-math>\n<?CDATA $z\\gtrsim 6$?>\n</tex-math>\n<!-- <mml:math>\n<mml:mi>z</mml:mi>\n<mml:mo>&gsim;</mml:mo>\n<mml:mn>6</mml:mn>\n</mml:math> -->\n<inline-graphic xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" xlink:href=\"apjaa3e3bieqn4.gif\"/>\n</inline-formula> (Zheng et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib51\">2012</xref>; Bradley et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib6\">2014</xref>; Atek et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib1\">2015</xref>; Coe et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib8\">2015</xref>), and beyond, to the current limits of detection at <italic>z</italic>&nbsp;&sim;&nbsp;11 (Coe et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib9\">2013</xref>; Zitrin et al. <xref rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib57\" ref-type=\"bibr\">2014</xref>). Construction of luminosity functions is feasible now to <inline-formula>\n<tex-math>\n<?CDATA $z\\sim 9$?>\n</tex-math>\n<!-- <mml:math>\n<mml:mi>z</mml:mi>\n<mml:mo>&sim;</mml:mo>\n<mml:mn>9</mml:mn>\n</mml:math> -->\n<inline-graphic xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" xlink:href=\"apjaa3e3bieqn5.gif\"/>\n</inline-formula> (Atek et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib1\">2015</xref>; Livermore et al. <xref rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib30\" ref-type=\"bibr\">2016</xref>; McLeod et al. <xref rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib32\" ref-type=\"bibr\">2016</xref>). Several lensed supernova have been discovered (e.g., Patel et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib38\">2014</xref>), including the first multiply&nbsp;imaged supernova as a quadrupole Einstein cross, and its subsequent reappearance (Kelly et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib24\">2016</xref>). Detailed studies of large highly magnified galaxies at <inline-formula>\n<tex-math>\n<?CDATA $z\\sim 1\\mbox{--}5$?>\n</tex-math>\n<!-- <mml:math>\n<mml:mi>z</mml:mi>\n<mml:mo>&sim;</mml:mo>\n<mml:mn>1</mml:mn>\n<mml:mo>&ndash;</mml:mo>\n<mml:mn>5</mml:mn>\n</mml:math> -->\n<inline-graphic xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" xlink:href=\"apjaa3e3bieqn6.gif\"/>\n</inline-formula> have helped constrain UV-escape fractions below the Ly-limit (e.g., Leethochawalit et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib27\">2016</xref>), metallicity gradients,&nbsp;and outflows (Jones et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib21\">2015</xref>) and star-formation details (e.g., Wuyts et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib50\">2012</xref>). Cosmological models have been examined with SL through arc and Einstein radius statistics (Oguri &amp; Blandford <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib37\">2009</xref>; Horesh et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib16\">2010</xref>; Waizmann et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib49\">2012</xref>) and multi-wavelength related discoveries have been made of magnified, X-ray, radio or sub-millimeter galaxies (e.g., Gonz&aacute;lez-L&oacute;pez et al. <xref rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib13\" ref-type=\"bibr\">2016</xref>; van Weeren et al. <xref rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib48\" ref-type=\"bibr\">2016</xref>).</p>\n<p>This progress in SL is inspiring new campaigns including the reionization cluster survey, RELICS (PI: Coe), informed by the CLASH and HFF programs dedicated to SL, and designed to enhance lensing-enabled science with future facilities and, in particular, the <italic>James Webb Space Telescope</italic> (<italic>JWST</italic>). Aside from the immediate science goals, part of the underlying motivation in these programs is to discover and characterize the &ldquo;best&rdquo; lensing targets for <italic>JWST</italic> for optimizing the detection of very distant background objects that lie beyond the reach of <italic>Hubble</italic>. Since there are many massive clusters in the sky (e.g., Oguri &amp; Blandford <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib37\">2009</xref>; Waizmann et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib49\">2012</xref>), choosing the largest and most powerful lenses requires systematic lens modeling of controlled samples of clusters with continued space imaging for the detection of the multiply lensed images required for this purpose. We are also using the <italic>HST</italic> archive for progressing in this work (e.g. Zitrin <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib58\">2016</xref>) with a backlog of numerous unanalyzed massive clusters, including the data analyzed here as well as other X-ray selected clusters from the MAssive Cluster Survey (MACS; Ebeling et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib11\">2010</xref>).</p>\n<p>We begin our systematic analysis with MACS J2135.2-0102 (<italic>z</italic>&nbsp;=&nbsp;0.33; hereafter MACS2135), which exhibits several prominent arcs ranging up to <inline-formula>\n<tex-math>\n<?CDATA $\\gtrsim 40^{\\prime\\prime} $?>\n</tex-math>\n<!-- <mml:math>\n<mml:mo>&gsim;</mml:mo>\n<mml:mn>40</mml:mn>\n<mml:mo accent=\"false\">&Prime;</mml:mo>\n</mml:math> -->\n<inline-graphic xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" xlink:href=\"apjaa3e3bieqn7.gif\"/>\n</inline-formula> from the brightest cluster galaxy (BCG), but lacks a recent lensing analysis that takes advantage of the achieved Hubble data. MACS2135 has been the subject of various previous studies. In particular, it became known as the cluster host of the cosmic eye galaxy&ndash;galaxy lens (Smail et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib44\">2007</xref>; Stark et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib46\">2008</xref>), one of the most distant clear examples of a typical star-forming galaxy at <italic>z</italic>&nbsp;=&nbsp;3.1. MASC2135 was later found to multiply&nbsp;image a prominent sub-millimeter galaxy (Ivison et al. <xref rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib18\" ref-type=\"bibr\">2010</xref>; Swinbank et al. <xref rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib47\" ref-type=\"bibr\">2010</xref>). In their analysis, Swinbank et al. (<xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib47\">2010</xref>) constructed an SL model for this cluster, based on the sub-millimeter galaxy system&mdash;for which they measured a spectroscopic redshift of <italic>z</italic>&nbsp;=&nbsp;2.3259 and identified a third counter image on the east side of the cluster. They also used and measured a redshift for <italic>z</italic>&nbsp;=&nbsp;2.32 for a triply imaged galaxy at a remarkable distance of <inline-formula>\n<tex-math>\n<?CDATA $\\sim 37^{\\prime\\prime} $?>\n</tex-math>\n<!-- <mml:math>\n<mml:mo>&sim;</mml:mo>\n<mml:mn>37</mml:mn>\n<mml:mo accent=\"false\">&Prime;</mml:mo>\n</mml:math> -->\n<inline-graphic xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" xlink:href=\"apjaa3e3bieqn8.gif\"/>\n</inline-formula> south of the BCG, two of its images straddling the critical curve into a giant arc. We did not find records of other, recent SL models for this cluster.</p>\n<p>Here we make use of the most recent <italic>HST</italic> imaging, that extends significantly the coverage of earlier work described above, to enhance the lens model with many new multiple&nbsp;images and to make this publicly available<xref rid=\"apjaa3e3bfn2\" ref-type=\"fn\"><sup>6</sup></xref>\n<fn id=\"apjaa3e3bfn2\">\n<label><sup>6</sup></label>\n<p>\n<ext-link ext-link-type=\"uri\" xlink:href=\"ftp://wise-ftp.tau.ac.il/pub/adiz/MACS2135/\">ftp://wise-ftp.tau.ac.il/pub/adiz/MACS2135/</ext-link>\n</p>\n</fn> given the expected large critical area (the model of Swinbank et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib47\">2010</xref> implied an Einstein radius of <inline-formula>\n<tex-math>\n<?CDATA $\\sim 35^{\\prime\\prime} $?>\n</tex-math>\n<!-- <mml:math>\n<mml:mo>&sim;</mml:mo>\n<mml:mn>35</mml:mn>\n<mml:mo accent=\"false\">&Prime;</mml:mo>\n</mml:math> -->\n<inline-graphic xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" xlink:href=\"apjaa3e3bieqn9.gif\"/>\n</inline-formula>) and relatively high ellipticity, which enhances the cross section of lensing clusters (Zitrin et al. <xref rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib56\" ref-type=\"bibr\">2013</xref>, and references therein). The paper is organized as follows. The final list of cluster members and their luminosities is the starting point for our mass modeling, as we now detail in Section <xref ref-type=\"sec\" rid=\"apjaa3e3bs3\">3</xref>.</p>\n</sec>\n<sec id=\"apjaa3e3bs3\">\n<label>3.</label>\n<title>LENS MODEL</title>\n<p>We use the light-traces-mass (LTM) modeling technique by Zitrin et al. (<xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib54\">2009b</xref>, see also Broadhurst et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib7\">2005</xref>) to construct a lensing model for MACS2135. We briefly describe the method here and refer the reader to the said works for full details. Our model generally consists of three components: a galaxy component, which is a superposition of all galaxy mass contributions;&nbsp;a DM smooth component, which is a smoothed version of the galaxy component; and a two-parameter external shear.</p>\n<p>As mentioned in Section <xref ref-type=\"sec\" rid=\"apjaa3e3bs2\">2</xref>, we start with the list of red-sequence cluster galaxies and their photometry. To construct the galaxy component, each member galaxy is assigned a power-law mass density distribution, scaled by its luminosity, where the superposition of all galaxies makes the total galaxy component of the model. The power-law exponent is the same for all galaxies and is a free parameter of the model.</p>\n<p>To obtain the DM smooth component, the galaxy component mass density map is then smoothed with a 2D Gaussian, whose width is a free parameter of the model. In that respect, both the galaxy and DM components follow the light distribution in an approximate sense as desired, since the finite statistical number of galaxies means we cannot expect an identical distribution. The two components are then combined with a relative weight, which is the third free parameter of the model. The overall normalization constitutes the fourth free parameter. A two-component external shear is then added to allow for more flexibility, introducing effective ellipticity to the lens model. We also allow here for the mass of a few central bright cluster galaxies, including the BCG, as well as their ellipticity and position-angle, to be freely optimized by the minimization procedure.</p>\n<p>In particular, after various iterations, the final model was minimized with seven key cluster members kept free: these include the three central bright galaxies embedded in the BCG&rsquo;s light in Figure <xref ref-type=\"fig\" rid=\"apjaa3e3bf1\">1</xref> (the relative weight between these affects the detailed shape of system 1&apos;s arc, for example), where the ellipticity and PA of the two brighter galaxies were also&nbsp;left to be freely optimized; a bright elliptical (21:35:15.192, &minus;01:03:01.70) next to images 1.3/4.3/5.3, which are the farthest from the critical curves; a bright galaxy (21:35:11.193, &minus;01:03:38.27) next to the most southern critical curve&rsquo;s tip and a bright galaxy (21:35:14.870, &minus;01:02:19.53) above its northern tip, since these help refine the shape of the curves in those regions; and another bright elliptical sitting within the critical curves (21:35:12.192, &minus;01:03:21.44), to allow for somewhat more flexibility south of the BCG. We note that the choice of cluster members to be left free is somewhat subjective, guided by the lensed image distribution and proximity to individual member galaxies, and naturally there may be other valid permutations in this respect.</p>\n<fig id=\"apjaa3e3bf1\" position=\"float\">\n<label>Figure 1.</label>\n<caption id=\"apjaa3e3bfc1\">\n<p>Central field of the galaxy cluster MACS2135 (R&nbsp;=&nbsp;[F140W&plus;F110W]; G&nbsp;=&nbsp;F814W; B&nbsp;=&nbsp;F606W). This is perhaps not surprising, since the LTM method was developed to yield maximal prediction power with a minimum of free parameters. Indeed, here we leave more galaxies to be freely weighted, as well as some of their ellipticities so the final number of free parameters is significantly increased. This is done to allow for somewhat more flexibility in the reproduction of images, but the overall solution still remains largely coupled to the light distribution. In other words, the LTM method may be less spatially flexible due to its coupling to the light distribution, than other purely analytic techniques with (often) inherently large numbers of free parameters, but has unprecedented prediction power to locate multiple images, even when none are known a priori. For example, Zitrin et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib52\">2012</xref> showed that a good, approximate solution can be obtained with as few as one parameter, the adopted mass-to-light relation. This prediction power has led us to constantly find, guided by preliminary LTM models, large numbers of multiple images previously undetected in many clusters. Additionally, on a technical note, it should be noted that the LTM model is in practice constructed on a grid, whose resolution is, for speed-up purposes, comparable to or somewhat lower than that of <italic>HST</italic>. In high magnification regions the round-up of the average source position to the grid&rsquo;s lower resolution pixel scale, introduces a finite, non-negligible <italic>rms</italic> error of order 0&farcs;1 per system, contributing to the global imprecision of the model (but, importantly, without harming its reliability nor prediction power). These points have been recently emphasized in more length in a community effort to compare lens modeling techniques to simulated clusters (Meneghetti et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib33\">2016</xref>), and we refer the interested reader to that work for more discussion on this end.</p>\n<p>We measure an effective Einstein radius of <inline-formula>\n<tex-math>\n<?CDATA ${\\theta }_{e}(z=2.32)=27\\pm 3^{\\prime\\prime} $?>\n</tex-math>\n<!-- <mml:math>\n<mml:msub>\n<mml:mrow>\n<mml:mi>&theta;</mml:mi>\n</mml:mrow>\n<mml:mrow>\n<mml:mi>e</mml:mi>\n</mml:mrow>\n</mml:msub>\n<mml:mo stretchy=\"true\">(</mml:mo>\n<mml:mi>z</mml:mi>\n<mml:mo>&equals;</mml:mo>\n<mml:mn>2.32</mml:mn>\n<mml:mo stretchy=\"true\">)</mml:mo>\n<mml:mo>&equals;</mml:mo>\n<mml:mn>27</mml:mn>\n<mml:mo>&PlusMinus;</mml:mo>\n<mml:mn>3</mml:mn>\n<mml:mo accent=\"false\">&Prime;</mml:mo>\n</mml:math> -->\n<inline-graphic xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" xlink:href=\"apjaa3e3bieqn26.gif\"/>\n</inline-formula> for the redshift of systems 1 and 2. We have correspondingly constructed a significantly improved mass model for MACS2135, which we present here and make available for the astronomical community. Our model agrees well with this cluster being a large lens, as perhaps is expected given the distance of system 2 from the BCG, and in broad agreement with the estimate presented in Swinbank et al. (<xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib47\">2010</xref>).</p>\n<p>Only a small fraction of the clusters well-studied in the literature are known to exhibit Einstein radii exceeding <inline-formula>\n<tex-math>\n<?CDATA $\\gtrsim 30^{\\prime\\prime} $?>\n</tex-math>\n<!-- <mml:math>\n<mml:mo>&gsim;</mml:mo>\n<mml:mn>30</mml:mn>\n<mml:mo accent=\"false\">&Prime;</mml:mo>\n</mml:math> -->\n<inline-graphic xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" xlink:href=\"apjaa3e3bieqn29.gif\"/>\n</inline-formula> (nominally, for sources at redshifts around <inline-formula>\n<tex-math>\n<?CDATA $z\\sim 2$?>\n</tex-math>\n<!-- <mml:math>\n<mml:mi>z</mml:mi>\n<mml:mo>&sim;</mml:mo>\n<mml:mn>2</mml:mn>\n</mml:math> -->\n<inline-graphic xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" xlink:href=\"apjaa3e3bieqn30.gif\"/>\n</inline-formula>). For example, only few out of the 25 X-ray selected CLASH clusters have Einstein radii comparable to, or slightly larger than that of MACS2135, and only a few clusters previously analyzed have considerably larger critical areas, e.g., Abell 1703, (Limousin et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib29\">2008</xref>); MACS 0717 (Zitrin et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib53\">2009a</xref>); RXJ1347 (Zitrin et al. <xref rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib55\" ref-type=\"bibr\">2015</xref>);&nbsp;Abell 1689 (Broadhurst et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib7\">2005</xref>); A370 (Richard et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib42\">2010</xref>); RCS2 J232727.6-020437, (Sharon et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib43\">2015</xref>); SDSS J120923.7&plus;264047 (Ofek et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib36\">2008</xref>); or CL0024 (Zitrin et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib54\">2009b</xref>). Indeed, thanks to their large critical areas, all of these clusters show numerous multiply&nbsp;imaged background galaxies, typically revealed in deep <italic>HST</italic> imaging. Additionally, the HFF clusters, for example, aside for the giant lens MACS0717 (Zitrin et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib53\">2009a</xref>) and perhaps A370 (Richard et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib42\">2010</xref>), show typically Einstein radii of <inline-formula>\n<tex-math>\n<?CDATA $\\sim 25^{\\prime\\prime} \\mbox{--}30^{\\prime\\prime} $?>\n</tex-math>\n<!-- <mml:math>\n<mml:mo>&sim;</mml:mo>\n<mml:mn>25</mml:mn>\n<mml:mo accent=\"false\">&Prime;</mml:mo>\n<mml:mo>&ndash;</mml:mo>\n<mml:mn>30</mml:mn>\n<mml:mo accent=\"false\">&Prime;</mml:mo>\n</mml:math> -->\n<inline-graphic xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" xlink:href=\"apjaa3e3bieqn31.gif\"/>\n</inline-formula>. Here we add&nbsp;MACS2135 to this important list, showing that despite its current relatively shallow imaging, it also lenses an abundance of highly magnified, multiply&nbsp;lensed background sources, and is comparable in size to the typical HFF cluster.</p>\n<p>Finding large and prominent lensing clusters is useful for probing the massive-end of the cluster mass function (Zitrin et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib53\">2009a</xref>; Waizmann et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib49\">2012</xref>; Redlich et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib40\">2014</xref>), for constraining cosmological models (Oguri &amp; Blandford <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib37\">2009</xref>), and also for studying the DM, substructure, morphology, and merging properties of the clusters (Merten et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib34\">2011</xref>; Harvey et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib15\">2015</xref>). Large lenses also increase the chances for finding very-high-redshift galaxies often pushing the redshift limit (e.g., Kneib et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib25\">2004</xref>; Coe et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib9\">2013</xref>), and in the case of multiple images, we can use the separation between the images to provide a purely &ldquo;geometric&rdquo; distance for the source as a means of testing the often ambiguous photometric redshift (Zitrin et al. <xref rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib57\" ref-type=\"bibr\">2014</xref>). In fact, two high-<italic>z</italic> candidates have already been reported in MACS 2135 (Repp et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib41\">2016</xref>), one of which our model predicts should lie nearly on top of&nbsp;the critical curves for high redshift, and thus might be highly magnified and potentially multiply imaged. We leave further examination of this candidate for other, dedicated work. The lensing approach to studying high-<italic>z</italic> galaxies is sensitive to the faint-end slope of the luminosity function, and complements the field work with Hubble that is also uncovering relatively luminous high-<italic>z</italic> galaxies over wider areas (e.g., Ellis et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib12\">2013</xref>; Bouwens et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib5\">2015</xref>).</p>\n<p>It should be appreciated that not only the Einstein radius of a lens is important in assessing the lensing efficiency of various clusters, but, as we have shown before, other factors must be considered, such as the magnification distribution (which is related to the gradient of the central mass distribution), substructure and sub-clumps that add nonlinearly to the magnification (Redlich et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib40\">2014</xref>), or the ellipticity of mass distribution, which enhances the lensing cross section (Zitrin et al. <xref rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib56\" ref-type=\"bibr\">2013</xref>), as well as of course, the redshifts involved and the magnification bias which depends on the slope of the luminosity function (Coe et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib8\">2015</xref>).</p>\n<p>We conclude that MACS2135 is among the top lenses currently known, especially in terms of its critical area, and will benefit from future attention. This includes deeper space imaging to uncover very distant high-redshift dropouts in the NIR, and as a compelling candidate target, in this respect, for <italic>JWST</italic>.</p>\n</sec> </body>\n<back>\n<ack>\n<p>We thank the anonymous reviewer of this work for useful comments. Multiply imaged background galaxies allow us to map in detail the otherwise-invisible dark matter (DM) distribution of the cluster, as well as to detect faint background objects that are highly magnified by the foreground cluster lens (see reviews by Bartelmann <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib2\">2010</xref>; Kneib &amp; Natarajan <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib26\">2011</xref>).</p>\n<p>The past decade, in particular, has seen a dramatic increase in SL-related science, thanks mainly to the continued impressive performance of the <italic>Hubble Space Telescope</italic>, from the combination of deep high-resolution optical and NIR imaging, and because of the development of improved lens modeling techniques (e.g., Broadhurst et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib7\">2005</xref>; Diego et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib10\">2005</xref>; Liesenborgs et al. <xref rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib28\" ref-type=\"bibr\">2006</xref>; Jullo et al. <xref rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib22\" ref-type=\"bibr\">2007</xref>; Zitrin et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib54\">2009b</xref>). Cluster lensing programs such as the Cluster Lensing and Supernova with Hubble (CLASH; PI: Postman, Postman et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib39\">2012</xref>), and the ongoing Hubble Frontier Fields (HFF; PI: Mountain &amp; Lotz; see Lotz et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib31\">2016</xref>) with <italic>HST</italic>, have proven to be&nbsp;extremely successful for SL, including the detection of hundreds of multiply lensed (e.g., Jauzac et al. <xref rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib19\" ref-type=\"bibr\">2014</xref>; Monna et al. <xref rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib35\" ref-type=\"bibr\">2014</xref>; Zitrin et al. <xref rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib55\" ref-type=\"bibr\">2015</xref>, as a&nbsp;few examples) and high-redshift, magnified background objects extending into the reionization era above <inline-formula>\n<tex-math>\n<?CDATA $z\\gtrsim 6$?>\n</tex-math>\n<!-- <mml:math>\n<mml:mi>z</mml:mi>\n<mml:mo>&gsim;</mml:mo>\n<mml:mn>6</mml:mn>\n</mml:math> -->\n<inline-graphic xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" xlink:href=\"apjaa3e3bieqn4.gif\"/>\n</inline-formula> (Zheng et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib51\">2012</xref>; Bradley et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib6\">2014</xref>; Atek et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib1\">2015</xref>; Coe et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib8\">2015</xref>), and beyond, to the current limits of detection at <italic>z</italic>&nbsp;&sim;&nbsp;11 (Coe et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib9\">2013</xref>; Zitrin et al. <xref rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib57\" ref-type=\"bibr\">2014</xref>). Construction of luminosity functions is feasible now to <inline-formula>\n<tex-math>\n<?CDATA $z\\sim 9$?>\n</tex-math>\n<!-- <mml:math>\n<mml:mi>z</mml:mi>\n<mml:mo>&sim;</mml:mo>\n<mml:mn>9</mml:mn>\n</mml:math> -->\n<inline-graphic xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" xlink:href=\"apjaa3e3bieqn5.gif\"/>\n</inline-formula> (Atek et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib1\">2015</xref>; Livermore et al. <xref rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib30\" ref-type=\"bibr\">2016</xref>; McLeod et al. <xref rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib32\" ref-type=\"bibr\">2016</xref>). Several lensed supernova have been discovered (e.g., Patel et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib38\">2014</xref>), including the first multiply&nbsp;imaged supernova as a quadrupole Einstein cross, and its subsequent reappearance (Kelly et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib24\">2016</xref>). Detailed studies of large highly magnified galaxies at <inline-formula>\n<tex-math>\n<?CDATA $z\\sim 1\\mbox{--}5$?>\n</tex-math>\n<!-- <mml:math>\n<mml:mi>z</mml:mi>\n<mml:mo>&sim;</mml:mo>\n<mml:mn>1</mml:mn>\n<mml:mo>&ndash;</mml:mo>\n<mml:mn>5</mml:mn>\n</mml:math> -->\n<inline-graphic xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" xlink:href=\"apjaa3e3bieqn6.gif\"/>\n</inline-formula> have helped constrain UV-escape fractions below the Ly-limit (e.g., Leethochawalit et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib27\">2016</xref>), metallicity gradients,&nbsp;and outflows (Jones et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib21\">2015</xref>) and star-formation details (e.g., Wuyts et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib50\">2012</xref>). Cosmological models have been examined with SL through arc and Einstein radius statistics (Oguri &amp; Blandford <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib37\">2009</xref>; Horesh et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib16\">2010</xref>; Waizmann et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib49\">2012</xref>) and multi-wavelength related discoveries have been made of magnified, X-ray, radio or sub-millimeter galaxies (e.g., Gonz&aacute;lez-L&oacute;pez et al. <xref rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib13\" ref-type=\"bibr\">2016</xref>; van Weeren et al. <xref rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib48\" ref-type=\"bibr\">2016</xref>).</p>\n<p>This progress in SL is inspiring new campaigns including the reionization cluster survey, RELICS (PI: Coe), informed by the CLASH and HFF programs dedicated to SL, and designed to enhance lensing-enabled science with future facilities and, in particular, the <italic>James Webb Space Telescope</italic> (<italic>JWST</italic>). Aside from the immediate science goals, part of the underlying motivation in these programs is to discover and characterize the &ldquo;best&rdquo; lensing targets for <italic>JWST</italic> for optimizing the detection of very distant background objects that lie beyond the reach of <italic>Hubble</italic>. Since there are many massive clusters in the sky (e.g., Oguri &amp; Blandford <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib37\">2009</xref>; Waizmann et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib49\">2012</xref>), choosing the largest and most powerful lenses requires systematic lens modeling of controlled samples of clusters with continued space imaging for the detection of the multiply lensed images required for this purpose. We are also using the <italic>HST</italic> archive for progressing in this work (e.g. Zitrin <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib58\">2016</xref>) with a backlog of numerous unanalyzed massive clusters, including the data analyzed here as well as other X-ray selected clusters from the MAssive Cluster Survey (MACS; Ebeling et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib11\">2010</xref>).</p>\n<p>We begin our systematic analysis with MACS J2135.2-0102 (<italic>z</italic>&nbsp;=&nbsp;0.33; hereafter MACS2135), which exhibits several prominent arcs ranging up to <inline-formula>\n<tex-math>\n<?CDATA $\\gtrsim 40^{\\prime\\prime} $?>\n</tex-math>\n<!-- <mml:math>\n<mml:mo>&gsim;</mml:mo>\n<mml:mn>40</mml:mn>\n<mml:mo accent=\"false\">&Prime;</mml:mo>\n</mml:math> -->\n<inline-graphic xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" xlink:href=\"apjaa3e3bieqn7.gif\"/>\n</inline-formula> from the brightest cluster galaxy (BCG), but lacks a recent lensing analysis that takes advantage of the achieved Hubble data. MACS2135 has been the subject of various previous studies. In particular, it became known as the cluster host of the cosmic eye galaxy&ndash;galaxy lens (Smail et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib44\">2007</xref>; Stark et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib46\">2008</xref>), one of the most distant clear examples of a typical star-forming galaxy at <italic>z</italic>&nbsp;=&nbsp;3.1. MASC2135 was later found to multiply&nbsp;image a prominent sub-millimeter galaxy (Ivison et al. <xref rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib18\" ref-type=\"bibr\">2010</xref>; Swinbank et al. <xref rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib47\" ref-type=\"bibr\">2010</xref>). In their analysis, Swinbank et al. (<xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib47\">2010</xref>) constructed an SL model for this cluster, based on the sub-millimeter galaxy system&mdash;for which they measured a spectroscopic redshift of <italic>z</italic>&nbsp;=&nbsp;2.3259 and identified a third counter image on the east side of the cluster. They also used and measured a redshift for <italic>z</italic>&nbsp;=&nbsp;2.32 for a triply imaged galaxy at a remarkable distance of <inline-formula>\n<tex-math>\n<?CDATA $\\sim 37^{\\prime\\prime} $?>\n</tex-math>\n<!-- <mml:math>\n<mml:mo>&sim;</mml:mo>\n<mml:mn>37</mml:mn>\n<mml:mo accent=\"false\">&Prime;</mml:mo>\n</mml:math> -->\n<inline-graphic xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" xlink:href=\"apjaa3e3bieqn8.gif\"/>\n</inline-formula> south of the BCG, two of its images straddling the critical curve into a giant arc. We did not find records of other, recent SL models for this cluster.</p>\n<p>Here we make use of the most recent <italic>HST</italic> imaging, that extends significantly the coverage of earlier work described above, to enhance the lens model with many new multiple&nbsp;images and to make this publicly available<xref rid=\"apjaa3e3bfn2\" ref-type=\"fn\"><sup>6</sup></xref>\n<fn id=\"apjaa3e3bfn2\">\n<label><sup>6</sup></label>\n<p>\n<ext-link ext-link-type=\"uri\" xlink:href=\"ftp://wise-ftp.tau.ac.il/pub/adiz/MACS2135/\">ftp://wise-ftp.tau.ac.il/pub/adiz/MACS2135/</ext-link>\n</p>\n</fn> given the expected large critical area (the model of Swinbank et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib47\">2010</xref> implied an Einstein radius of <inline-formula>\n<tex-math>\n<?CDATA $\\sim 35^{\\prime\\prime} $?>\n</tex-math>\n<!-- <mml:math>\n<mml:mo>&sim;</mml:mo>\n<mml:mn>35</mml:mn>\n<mml:mo accent=\"false\">&Prime;</mml:mo>\n</mml:math> -->\n<inline-graphic xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" xlink:href=\"apjaa3e3bieqn9.gif\"/>\n</inline-formula>) and relatively high ellipticity, which enhances the cross section of lensing clusters (Zitrin et al. <xref rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib56\" ref-type=\"bibr\">2013</xref>, and references therein). The data we use here includes imaging in two bands with the ACS: F606W, total exposure time of 1200 s, taken on 2006 May 08 (program ID 1049, PI: Ebeling); and F814W, with a total exposure time of 1440 s, taken on 2013 August 19 (program ID: 12884, PI: Ebeling); and two bands with the WFC3/IR: F110W and F140W, for a total of 705.88 s each, taken on 2011 August 23 (program ID 12166, PI: Ebeling). The final list of cluster members and their luminosities is the starting point for our mass modeling, as we now detail in Section <xref ref-type=\"sec\" rid=\"apjaa3e3bs3\">3</xref>.</p>\n</sec>\n<sec id=\"apjaa3e3bs3\">\n<label>3.</label>\n<title>LENS MODEL</title>\n<p>We use the light-traces-mass (LTM) modeling technique by Zitrin et al. (<xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib54\">2009b</xref>, see also Broadhurst et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib7\">2005</xref>) to construct a lensing model for MACS2135. We briefly describe the method here and refer the reader to the said works for full details. Our model generally consists of three components: a galaxy component, which is a superposition of all galaxy mass contributions;&nbsp;a DM smooth component, which is a smoothed version of the galaxy component; and a two-parameter external shear.</p>\n<p>As mentioned in Section <xref ref-type=\"sec\" rid=\"apjaa3e3bs2\">2</xref>, we start with the list of red-sequence cluster galaxies and their photometry. To construct the galaxy component, each member galaxy is assigned a power-law mass density distribution, scaled by its luminosity, where the superposition of all galaxies makes the total galaxy component of the model. The power-law exponent is the same for all galaxies and is a free parameter of the model.</p>\n<p>To obtain the DM smooth component, the galaxy component mass density map is then smoothed with a 2D Gaussian, whose width is a free parameter of the model. In that respect, both the galaxy and DM components follow the light distribution in an approximate sense as desired, since the finite statistical number of galaxies means we cannot expect an identical distribution. The two components are then combined with a relative weight, which is the third free parameter of the model. The overall normalization constitutes the fourth free parameter. A two-component external shear is then added to allow for more flexibility, introducing effective ellipticity to the lens model. We also allow here for the mass of a few central bright cluster galaxies, including the BCG, as well as their ellipticity and position-angle, to be freely optimized by the minimization procedure.</p>\n<p>In particular, after various iterations, the final model was minimized with seven key cluster members kept free: these include the three central bright galaxies embedded in the BCG&rsquo;s light in Figure <xref ref-type=\"fig\" rid=\"apjaa3e3bf1\">1</xref> (the relative weight between these affects the detailed shape of system 1&apos;s arc, for example), where the ellipticity and PA of the two brighter galaxies were also&nbsp;left to be freely optimized; a bright elliptical (21:35:15.192, &minus;01:03:01.70) next to images 1.3/4.3/5.3, which are the farthest from the critical curves; a bright galaxy (21:35:11.193, &minus;01:03:38.27) next to the most southern critical curve&rsquo;s tip and a bright galaxy (21:35:14.870, &minus;01:02:19.53) above its northern tip, since these help refine the shape of the curves in those regions; and another bright elliptical sitting within the critical curves (21:35:12.192, &minus;01:03:21.44), to allow for somewhat more flexibility south of the BCG. This is perhaps not surprising, since the LTM method was developed to yield maximal prediction power with a minimum of free parameters. Indeed, here we leave more galaxies to be freely weighted, as well as some of their ellipticities so the final number of free parameters is significantly increased. This is done to allow for somewhat more flexibility in the reproduction of images, but the overall solution still remains largely coupled to the light distribution. In other words, the LTM method may be less spatially flexible due to its coupling to the light distribution, than other purely analytic techniques with (often) inherently large numbers of free parameters, but has unprecedented prediction power to locate multiple images, even when none are known a priori. For example, Zitrin et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib52\">2012</xref> showed that a good, approximate solution can be obtained with as few as one parameter, the adopted mass-to-light relation. This prediction power has led us to constantly find, guided by preliminary LTM models, large numbers of multiple images previously undetected in many clusters. Additionally, on a technical note, it should be noted that the LTM model is in practice constructed on a grid, whose resolution is, for speed-up purposes, comparable to or somewhat lower than that of <italic>HST</italic>. In high magnification regions the round-up of the average source position to the grid&rsquo;s lower resolution pixel scale, introduces a finite, non-negligible <italic>rms</italic> error of order 0&farcs;1 per system, contributing to the global imprecision of the model (but, importantly, without harming its reliability nor prediction power). These points have been recently emphasized in more length in a community effort to compare lens modeling techniques to simulated clusters (Meneghetti et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib33\">2016</xref>), and we refer the interested reader to that work for more discussion on this end.</p>\n<p>We measure an effective Einstein radius of <inline-formula>\n<tex-math>\n<?CDATA ${\\theta }_{e}(z=2.32)=27\\pm 3^{\\prime\\prime} $?>\n</tex-math>\n<!-- <mml:math>\n<mml:msub>\n<mml:mrow>\n<mml:mi>&theta;</mml:mi>\n</mml:mrow>\n<mml:mrow>\n<mml:mi>e</mml:mi>\n</mml:mrow>\n</mml:msub>\n<mml:mo stretchy=\"true\">(</mml:mo>\n<mml:mi>z</mml:mi>\n<mml:mo>&equals;</mml:mo>\n<mml:mn>2.32</mml:mn>\n<mml:mo stretchy=\"true\">)</mml:mo>\n<mml:mo>&equals;</mml:mo>\n<mml:mn>27</mml:mn>\n<mml:mo>&PlusMinus;</mml:mo>\n<mml:mn>3</mml:mn>\n<mml:mo accent=\"false\">&Prime;</mml:mo>\n</mml:math> -->\n<inline-graphic xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" xlink:href=\"apjaa3e3bieqn26.gif\"/>\n</inline-formula> for the redshift of systems 1 and 2. We have correspondingly constructed a significantly improved mass model for MACS2135, which we present here and make available for the astronomical community. Our model agrees well with this cluster being a large lens, as perhaps is expected given the distance of system 2 from the BCG, and in broad agreement with the estimate presented in Swinbank et al. (<xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib47\">2010</xref>).</p>\n<p>Only a small fraction of the clusters well-studied in the literature are known to exhibit Einstein radii exceeding <inline-formula>\n<tex-math>\n<?CDATA $\\gtrsim 30^{\\prime\\prime} $?>\n</tex-math>\n<!-- <mml:math>\n<mml:mo>&gsim;</mml:mo>\n<mml:mn>30</mml:mn>\n<mml:mo accent=\"false\">&Prime;</mml:mo>\n</mml:math> -->\n<inline-graphic xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" xlink:href=\"apjaa3e3bieqn29.gif\"/>\n</inline-formula> (nominally, for sources at redshifts around <inline-formula>\n<tex-math>\n<?CDATA $z\\sim 2$?>\n</tex-math>\n<!-- <mml:math>\n<mml:mi>z</mml:mi>\n<mml:mo>&sim;</mml:mo>\n<mml:mn>2</mml:mn>\n</mml:math> -->\n<inline-graphic xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" xlink:href=\"apjaa3e3bieqn30.gif\"/>\n</inline-formula>). For example, only few out of the 25 X-ray selected CLASH clusters have Einstein radii comparable to, or slightly larger than that of MACS2135, and only a few clusters previously analyzed have considerably larger critical areas, e.g., Abell 1703, (Limousin et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib29\">2008</xref>); MACS 0717 (Zitrin et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib53\">2009a</xref>); RXJ1347 (Zitrin et al. <xref rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib55\" ref-type=\"bibr\">2015</xref>);&nbsp;Abell 1689 (Broadhurst et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib7\">2005</xref>); A370 (Richard et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib42\">2010</xref>); RCS2 J232727.6-020437, (Sharon et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib43\">2015</xref>); SDSS J120923.7&plus;264047 (Ofek et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib36\">2008</xref>); or CL0024 (Zitrin et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib54\">2009b</xref>). Indeed, thanks to their large critical areas, all of these clusters show numerous multiply&nbsp;imaged background galaxies, typically revealed in deep <italic>HST</italic> imaging. Additionally, the HFF clusters, for example, aside for the giant lens MACS0717 (Zitrin et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib53\">2009a</xref>) and perhaps A370 (Richard et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib42\">2010</xref>), show typically Einstein radii of <inline-formula>\n<tex-math>\n<?CDATA $\\sim 25^{\\prime\\prime} \\mbox{--}30^{\\prime\\prime} $?>\n</tex-math>\n<!-- <mml:math>\n<mml:mo>&sim;</mml:mo>\n<mml:mn>25</mml:mn>\n<mml:mo accent=\"false\">&Prime;</mml:mo>\n<mml:mo>&ndash;</mml:mo>\n<mml:mn>30</mml:mn>\n<mml:mo accent=\"false\">&Prime;</mml:mo>\n</mml:math> -->\n<inline-graphic xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" xlink:href=\"apjaa3e3bieqn31.gif\"/>\n</inline-formula>. Here we add&nbsp;MACS2135 to this important list, showing that despite its current relatively shallow imaging, it also lenses an abundance of highly magnified, multiply&nbsp;lensed background sources, and is comparable in size to the typical HFF cluster.</p>\n<p>Finding large and prominent lensing clusters is useful for probing the massive-end of the cluster mass function (Zitrin et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib53\">2009a</xref>; Waizmann et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib49\">2012</xref>; Redlich et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib40\">2014</xref>), for constraining cosmological models (Oguri &amp; Blandford <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib37\">2009</xref>), and also for studying the DM, substructure, morphology, and merging properties of the clusters (Merten et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib34\">2011</xref>; Harvey et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib15\">2015</xref>). Large lenses also increase the chances for finding very-high-redshift galaxies often pushing the redshift limit (e.g., Kneib et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib25\">2004</xref>; Coe et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib9\">2013</xref>), and in the case of multiple images, we can use the separation between the images to provide a purely &ldquo;geometric&rdquo; distance for the source as a means of testing the often ambiguous photometric redshift (Zitrin et al. <xref rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib57\" ref-type=\"bibr\">2014</xref>). In fact, two high-<italic>z</italic> candidates have already been reported in MACS 2135 (Repp et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib41\">2016</xref>), one of which our model predicts should lie nearly on top of&nbsp;the critical curves for high redshift, and thus might be highly magnified and potentially multiply imaged. We leave further examination of this candidate for other, dedicated work. The lensing approach to studying high-<italic>z</italic> galaxies is sensitive to the faint-end slope of the luminosity function, and complements the field work with Hubble that is also uncovering relatively luminous high-<italic>z</italic> galaxies over wider areas (e.g., Ellis et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib12\">2013</xref>; Bouwens et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib5\">2015</xref>).</p>\n<p>It should be appreciated that not only the Einstein radius of a lens is important in assessing the lensing efficiency of various clusters, but, as we have shown before, other factors must be considered, such as the magnification distribution (which is related to the gradient of the central mass distribution), substructure and sub-clumps that add nonlinearly to the magnification (Redlich et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib40\">2014</xref>), or the ellipticity of mass distribution, which enhances the lensing cross section (Zitrin et al. <xref rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib56\" ref-type=\"bibr\">2013</xref>), as well as of course, the redshifts involved and the magnification bias which depends on the slope of the luminosity function (Coe et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa3e3bbib8\">2015</xref>).</p>\n<p>We conclude that MACS2135 is among the top lenses currently known, especially in terms of its critical area, and will benefit from future attention. This includes deeper space imaging to uncover very distant high-redshift dropouts in the NIR, and as a compelling candidate target, in this respect, for <italic>JWST</italic>.</p>\n</sec> </body>\n<back>\n<ack>\n<p>We thank the anonymous reviewer of this work for useful comments.
