itemprop=\"description\">\n            <P>We report the discovery of RG1M0150, a massive, recently quenched galaxy at <I>z</I> = 2.636 that is multiply imaged by the cluster MACSJ0150.3-1005. We derive a stellar mass of <IMG ALIGN=\"MIDDLE\" alt=\"$\\mathrm{log}{M}_{*}={11.49}_{-0.16}^{+0.10}$\" src=\"https://static.iopscience.com/2.44.0/img/lazy-loading-placeholder.gif\" data-src=\"https://cdn.iopscience.com/images/2041-8205/813/1/L7/apjl521011ieqn1.gif\"></IMG> and a half-light radius of <IMG ALIGN=\"MIDDLE\" alt=\"${R}_{e,\\mathrm{maj}}=1.8\\pm 0.4\\;{\\rm{kpc}}.$\" src=\"https://static.iopscience.com/2.44.0/img/lazy-loading-placeholder.gif\" data-src=\"https://cdn.iopscience.com/images/2041-8205/813/1/L7/apjl521011ieqn2.gif\"></IMG> Taking advantage of the lensing magnification, we are able to spatially resolve a remarkably massive yet compact quiescent galaxy at <IMG ALIGN=\"MIDDLE\" alt=\"$z\\gt 2$\" src=\"https://static.iopscience.com/2.44.0/img/lazy-loading-placeholder.gif\" data-src=\"https://cdn.iopscience.com/images/2041-8205/813/1/L7/apjl521011ieqn3.gif\"></IMG> in ground-based near-infrared spectroscopic observations using Magellan/FIRE and Keck/MOSFIRE. We find no gradient in the strength of the Balmer absorption lines over <IMG ALIGN=\"MIDDLE\" alt=\"$0.6{R}_{e}-1.6{R}_{e},$\" src=\"https://static.iopscience.com/2.44.0/img/lazy-loading-placeholder.gif\" data-src=\"https://cdn.iopscience.com/images/2041-8205/813/1/L7/apjl521011ieqn4.gif\"></IMG> which are consistent with an age of 760 Myr. Gas emission in [N <span class=\"small-caps\">ii</span>] broadly traces the spatial distribution of the stars and is coupled with weak H<I>\u03b1</I> emission (log [N <span class=\"small-caps\">ii</span>]/<IMG ALIGN=\"MIDDLE\" alt=\"${\\rm{H}}\\alpha =0.6\\pm 0.2$\" src=\"https://static.iopscience.com/2.44.0/img/lazy-loading-placeholder.gif\" data-src=\"https://cdn.iopscience.com/images/2041-8205/813/1/L7/apjl521011ieqn5.gif\"></IMG>), indicating that OB stars are not the primary ionizing source. The velocity dispersion within the effective radius is <IMG ALIGN=\"MIDDLE\" alt=\"${\\sigma }_{e,\\mathrm{stars}}=271\\pm 41$\" src=\"https://static.iopscience.com/2.44.0/img/lazy-loading-placeholder.gif\" data-src=\"https://cdn.iopscience.com/images/2041-8205/813/1/L7/apjl521011ieqn6.gif\"></IMG> km s<IMG ALIGN=\"MIDDLE\" alt=\"${}^{-1}.$\" src=\"https://static.iopscience.com/2.44.0/img/lazy-loading-placeholder.gif\" data-src=\"https://cdn.iopscience.com/images/2041-8205/813/1/L7/apjl521011ieqn7.gif\"></IMG> We detect rotation in the stellar absorption lines for the first time beyond <IMG ALIGN=\"MIDDLE\" alt=\"$z\\sim 1.$\" src=\"https://static.iopscience.com/2.44.0/img/lazy-loading-placeholder.gif\" data-src=\"https://cdn.iopscience.com/images/2041-8205/813/1/L7/apjl521011ieqn8.gif\"></IMG> Using a two-integral Jeans model that accounts for observational effects, we measure a dynamical mass of <IMG ALIGN=\"MIDDLE\" alt=\"$\\mathrm{log}{M}_{{\\rm{dyn}}}=11.24\\pm 0.14$\" src=\"https://static.iopscience.com/2.44.0/img/lazy-loading-placeholder.gif\" data-src=\"https://cdn.iopscience.com/images/2041-8205/813/1/L7/apjl521011ieqn9.gif\"></IMG> and <IMG ALIGN=\"MIDDLE\" alt=\"$V/\\sigma =0.70\\pm 0.21.$\" src=\"https://static.iopscience.com/2.44.0/img/lazy-loading-placeholder.gif\" data-src=\"https://cdn.iopscience.com/images/2041-8205/813/1/L7/apjl521011ieqn10.gif\"></IMG> This is a high degree of rotation considering the modest observed ellipticity of 0.12 \u00b1 0.08, but it is consistent with predictions from dissipational merger simulations that produce compact remnants. The mass of RG1M0150 implies that it is likely to become a slowly rotating elliptical. If it is typical, this suggests that the progenitors of massive ellipticals retain significant net angular momentum after quenching which later declines, perhaps through accretion of satellites.</P></div>\n    <!-- End abstract -->\n<!-- Start Export citation and abstract tools -->\n\t<p>\n\t\t<small>Export citation and abstract</small>\n\t\t<span class=\"wd-jnl-art-full-text article-text\">\n<h2 class=\"header-anchor\" id=\"apjl521011s1\" name=\"apjl521011s1\">1.\u00a0INTRODUCTION</h2><div class=\"article-text\" data-mobile-collapse=\"\"><p>Observations are beginning to reveal the bulk properties of the high-redshift progenitors of massive elliptical galaxies. Massive quiescent galaxies are present at <span xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"inline-eqn\"><span class=\"tex\"><span class=\"texImage\"><img src=\"https://static.iopscience.com/2.44.0/img/lazy-loading-placeholder.gif\" data-src=\"https://cdn.iopscience.com/images/2041-8205/813/1/L7/Full/apjl521011ieqn11.gif\" alt=\"$z\\gt 2$\" align=\"top\"></img></span><script type=\"math/tex\">z\\gt 2</script></span></span> but have very compact sizes compared to local early-type galaxies (ETGs). Much effort in recent years has focused on understanding the growth of these \"nuggets\" and the emergence of extended stellar envelopes (e.g., Trujillo et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjl521011bib39\" id=\"fnref-apjl521011bib39\">2006</a>; van Dokkum et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjl521011bib46\" id=\"fnref-apjl521011bib46\">2010</a>; Newman et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjl521011bib28\" id=\"fnref-apjl521011bib28\">2012</a>; Patel et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjl521011bib31\" id=\"fnref-apjl521011bib31\">2013</a>; Belli et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjl521011bib3\" id=\"fnref-apjl521011bib3\">2015</a>). Recent observations are also beginning to link these early quiescent galaxies to star-forming progenitors at even earlier epochs via their masses, sizes, and kinematics (Barro et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjl521011bib2\" id=\"fnref-apjl521011bib2\">2014</a>; Toft et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjl521011bib38\" id=\"fnref-apjl521011bib38\">2014</a>; van Dokkum et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjl521011bib45\" id=\"fnref-apjl521011bib45\">2015</a>).</p><p>Numerical simulations have made increasingly detailed predictions for the properties of ETGs formed in disk mergers (e.g., Cox et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjl521011bib11\" id=\"fnref-apjl521011bib11\">2006</a>; Robertson et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjl521011bib34\" id=\"fnref-apjl521011bib34\">2006</a>; Hopkins et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjl521011bib17\" id=\"fnref-apjl521011bib17\">2009</a>; Zolotov et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjl521011bib50\" id=\"fnref-apjl521011bib50\">2015</a>). Forming a dense stellar core requires significant dissipation, and such gas-rich mergers are predicted to produce rotating remnants\u2014possibly with a velocity exceeding the bulk of today's ETGs (Wuyts et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjl521011bib48\" id=\"fnref-apjl521011bib48\">2010</a>). Different channels for the formation of compact galaxies can be tested with stellar population gradients (Wellons et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjl521011bib47\" id=\"fnref-apjl521011bib47\">2015</a>) and kinematic data. However, such comparisons require <em>spatially resolved</em> observations, which are very challenging given the small angular sizes of typical <span xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"inline-eqn\"><span class=\"tex\"><span class=\"texImage\"><img src=\"https://static.iopscience.com/2.44.0/img/lazy-loading-placeholder.gif\" data-src=\"https://cdn.iopscience.com/images/2041-8205/813/1/L7/Full/apjl521011ieqn12.gif\" alt=\"$z\\gt 2$\" align=\"top\"></img></span><script type=\"math/tex\">z\\gt 2</script></span></span> quiescent galaxies (~0<img src=\"https://cdn.iopscience.com/icons/Entities/farcs.gif\" alt=\"farcs\" align=\"bottom\" />1\u20130<img src=\"https://cdn.iopscience.com/icons/Entities/farcs.gif\" alt=\"farcs\" align=\"bottom\" />2). Gravitational lensing provides a promising route toward resolving these compact systems. The main challenge in locating lensed examples is the need for wide-area near-infrared (NIR) imaging to overcome their rarity and faintness at optical wavelengths. Thus far only three example have been published (Muzzin et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjl521011bib25\" id=\"fnref-apjl521011bib25\">2012</a>; Geier et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjl521011bib16\" id=\"fnref-apjl521011bib16\">2013</a>), and none has spatially resolved spectroscopic data.</p><p>In this Letter we present the discovery of RG1M0150, a rare example of a massive, quiescent galaxy at <em>z</em> = 2.636 that is multiply imaged by the cluster MACSJ0150.3\u20131005 (R.A. <span xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"inline-eqn\"><span class=\"tex\"><span class=\"texImage\"><img src=\"https://static.iopscience.com/2.44.0/img/lazy-loading-placeholder.gif\" data-src=\"https://cdn.iopscience.com/images/2041-8205/813/1/L7/Full/apjl521011ieqn13.gif\" alt=\"${1}^{{\\rm{h}}}{50}^{{\\rm{m}}}21\\buildrel{\\rm{s}}\\over{.} 3,$\" align=\"top\"></img></span><script type=\"math/tex\">{1}^{{\\rm{h}}}{50}^{{\\rm{m}}}21\\buildrel{\\rm{s}}\\over{.} 3,</script></span></span> decl. <span xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"inline-eqn\"><span class=\"tex\"><span class=\"texImage\"><img src=\"https://static.iopscience.com/2.44.0/img/lazy-loading-placeholder.gif\" data-src=\"https://cdn.iopscience.com/images/2041-8205/813/1/L7/Full/apjl521011ieqn14.gif\" alt=\"$-10^\\circ 05^{\\prime} 30^{\\prime\\prime} ,$\" align=\"top\"></img></span><script type=\"math/tex\">-10^\\circ 05^{\\prime} 30^{\\prime\\prime} ,</script></span></span> <span xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"inline-eqn\"><span class=\"tex\"><span class=\"texImage\"><img src=\"https://static.iopscience.com/2.44.0/img/lazy-loading-placeholder.gif\" data-src=\"https://cdn.iopscience.com/images/2041-8205/813/1/L7/Full/apjl521011ieqn15.gif\" alt=\"$z=0.365;$\" align=\"top\"></img></span><script type=\"math/tex\">z=0.365;</script></span></span> Ebeling et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjl521011bib13\" id=\"fnref-apjl521011bib13\">2001</a>). RG1M0150 was located in a search for bright sources with red NIR colors that are magnified by clusters in the <em>Hubble Space Telescope</em> (<em>HST</em>) archive. We find that the stellar and gas line widths are closer in RG1M0150, with <span xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"inline-eqn\"><span class=\"tex\"><span class=\"texImage\"><img src=\"https://static.iopscience.com/2.44.0/img/lazy-loading-placeholder.gif\" data-src=\"https://cdn.iopscience.com/images/2041-8205/813/1/L7/Full/apjl521011ieqn137.gif\" alt=\"${\\sigma }_{e,\\mathrm{stars}}/{\\sigma }_{e,\\mathrm{gas}}=1.1\\pm 0.2,$\" align=\"top\"></img></span><script type=\"math/tex\">{\\sigma }_{e,\\mathrm{stars}}/{\\sigma }_{e,\\mathrm{gas}}=1.1\\pm 0.2,</script></span></span> but larger samples will be needed to test this hypothesis due to the strong inclination dependence.</p><p>Nearly all local ETGs with <span xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"inline-eqn\"><span class=\"tex\"><span class=\"texImage\"><img src=\"https://static.iopscience.com/2.44.0/img/lazy-loading-placeholder.gif\" data-src=\"https://cdn.iopscience.com/images/2041-8205/813/1/L7/Full/apjl521011ieqn138.gif\" alt=\"${M}_{*}\\gt {10}^{11.3}\\;{M}_{\\odot }$\" align=\"top\"></img></span><script type=\"math/tex\">{M}_{*}\\gt {10}^{11.3}\\;{M}_{\\odot }</script></span></span> (scaled to a Chabrier IMF) are \"slow rotators\" with <span xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"inline-eqn\"><span class=\"tex\"><span class=\"texImage\"><img src=\"https://static.iopscience.com/2.44.0/img/lazy-loading-placeholder.gif\" data-src=\"https://cdn.iopscience.com/images/2041-8205/813/1/L7/Full/apjl521011ieqn139.gif\" alt=\"$V/\\sigma $\" align=\"top\"></img></span><script type=\"math/tex\">V/\\sigma</script></span></span> much smaller than RG1M0150 (red points in Figure <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" href=\"#apjl521011f5\">5</a>). Our best estimate for the stellar mass of RG1M0150 already places it within this range. Even allowing a somewhat smaller mass (e.g., our <span xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"inline-eqn\"><span class=\"tex\"><span class=\"texImage\"><img src=\"https://static.iopscience.com/2.44.0/img/lazy-loading-placeholder.gif\" data-src=\"https://cdn.iopscience.com/images/2041-8205/813/1/L7/Full/apjl521011ieqn140.gif\" alt=\"${M}_{{\\rm{dyn}}}$\" align=\"top\"></img></span><script type=\"math/tex\">{M}_{{\\rm{dyn}}}</script></span></span>), it is quite likely that RG1M0150 will evolve into a slow rotator: empirical estimates based on number density arguments (e.g., Muzzin et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjl521011bib26\" id=\"fnref-apjl521011bib26\">2013</a>; Patel et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjl521011bib31\" id=\"fnref-apjl521011bib31\">2013</a>) imply that RG1M0150 will grow in stellar mass by 0.2\u20130.4 dex by <em>z</em> = 0. Stellar populations in such ultra-massive galaxies today are almost exclusively very old (e.g., McDermid et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjl521011bib23\" id=\"fnref-apjl521011bib23\">2015</a>), so significant later star formation is unlikely. If RG1M0150 is typical, this suggests that the progenitors of giant ellipticals are still rotating significantly after they have been quenched and must reduce their net angular momentum later. A sequence of mostly dry mergers, suspected of driving the observed <span xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"inline-eqn\"><span class=\"tex\"><span class=\"texImage\"><img src=\"https://static.iopscience.com/2.44.0/img/lazy-loading-placeholder.gif\" data-src=\"https://cdn.iopscience.com/images/2041-8205/813/1/L7/Full/apjl521011ieqn141.gif\" alt=\"$\\simeq 4\\times $\" align=\"top\"></img></span><script type=\"math/tex\">\\simeq 4\\times</script></span></span> growth in effective radius, might provide a physical explanation.</p><p>General conclusions about galaxy formation obviously cannot be drawn from RG1M0150 alone. Fortunately, a larger sample of lensed compact galaxies at <span xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"inline-eqn\"><span class=\"tex\"><span class=\"texImage\"><img src=\"https://static.iopscience.com/2.44.0/img/lazy-loading-placeholder.gif\" data-src=\"https://cdn.iopscience.com/images/2041-8205/813/1/L7/Full/apjl521011ieqn142.gif\" alt=\"$z\\sim 2$\" align=\"top\"></img></span><script type=\"math/tex\">z\\sim 2</script></span></span> (mostly at lower masses) is within reach and provides the most feasible route toward resolving the stellar populations and dynamics of these intriguing objects.</p></div> <div class=\"article-text\" data-mobile-collapse=\"\"><p>We thank Dan Kelson for expertise reducing the FourStar data and insightful discussions.
