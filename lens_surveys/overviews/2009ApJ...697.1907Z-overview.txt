They are undetected in the deep ACS images below 8500 &Aring; and have clear detections in the <italic>J</italic> and <italic>H</italic> bands. Fits to the ACS, NICMOS, and IRAC data yield robust photometric redshifts in the range <italic>z</italic> &sim; 6&ndash;7 and largely rule out the possibility that they are low-redshift interlopers. All three objects are extended, and resolved into a pair of bright knots. The bright <italic>i</italic>-band dropout in Abell 1703 has an <italic>H</italic>-band AB magnitude of 23.9, which makes it one of the brightest known galaxy candidates at <italic>z</italic> &gt; 5.5. Our model fits suggest a young, massive galaxy only &sim;60 million years old with a mass of &sim;10<sup>10</sup>&thinsp;<italic>M</italic><sub>&sun;</sub>. The dropout galaxy candidates behind CL0024+16 are separated by 2&farcs;5 (&sim;2 kpc in the source plane), and have <italic>H</italic>-band AB magnitudes of 25.0 and 25.6. Lensing models of CL0024+16 suggest that the objects have comparable intrinsic magnitudes of AB &sim;27.3, approximately one magnitude fainter than L* at <italic>z</italic> &sim; 6.5. Their similar redshifts, spectral energy distribution, and luminosities, coupled with their very close proximity on the sky, suggest that they are spatially associated, and plausibly are physically bound. Combining this sample with two previously reported, similarly magnified galaxy candidates at <italic>z</italic> &sim; 6&ndash;8, we find that complex systems with dual nuclei may be a common feature of high-redshift galaxies.</p></abstract>\n<kwd-group kwd-group-type=\"AAS\">\n<kwd>cosmology: observations</kwd>\n<kwd>galaxies: clusters: general</kwd>\n<kwd>galaxies: high-redshift</kwd>\n<kwd>gravitational lensing</kwd>\n</kwd-group>\n<custom-meta-wrap>\n<custom-meta><meta-name>ccc</meta-name><meta-value>0004-637X/09/021907+11&#x0024;30.00</meta-value></custom-meta>\n<custom-meta><meta-name>printed</meta-name><meta-value>Printed in the U.S.A.</meta-value></custom-meta>\n</custom-meta-wrap>\n</article-meta>\n</front>\n<body>\n<sec id=\"apj295677s1\"><label>1.</label><title>INTRODUCTION</title>\n<p>Deep observations and surveys with the <italic>Hubble Space Telescope</italic> (<italic>HST</italic>), such as GOODS (Giavalisco et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r24\">2004</xref>; Dickinson et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r19\">2004</xref>), the HUDF (Beckwith et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r1\">2006</xref>), the HUDF05 (Oesch et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r36\">2007</xref>), and the HUDF parallel observations (Bouwens et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r7\">2004a</xref>), have greatly enriched our knowledge of the distant universe, enabling the discovery of more than 600 <italic>i</italic><sub>775</sub>-band dropouts (<italic>i</italic>-dropout; Bouwens et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r4\">2006</xref>, <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r5\">2007</xref>), and eight <italic>z</italic><sub>850</sub>-band dropouts (<italic>z</italic>-dropout; Bouwens et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r9\">2008</xref>) which are believed to be galaxies at <italic>z</italic> &sim; 6 and 7, respectively. An L* galaxy of <italic>M</italic> &equals; &minus;20.3 at <italic>z</italic> &sim; 6 exhibits an apparent magnitude &sim;26.8 (AB, and hereafter); consequently, the majority of <italic>z</italic> &gt; 5.5 galaxies are fainter than AB = 26.</p>\n<p>Bright high-redshift galaxies are rare. Ten <italic>i</italic>-dropouts brighter than AB &equals; 25.5 were found in &sim;400 arcmin<sup>2</sup> of GOODS, HUDF, and HUDF parallel fields (Bouwens et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r4\">2006</xref>, <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r5\">2007</xref>). Two of them have been confirmed to be high-redshift objects spectroscopically (Bunker et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r15\">2003</xref>; Stanway et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r49\">2004</xref>). In a survey of the Subaru Deep Field of 767 arcmin<sup>2</sup> (Shimasaku et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r47\">2005</xref>; Taniguchi et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r52\">2005</xref>), 18 <italic>i</italic>-dropout galaxies were spectroscopically confirmed to be at <italic>z</italic> &sim; 5.7, and nine at <italic>z</italic> &sim; 6.6, but the brightest among them is only at magnitude of 25.4. In a recent survey that covers 1 deg<sup>2</sup> in the field of the Subaru/<italic>XMM-Newton</italic> Deep Survey (Ouchi et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r38\">2008b</xref>), 17 Ly&alpha; emitters (LAEs) at <italic>z</italic> &sim; 5.7 were found, of which only one is brighter than AB = 25.5 in the <italic>z</italic> band. At least one bright LAE has been found at <italic>z</italic> &sim; 6.6 (<italic>z</italic>-band magnitude 25.4, narrow band (921nm) magnitude 23.5; Ouchi et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r37\">2008a</xref>). Combining the results of these three surveys, the brightest <italic>z</italic> &sim; 6 candidate over 5000 arcmin<sup>2</sup> is at AB = 24.5 (in broad bands&ndash;LAEs are intrinsically brighter in a narrow band).</p>\n<p>Gravitational lensing by massive galaxy clusters enables the detection of high-redshift galaxies that are fainter than can be found even in very deep surveys. In addition, lensed galaxies can be resolved spatially and, if bright enough, can be candidates for spectroscopy (Hu et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r25\">2002</xref>; Kneib et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r27\">2004</xref>; Pell&oacute; et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r39\">2004</xref>; Schaerer &amp; Pell&oacute; <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r46\">2005</xref>; Richard et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r42\">2006</xref>; Frye et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r23\">2008</xref>; Bradley et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r11\">2008</xref>; Bouwens et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r10\">2009</xref>; Richard et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r43\">2008</xref>). The number of <italic>z</italic> &gsim; 6 candidates reported, however, has varied considerably depending on how aggressive the selection criteria are (and consequently how reliable the detected sources are: see, e.g., discussions in Bouwens et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r10\">2009</xref>). The <italic>HST</italic> images of clusters are the best in terms of spatial resolution and photometric accuracy, thus permitting the most reliable selections of high-redshift galaxies. One salient example is the three <italic>i</italic>-dropout arclets in Abell 2218 found by Kneib et al. (<xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r27\">2004</xref>). These arclets represent different views (multiple images) of the same source at <italic>z</italic> &equals; 6.75 &plusmn; 0.1. Bouwens et al. (<xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r6\">2003</xref>) found an <italic>i</italic>-dropout object in the field of cluster RDS1252 &minus; 29 as bright as AB = 24.2, and follow-up spectroscopy (Dow-Hygelund et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r20\">2005</xref>) confirmed it as a Lyman-break galaxy at <italic>z</italic> &equals; 5.515 and revealed stellar absorption lines in the continuum. Recently, Bradley et al. (<xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r11\">2008</xref>) discovered a galaxy candidate at <italic>z</italic> &sim; 7.6 in the field of Abell 1689 that is spatially resolved and has an observed <italic>H</italic>-band magnitude of 24.7. The cluster lensing magnifies this galaxy (A1689-zD1) by a factor of &sim;10.</p>\n<p>Taking advantage of the NICMOS data of 21 arcmin<sup>2</sup> over 11 galaxy clusters (including Abell 1689), Bouwens et al. (<xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r10\">2009</xref>) searched for strongly lensed <italic>z</italic>-dropout galaxies at <italic>z</italic> &gsim; 7 and found that A1689-zD1 is the only robust candidate in these fields. This suggests that bright high-redshift galaxies may be rare in cluster fields, even given the smaller field size of cluster regions. Since the amount of NICMOS data available over cluster fields is still very limited, there is reason to look at the surface density of such objects over larger areas and at somewhat lower redshifts, in order to overcome the effect of small number statistics.</p>\n<p>The purpose of this paper is to report on the results of a search for bright (<italic>H</italic>-band magnitude &les;25.6), highly magnified <italic>i</italic>- and <italic>z</italic>-dropout galaxies over a larger area than was used by Bouwens et al. (<xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r10\">2009</xref>). Such sources provide us with a unique opportunity to examine the properties of lower-luminosity galaxies in detail. The lower-luminosity galaxy population is of great interest because it appears to dominate the UV luminosity density, SFR density, and production of ionizing (&lambda;<sub>0</sub> &lsim; 912 &Aring;) photons (Yan &amp; Windhorst <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r54\">2004</xref>; Yoshida et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r57\">2006</xref>; Beckwith et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r1\">2006</xref>; Bouwens et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r4\">2006</xref>, <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r5\">2007</xref>; Oesch et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r36\">2007</xref>; Reddy et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r41\">2008</xref>; Frye et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r23\">2008</xref>; Chen et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r18\">2009</xref>). In particular, we consider &sim;4 arcmin<sup>2</sup> of additional search area over CL0024+16 and CL1056 &minus; 03 obtained in a recent NICMOS program HST-GO10874 (PI: W. Zheng). These areas were not included in the <italic>z</italic>-dropout search of Bouwens et al. (<xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r10\">2009</xref>).</p>\n<p>The plan for this paper is as follows. In Section <xref ref-type=\"sec\" rid=\"apj295677s2\">2</xref>, we describe the observational data. In Section <xref ref-type=\"sec\" rid=\"apj295677s3\">3</xref>, we outline our search criteria and summarize the results of our search for bright <italic>z</italic> &sim; 6&ndash;7 galaxies. In Section <xref ref-type=\"sec\" rid=\"apj295677s4\">4</xref>, we use stellar-population modeling and the available lensing models to remark on the physical properties of the high-redshift galaxy candidates we are examining, and in Section <xref ref-type=\"sec\" rid=\"apj295677s5\">5</xref>, we summarize our results.</p></sec>\n<sec id=\"apj295677s2\"><label>2.</label><title>OBSERVATIONS</title>\n<p>A summary of the <italic>HST</italic> ACS, WFPC2, and NICMOS imaging observations used here to search for <italic>z</italic> &sim; 6&ndash;7 <italic>i</italic>- or <italic>z</italic>-dropout galaxies is given in Table <xref ref-type=\"table\" rid=\"apj295677t1\">1</xref>. We coadded the available NICMOS images for the clusters under study to create the detection image (Szalay et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r51\">1999</xref>). We then measured the fluxes on each of the ACS, WFPC2, and NICMOS images using the same scalable apertures, after PSF-correcting the higher resolution ACS and WFPC2 images to match the NICMOS images. As in Bouwens et al. (<xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r10\">2009</xref>), we measure colors from smaller scalable apertures (Kron factor of 1.2; Kron <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r29\">1980</xref>), typically &sim;0&farcs;3. We then correct the fluxes measured in these smaller apertures to total magnitudes by comparing the light inside a larger scalable aperture (Kron factor of 2.5) of &sim;0&farcs;6, on the detection image to that inside a smaller aperture.</p>\n<p>Our color selection is based on the magnitudes measured in small-scalable apertures. The selection criteria for <italic>i</italic>-dropouts are similar to that of Giavalisco et al. (<xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r24\">2004</xref>) and Bouwens et al. (<xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r4\">2006</xref>), but with some variations:\n<disp-formula id=\"apj295677ufd1\">\n<tex-math><?CDATA\n\\begin{eqnarray*}\n(i_{775}-z_{850}) > (1.3 + 0.9 (z_{850} - J_{110}))\n\\end{eqnarray*}\n?></tex-math><graphic xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" xlink:href=\"apj295677ufd1.gif\"/></disp-formula>or\n<disp-formula id=\"apj295677ufd2\">\n<tex-math><?CDATA\n\\begin{eqnarray*}\n(i_{775} - z_{850}) > 2.0 \\wedge (z_{850} - J_{110}) < 0.8\n\\end{eqnarray*}\n?></tex-math><graphic xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" xlink:href=\"apj295677ufd2.gif\"/></disp-formula>where &and; represents the logical <bold>AND</bold> symbol. Since these data have a considerably larger PSF than the ACS&sol;NICMOS data, this poses a challenge for accurate photometry. First, the IRAC PSF (&sim;1&farcs;6 at 3.6 &mu;m) is considerably larger than the apertures used in the <italic>HST</italic> images (typically &lsim; 0&farcs;5 in radius). We used an aperture size of 2&farcs;5 diameter, and then applied corrections of 0.56 and 0.6 mag for the 3.6&thinsp;&mu;m and 4.5&thinsp;&mu;m band, respectively, to account for flux outside of the aperture. These corrections were derived for stellar profiles (see, e.g., Bradley et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r11\">2008</xref>). In the field of A1703-iD1, there are two nearby sources of comparable brightness approximately 2&Prime; away (Figure <xref ref-type=\"fig\" rid=\"apj295677f3\">3</xref>). We ran the task <italic>Galfit</italic> (Peng et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r40\">2002</xref>) to deblend the source fluxes with fixed source positions and a standard PSF profile of FWHM = 1&farcs;6. The derived magnitudes are presented in Table <xref ref-type=\"table\" rid=\"apj295677t2\">2</xref>.</p>\n<p>A1703-iD1 is a prominent <italic>i</italic>-dropout object, with a <italic>z</italic>-band magnitude AB = 24.2. As shown in Figure <xref ref-type=\"fig\" rid=\"apj295677f3\">3</xref>, it is well detected in the ACS <italic>z</italic>-band and NICMOS <italic>J</italic> and <italic>H</italic> bands, but not in the <italic>i</italic>-band and the other bands at shorter wavelengths. The object is detected in the Gemini-N image in the <italic>J</italic> band, with a magnitude of 24.2 &plusmn; 0.4. Our confidence that this source is a galaxy at <italic>z</italic> &sim; 6 rather than a lower-redshift object (Stanway et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r50\">2005</xref>) is largely motivated by the source&apos;s large color decrement of >2.6, which is twice the threshold in a normal selection (1.3&ndash;1.5). The possibility that this source is a dusty galaxy at lower redshift is ruled out as the source is roughly equally bright in the <italic>z</italic>- and <italic>J</italic>-bands, and entirely undetected in the <italic>r</italic><sub>606</sub>- (<italic>r</italic>) and <italic>i</italic>-bands, which is clearly a sign of a break in the spectral energy distribution (SED) instead of a gradual decrease toward bluer wavelengths. The source is resolved into two components (magnitudes 24.3 and 24.9; Figure <xref ref-type=\"fig\" rid=\"apj295677f3\">3</xref>) separated by &sim;0&farcs;45. The only other objects with such colors are T-dwarfs, which can be ruled out by the fact that the source is extended. The FWHM for each component is &sim;0&farcs;4, which is four times the ACS resolution. This source is detected at low significance (&lt;2&sigma;) in the F435W band (magnitude 27.7 &plusmn; 0.5), but not in an overlapping band F475W (>28.5) and F555W and F625W. This weak detection is probably just an upward statistical fluctuation and does not affect the SED fitting results noticeably.</p>\n<p>The two sources in CL0024 pose an interesting question. These sources, CL0024-zD1 and CL0024-iD1, are separated by only 2&farcs;5. As we shall see below they have very similar redshifts and SEDs, and this small separation corresponds to just 2 kpc in the source plane. Thus they are likely to be spatially associated. This will be discussed further below, but for now we discuss their individual properties.</p>\n<p>The source CL0024-iD1 is resolved in both the <italic>J</italic>- and <italic>H</italic>-band images. Its NICMOS <italic>J</italic>-band magnitude is 25.1 (Table <xref ref-type=\"table\" rid=\"apj295677t2\">2</xref>). An independent check comes from our Magellan&sol;PANIC image data which gives 25.8 &plusmn; 1.1, which is consistent with the NICMOS measurement. The source CL0024-zD1 appears to be marginally resolved into two components in the <italic>J</italic><sub>110</sub> band image (Figure <xref ref-type=\"fig\" rid=\"apj295677f4\">4</xref>), but not in the <italic>H</italic><sub>160</sub> band. In the Magellan images, its magnitude is 26.3 &plusmn; 1.3, which again is consistent with the NICMOS data. The detection in the 4.5 &mu;m band is marginal, and appears to be consistent with background fluctuations.</p>\n<p>It is interesting to note that A1689-zD1 (<italic>z</italic> &sim; 7.6; Bradley et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r11\">2008</xref>) also consists of two components separated by &sim;1&nbsp;kpc in the source plane. The apparent frequency of high-redshift galaxies with two bright components is perhaps not surprising, in that we expect to see bright star-bursting galaxies imbedded in massive halos that are merging at this very early epoch.</p>\n<p>Because of the long time baseline of our ACS, NICMOS, IRAC, and ground-based observations, none of these sources are likely to be supernova outbursts or transiting objects. In addition, because the sources are detected in multiple bands, it is highly unlikely that they are spurious detections.</p></sec>\n<sec id=\"apj295677s4\"><label>4.</label><title>DISCUSSION</title>\n<p>Current studies of the UV luminosity function (LF) at <italic>z</italic> &sim; 2&ndash;6 suggest that lower-luminosity galaxies provide the dominant contribution to the overall luminosity density in the UV, the stellar mass density, the ionizing photon budget necessary for reionization, and the build-up of metals (Yan &amp; Windhorst <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r54\">2004</xref>; Yoshida et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r57\">2006</xref>; Beckwith et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r1\">2006</xref>; Bouwens et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r4\">2006</xref>, <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r5\">2007</xref>; Reddy et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r41\">2008</xref>; Chen et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r18\">2009</xref>). As such, clearly it is important to quantify the properties of this faint population. Unfortunately, this can be difficult to do in the field, due to the integration times required and due to crowding issues in deep IRAC images. This makes the present sample of highly magnified galaxy candidates at <italic>z</italic> &sim; 6&ndash;7 very useful for investigating the properties of lower-luminosity galaxies at high redshifts.</p>\n<sec id=\"apj295677s4-1\"><label>4.1.</label><title>Intrinsic Luminosity</title>\n<p>To determine the intrinsic properties of the high-redshift galaxy candidates identified in our search, it is necessary to model the effect of gravitational lensing on these sources. Fortunately, both clusters behind which our galaxy candidates lie have been the subject of substantial modeling efforts. Abell 1703 (<italic>z</italic> &equals; 0.258) is known to have a large number (i.e., 16) of multiply lensed galaxies and has been the subject of detailed modeling (Limousin et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r32\">2008</xref>). CL0024+16 (<italic>z</italic> &equals; 0.396) has also been the subject of several detailed efforts to model the lensing (Kneib et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r28\">2003</xref>; Jee et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r26\">2007</xref>; Natarajan et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r35\">2009</xref>; Zitrin et al. (<xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r58\">2009</xref>). We adopt the model of Zitrin et al. (<xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r58\">2009</xref>) as our fiducial lensing model, but compare with other models to gauge the approximate uncertainties. As shown in Table&nbsp;4, we adopt a magnification at our source position of 3.1 for A1703-iD1, 6.3 for CL0024-iD1, and 6.2 for CL0024-zD1. For CL0024+16, a wide range of magnification exists in the literature. The model of Natarajan et al. (<xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r35\">2009</xref>) yields a magnification of 2 and that of Jee et al. (<xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r26\">2007</xref>) predicts a magnification of 17&ndash;20. The low magnification value probably is arose because Natarajan et al. (<xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r35\">2009</xref>) assume the cluster mass profile as singular isothermal, and they used five multiple images of the same source near the center (as well as weak lensing constraints). The recent work of Zitrin et al. (<xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r58\">2009</xref>) takes advantage of high-quality, multiband ACS data and identifies 11 sets of multiple images covering a wide range of background redshift 0.5 &lt; <italic>z</italic> &lt; 4.5, so that the gradient of mass profile slope can be meaningfully constrained and hence more reliable magnification measurements are obtained. While we have adopted perhaps the most reliable magnification model, the actual magnification factors in current lensing models are sensitive to details in the mass distribution of the foreground cluster and thus subject to significant uncertainties.</p>\n<p>Adopting the photometric redshifts for A1703-iD1, CL0024-iD1, and CL0024-zD1 that we will determine in the next section (i.e., <italic>z</italic> &equals; 6.0, <italic>z</italic> &equals; 6.5, and <italic>z</italic> &equals; 6.6, respectively), we estimate that their amplification factors are approximately 3.1, 6.3, and 6.2. respectively. This implies intrinsic <italic>J</italic>-band luminosities for these sources of AB = 25.2, 27.1, and 28.0, respectively. The two fainter dropout sources in CL0024+16 are estimated to have intrinsic magnitudes fainter than L* after demagnification and would not likely have been found in even the NICMOS data to the HUDF (Bouwens et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r8\">2004b</xref>; Thompson et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r53\">2005</xref>). A1703-iD1, on the other hand, appears to be quite bright, having an intrinsic magnitude of &sim;25.</p></sec>\n<sec id=\"apj295677s4-2\"><label>4.2.</label><title>Spectral Energy Distribution and Photometric Redshift</title>\n<p>We performed fits to the multiband photometry of these three sources by using the stellar-population models of Bruzual &amp; Charlot (<xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r14\">2003</xref>). For all of the models, we assume a simple stellar population (single-burst; SSP) with a Salpeter (<xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r45\">1955</xref>) initial mass function with mass cutoffs of 0.1 and 100 <italic>M</italic><sub>&sun;</sub>. We explored models with both solar (<italic>Z</italic> &equals; 0.02) and subsolar (<italic>Z</italic> &equals; 0.0004 &equals; <italic>Z</italic><sub>&sun;</sub>&sol;50) metallicities. The models use the obscuration law of Calzetti et al. (<xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r16\">2000</xref>) and are corrected for Lyman-series line-blanketing and photoelectric absorption following the procedure of Madau (<xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r33\">1995</xref>). In the stellar-population model fits, we constrain the stellar age to be less than the age of the universe at the fit redshift (e.g., 0.8 Gyr at <italic>z</italic> &equals; 6.5).</p>\n<p>The best-fit stellar-population models are shown in Figures <xref ref-type=\"fig\" rid=\"apj295677f5\">5</xref>&ndash;<xref ref-type=\"fig\" rid=\"apj295677f7\">7</xref> and the parameters are given in Table <xref ref-type=\"table\" rid=\"apj295677t3\">3</xref>. While the nondetections are shown in these figures as 2 &sigma; upper limits, we used the measured fluxes, not the limits, to fit the SEDs. For both solar and subsolar metallicities, we found acceptable fits for the models. IRAC photometry places important constraints on the stellar-population models as it provides the rest-frame optical magnitudes for high-redshift galaxies (Labb&eacute; et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r30\">2006</xref>). Using the ratio of UV-optical luminosity one can estimate the galaxy age with considerable accuracy. Considering the uncertainties associated with the mass models themselves and in potential reddening factors, the real errors are likely somewhat higher than those listed in Table <xref ref-type=\"table\" rid=\"apj295677t3\">3</xref>.</p>\n<fig id=\"apj295677f5\" position=\"float\"><label>Figure 5.</label>\n<caption><p>Best-fit stellar-population models for A1703-iD1. The stellar masses tell us about the past star formation rate in a galaxy and therefore its likely contribution to the reionization of the universe as well as the production of metals. The stellar masses estimated in the previous section are useful for assessing the contribution of individual galaxies to the reionization of the universe. However, what we really want to know about the galaxy population at <italic>z</italic> &sim; 6&ndash;7 is their stellar mass density&ndash;since we can use this to constrain the SFR history at <italic>z</italic> &gt; 7. It is difficult to determine this directly from the above measurements since we only have five high-redshift galaxy candidates from which to derive a mass function (and also the masses we derive will be sensitive to uncertainties in the model magnification factors: see, e.g., R. J. Bouwens et al. 2009, in preparation).</p>\n<p>A better way of estimating the total stellar mass density is to start with determinations of the UV LF at <italic>z</italic> &sim; 6&ndash;7 and then use the measurements of UV-to-optical colors of <italic>z</italic> &sim; 6&ndash;7 galaxy candidates to estimate the stellar mass function of the galaxies (given the link between rest-frame optical colors and our estimates of stellar mass). Such colors have been available for the more UV luminous galaxy candidates at <italic>z</italic> &sim; 6&ndash;7 (Eyles et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r22\">2005</xref>; Yan et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r56\">2006</xref>; Stark et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r48\">2007</xref>), but until the present have been lacking for the less UV luminous galaxy candidates at <italic>z</italic> &sim; 6&ndash;7. This is where our sample of highly magnified galaxy candidates at <italic>z</italic> &sim; 6&ndash;7 is useful since they provide a luminosity-to-mass calibration for the less luminous galaxies that have previously lacked colors.</p>\n<p>For the sake of uniformity, we will use the <italic>H</italic> &minus; 3.6&thinsp;&mu;m colors of our sample of high-redshift galaxy candidates to represent the rest-frame UV-to-optical colors. We plot this color distribution for the four strongly lensed galaxy candidates in our sample with IRAC fluxes in Figure <xref ref-type=\"fig\" rid=\"apj295677f9\">9</xref>. We compare this distribution with the colors of more luminous galaxy candidates at <italic>z</italic> &sim; 6&ndash;7 identified in the HUDF (Yan et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r55\">2005</xref>; Labb&eacute; et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r30\">2006</xref>). No significant differences are observed between the <italic>H</italic> &minus; 3.6&thinsp;&mu;m colors of our sample and the more luminous galaxy candidates Labb&eacute; et al. (<xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r30\">2006</xref>) examined in the HUDF. A t-test yields a significance of 51&percnt;, suggesting no large differences between the ages (and mass-to-light ratios) of the lower-luminosity galaxy population at high redshift and that at higher luminosities. Our result does not suggest that lower luminosity galaxies have substantially different M&sol;L ratios than at higher luminosities, and therefore provide no indication that lower luminosity galaxies contribute anything significantly different from what we might expect based upon their contribution to the luminosity density. Our sample of lensed galaxies has similar <italic>H</italic> &minus; 3.6&thinsp;&mu;m colors to those of the more luminous sample. This suggests that the lower-luminosity galaxy population may have similar mass-to-light ratios as higher-luminosity galaxies (see Section <xref ref-type=\"sec\" rid=\"apj295677s4-4\">4.4</xref>).</p></caption>\n<graphic id=\"apj295677f9_eps\" content-type=\"print\" alt-version=\"yes\" xlink:href=\"apj295677f9_pr.eps\"/>\n<graphic id=\"apj295677f9_tb\" content-type=\"thumb\" alt-version=\"yes\" xlink:href=\"apj295677f9_tb.jpg\"/>\n<graphic id=\"apj295677f9_lr\" content-type=\"low\" alt-version=\"yes\" xlink:href=\"apj295677f9_lr.jpg\"/>\n<graphic id=\"apj295677f9_hr\" content-type=\"high\" alt-version=\"yes\" xlink:href=\"apj295677f9_hr.jpg\"/>\n<graphic id=\"apj295677f9_au\" content-type=\"author\" xlink:href=\"apj295677f9_au.ps\"/></fig></sec>\n<sec id=\"apj295677s4-6\"><label>4.6.</label><title>Morphology of High-Redshift Galaxies</title>\n<p>Strong lensing enables us to resolve otherwise unobservable spatial structures in high-redshift galaxies, and thereby investigate the structure of young galaxies and the physical processes that drive both their growth and vigorous star formation. The observed and inferred physical sizes and separations of the resolved components of the three sources reported here are given in Table <xref ref-type=\"table\" rid=\"apj295677t4\">4</xref>. For A1703-iD1, there are two knots separated by 0&farcs;45. Each of them can be fitted with a two-dimensional Gaussian with FWHM of 0&farcs;66 and 0&farcs;51, respectively. After demagnification (Table <xref ref-type=\"table\" rid=\"apj295677t4\">4</xref>), this pair of components is separated by just 0.9 kpc, suggesting a merging event that drives star formation. Both the candidates in CL0024+16, which themselves are probably related as discussed in Section <xref ref-type=\"sec\" rid=\"apj295677s4-3\">4.3</xref>, seem to consist of two components, which are best seen in the <italic>J</italic><sub>110</sub> image.</p>\n<p>It is interesting to note that the <italic>z</italic>-dropout in Abell 1689 (A1689-zD1) also has double nuclei, separated by &sim;1 kpc (Table <xref ref-type=\"table\" rid=\"apj295677t4\">4</xref>), in the source plane. Double nuclei seem to be almost ubiquitous in this sample (see Table <xref ref-type=\"table\" rid=\"apj295677t4\">4</xref>), and even A2218-iD1 (Kneib et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r27\">2004</xref>) appears to show significant substructure. One interpretation is that these galaxies may consist of multiple star-forming regions that are UV luminous. The separation between the two sub-structures seems to be sufficiently large (Table <xref ref-type=\"table\" rid=\"apj295677t4\">4</xref>) that they may be two merging systems. For A1689-zD1 or A1703-iD1 the models of Limousin et al. (<xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r31\">2007</xref>, <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r32\">2008</xref>) do not predict any counter images for these sources, and there is a lack of any luminous foreground sources that would be responsible for this lensing. Similarly as noted in Section <xref ref-type=\"sec\" rid=\"apj295677s3\">3</xref>, our models for CL0024+16 do not suggest multiple images for the sources. The complex and multiple nature of all these sources certainly suggests, as we would expect from numerical models, that interaction, merging and clumpy star formation are all aspects of the very dynamical nature of the galaxy buildup process at these redshifts.</p></sec></sec>\n<sec id=\"apj295677s5\"><label>5.</label><title>SUMMARY</title>\n<p>We have found three bright, strongly lensed <italic>i</italic>- and <italic>z</italic>-dropouts in the fields of the massive clusters Abell 1703 (see also Richard et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r44\">2009</xref>) and CL0024+16. Their large color decrements make the selection of these high-redshift galaxy candidates robust (with the exception of CL0024-zD1 for which the evidence is a little weaker), and detailed cluster mass models enable us to derive the intrinsic properties of the high-redshift galaxies. The similar redshifts, SEDs, and luminosities, and the quite small separation in projection, of the two sources in CL0024+16 suggest that they may be spatially associated with a separation of &sim;2 kpc. Using stellar-population models to fit the rest-frame UV and optical fluxes, we derive best-fit stellar masses on the order of 10<sup>9&ndash;10</sup> <italic>M</italic><sub>&sun;</sub>. Even after correcting for the lensing magnification (a factor of 3), A1703-iD1 remains intrinsically luminous. The three galaxy candidates are extended, with intrinsic sizes between 0.2 and 2 kpc, and resolved into a pair of nuclei. They, along with A1689-zD1 (Bradley et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r11\">2008</xref>), which is also double with component separation of &sim;1 kpc in the source plane, suggest that merger driven growth and star formation may be a defining characteristic of high-redshift galaxies. Bright resolved galaxies such as these will be important targets for spectroscopy with the largest ground based telescopes and future large-aperture space telescopes like the <italic>James Webb Space Telescope</italic>.</p></sec></body>\n<back>\n<ack><p>The ACS was developed under NASA contract NAS 5-32865, and this research has been supported by NASA grant NAG5-7697. They are undetected in the deep ACS images below 8500 &Aring; and have clear detections in the <italic>J</italic> and <italic>H</italic> bands. Fits to the ACS, NICMOS, and IRAC data yield robust photometric redshifts in the range <italic>z</italic> &sim; 6&ndash;7 and largely rule out the possibility that they are low-redshift interlopers. All three objects are extended, and resolved into a pair of bright knots. The bright <italic>i</italic>-band dropout in Abell 1703 has an <italic>H</italic>-band AB magnitude of 23.9, which makes it one of the brightest known galaxy candidates at <italic>z</italic> &gt; 5.5. Our model fits suggest a young, massive galaxy only &sim;60 million years old with a mass of &sim;10<sup>10</sup>&thinsp;<italic>M</italic><sub>&sun;</sub>. The dropout galaxy candidates behind CL0024+16 are separated by 2&farcs;5 (&sim;2 kpc in the source plane), and have <italic>H</italic>-band AB magnitudes of 25.0 and 25.6. Lensing models of CL0024+16 suggest that the objects have comparable intrinsic magnitudes of AB &sim;27.3, approximately one magnitude fainter than L* at <italic>z</italic> &sim; 6.5. Their similar redshifts, spectral energy distribution, and luminosities, coupled with their very close proximity on the sky, suggest that they are spatially associated, and plausibly are physically bound. Combining this sample with two previously reported, similarly magnified galaxy candidates at <italic>z</italic> &sim; 6&ndash;8, we find that complex systems with dual nuclei may be a common feature of high-redshift galaxies.</p></abstract>\n<kwd-group kwd-group-type=\"AAS\">\n<kwd>cosmology: observations</kwd>\n<kwd>galaxies: clusters: general</kwd>\n<kwd>galaxies: high-redshift</kwd>\n<kwd>gravitational lensing</kwd>\n</kwd-group>\n<custom-meta-wrap>\n<custom-meta><meta-name>ccc</meta-name><meta-value>0004-637X/09/021907+11&#x0024;30.00</meta-value></custom-meta>\n<custom-meta><meta-name>printed</meta-name><meta-value>Printed in the U.S.A.</meta-value></custom-meta>\n</custom-meta-wrap>\n</article-meta>\n</front>\n<body>\n<sec id=\"apj295677s1\"><label>1.</label><title>INTRODUCTION</title>\n<p>Deep observations and surveys with the <italic>Hubble Space Telescope</italic> (<italic>HST</italic>), such as GOODS (Giavalisco et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r24\">2004</xref>; Dickinson et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r19\">2004</xref>), the HUDF (Beckwith et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r1\">2006</xref>), the HUDF05 (Oesch et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r36\">2007</xref>), and the HUDF parallel observations (Bouwens et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r7\">2004a</xref>), have greatly enriched our knowledge of the distant universe, enabling the discovery of more than 600 <italic>i</italic><sub>775</sub>-band dropouts (<italic>i</italic>-dropout; Bouwens et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r4\">2006</xref>, <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r5\">2007</xref>), and eight <italic>z</italic><sub>850</sub>-band dropouts (<italic>z</italic>-dropout; Bouwens et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r9\">2008</xref>) which are believed to be galaxies at <italic>z</italic> &sim; 6 and 7, respectively. An L* galaxy of <italic>M</italic> &equals; &minus;20.3 at <italic>z</italic> &sim; 6 exhibits an apparent magnitude &sim;26.8 (AB, and hereafter); consequently, the majority of <italic>z</italic> &gt; 5.5 galaxies are fainter than AB = 26.</p>\n<p>Bright high-redshift galaxies are rare. Ten <italic>i</italic>-dropouts brighter than AB &equals; 25.5 were found in &sim;400 arcmin<sup>2</sup> of GOODS, HUDF, and HUDF parallel fields (Bouwens et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r4\">2006</xref>, <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r5\">2007</xref>). Two of them have been confirmed to be high-redshift objects spectroscopically (Bunker et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r15\">2003</xref>; Stanway et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r49\">2004</xref>). In a survey of the Subaru Deep Field of 767 arcmin<sup>2</sup> (Shimasaku et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r47\">2005</xref>; Taniguchi et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r52\">2005</xref>), 18 <italic>i</italic>-dropout galaxies were spectroscopically confirmed to be at <italic>z</italic> &sim; 5.7, and nine at <italic>z</italic> &sim; 6.6, but the brightest among them is only at magnitude of 25.4. In a recent survey that covers 1 deg<sup>2</sup> in the field of the Subaru/<italic>XMM-Newton</italic> Deep Survey (Ouchi et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r38\">2008b</xref>), 17 Ly&alpha; emitters (LAEs) at <italic>z</italic> &sim; 5.7 were found, of which only one is brighter than AB = 25.5 in the <italic>z</italic> band. At least one bright LAE has been found at <italic>z</italic> &sim; 6.6 (<italic>z</italic>-band magnitude 25.4, narrow band (921nm) magnitude 23.5; Ouchi et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r37\">2008a</xref>). Combining the results of these three surveys, the brightest <italic>z</italic> &sim; 6 candidate over 5000 arcmin<sup>2</sup> is at AB = 24.5 (in broad bands&ndash;LAEs are intrinsically brighter in a narrow band).</p>\n<p>Gravitational lensing by massive galaxy clusters enables the detection of high-redshift galaxies that are fainter than can be found even in very deep surveys. In addition, lensed galaxies can be resolved spatially and, if bright enough, can be candidates for spectroscopy (Hu et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r25\">2002</xref>; Kneib et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r27\">2004</xref>; Pell&oacute; et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r39\">2004</xref>; Schaerer &amp; Pell&oacute; <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r46\">2005</xref>; Richard et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r42\">2006</xref>; Frye et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r23\">2008</xref>; Bradley et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r11\">2008</xref>; Bouwens et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r10\">2009</xref>; Richard et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r43\">2008</xref>). The number of <italic>z</italic> &gsim; 6 candidates reported, however, has varied considerably depending on how aggressive the selection criteria are (and consequently how reliable the detected sources are: see, e.g., discussions in Bouwens et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r10\">2009</xref>). The <italic>HST</italic> images of clusters are the best in terms of spatial resolution and photometric accuracy, thus permitting the most reliable selections of high-redshift galaxies. One salient example is the three <italic>i</italic>-dropout arclets in Abell 2218 found by Kneib et al. (<xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r27\">2004</xref>). These arclets represent different views (multiple images) of the same source at <italic>z</italic> &equals; 6.75 &plusmn; 0.1. Bouwens et al. (<xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r6\">2003</xref>) found an <italic>i</italic>-dropout object in the field of cluster RDS1252 &minus; 29 as bright as AB = 24.2, and follow-up spectroscopy (Dow-Hygelund et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r20\">2005</xref>) confirmed it as a Lyman-break galaxy at <italic>z</italic> &equals; 5.515 and revealed stellar absorption lines in the continuum. Recently, Bradley et al. (<xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r11\">2008</xref>) discovered a galaxy candidate at <italic>z</italic> &sim; 7.6 in the field of Abell 1689 that is spatially resolved and has an observed <italic>H</italic>-band magnitude of 24.7. The cluster lensing magnifies this galaxy (A1689-zD1) by a factor of &sim;10.</p>\n<p>Taking advantage of the NICMOS data of 21 arcmin<sup>2</sup> over 11 galaxy clusters (including Abell 1689), Bouwens et al. (<xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r10\">2009</xref>) searched for strongly lensed <italic>z</italic>-dropout galaxies at <italic>z</italic> &gsim; 7 and found that A1689-zD1 is the only robust candidate in these fields. This suggests that bright high-redshift galaxies may be rare in cluster fields, even given the smaller field size of cluster regions. Since the amount of NICMOS data available over cluster fields is still very limited, there is reason to look at the surface density of such objects over larger areas and at somewhat lower redshifts, in order to overcome the effect of small number statistics.</p>\n<p>The purpose of this paper is to report on the results of a search for bright (<italic>H</italic>-band magnitude &les;25.6), highly magnified <italic>i</italic>- and <italic>z</italic>-dropout galaxies over a larger area than was used by Bouwens et al. (<xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r10\">2009</xref>). Such sources provide us with a unique opportunity to examine the properties of lower-luminosity galaxies in detail. The lower-luminosity galaxy population is of great interest because it appears to dominate the UV luminosity density, SFR density, and production of ionizing (&lambda;<sub>0</sub> &lsim; 912 &Aring;) photons (Yan &amp; Windhorst <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r54\">2004</xref>; Yoshida et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r57\">2006</xref>; Beckwith et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r1\">2006</xref>; Bouwens et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r4\">2006</xref>, <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r5\">2007</xref>; Oesch et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r36\">2007</xref>; Reddy et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r41\">2008</xref>; Frye et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r23\">2008</xref>; Chen et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r18\">2009</xref>). In particular, we consider &sim;4 arcmin<sup>2</sup> of additional search area over CL0024+16 and CL1056 &minus; 03 obtained in a recent NICMOS program HST-GO10874 (PI: W. Zheng). These areas were not included in the <italic>z</italic>-dropout search of Bouwens et al. (<xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r10\">2009</xref>).</p>\n<p>The plan for this paper is as follows. In Section <xref ref-type=\"sec\" rid=\"apj295677s2\">2</xref>, we describe the observational data. In Section <xref ref-type=\"sec\" rid=\"apj295677s3\">3</xref>, we outline our search criteria and summarize the results of our search for bright <italic>z</italic> &sim; 6&ndash;7 galaxies. In Section <xref ref-type=\"sec\" rid=\"apj295677s4\">4</xref>, we use stellar-population modeling and the available lensing models to remark on the physical properties of the high-redshift galaxy candidates we are examining, and in Section <xref ref-type=\"sec\" rid=\"apj295677s5\">5</xref>, we summarize our results.</p></sec>\n<sec id=\"apj295677s2\"><label>2.</label><title>OBSERVATIONS</title>\n<p>A summary of the <italic>HST</italic> ACS, WFPC2, and NICMOS imaging observations used here to search for <italic>z</italic> &sim; 6&ndash;7 <italic>i</italic>- or <italic>z</italic>-dropout galaxies is given in Table <xref ref-type=\"table\" rid=\"apj295677t1\">1</xref>. The object is detected in the Gemini-N image in the <italic>J</italic> band, with a magnitude of 24.2 &plusmn; 0.4. Our confidence that this source is a galaxy at <italic>z</italic> &sim; 6 rather than a lower-redshift object (Stanway et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r50\">2005</xref>) is largely motivated by the source&apos;s large color decrement of >2.6, which is twice the threshold in a normal selection (1.3&ndash;1.5). The possibility that this source is a dusty galaxy at lower redshift is ruled out as the source is roughly equally bright in the <italic>z</italic>- and <italic>J</italic>-bands, and entirely undetected in the <italic>r</italic><sub>606</sub>- (<italic>r</italic>) and <italic>i</italic>-bands, which is clearly a sign of a break in the spectral energy distribution (SED) instead of a gradual decrease toward bluer wavelengths. The source is resolved into two components (magnitudes 24.3 and 24.9; Figure <xref ref-type=\"fig\" rid=\"apj295677f3\">3</xref>) separated by &sim;0&farcs;45. The only other objects with such colors are T-dwarfs, which can be ruled out by the fact that the source is extended. The FWHM for each component is &sim;0&farcs;4, which is four times the ACS resolution. This source is detected at low significance (&lt;2&sigma;) in the F435W band (magnitude 27.7 &plusmn; 0.5), but not in an overlapping band F475W (>28.5) and F555W and F625W. This weak detection is probably just an upward statistical fluctuation and does not affect the SED fitting results noticeably.</p>\n<p>The two sources in CL0024 pose an interesting question. These sources, CL0024-zD1 and CL0024-iD1, are separated by only 2&farcs;5. As we shall see below they have very similar redshifts and SEDs, and this small separation corresponds to just 2 kpc in the source plane. Thus they are likely to be spatially associated. This will be discussed further below, but for now we discuss their individual properties.</p>\n<p>The source CL0024-iD1 is resolved in both the <italic>J</italic>- and <italic>H</italic>-band images. Its NICMOS <italic>J</italic>-band magnitude is 25.1 (Table <xref ref-type=\"table\" rid=\"apj295677t2\">2</xref>). An independent check comes from our Magellan&sol;PANIC image data which gives 25.8 &plusmn; 1.1, which is consistent with the NICMOS measurement. The source CL0024-zD1 appears to be marginally resolved into two components in the <italic>J</italic><sub>110</sub> band image (Figure <xref ref-type=\"fig\" rid=\"apj295677f4\">4</xref>), but not in the <italic>H</italic><sub>160</sub> band. In the Magellan images, its magnitude is 26.3 &plusmn; 1.3, which again is consistent with the NICMOS data. The detection in the 4.5 &mu;m band is marginal, and appears to be consistent with background fluctuations.</p>\n<p>It is interesting to note that A1689-zD1 (<italic>z</italic> &sim; 7.6; Bradley et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r11\">2008</xref>) also consists of two components separated by &sim;1&nbsp;kpc in the source plane. The apparent frequency of high-redshift galaxies with two bright components is perhaps not surprising, in that we expect to see bright star-bursting galaxies imbedded in massive halos that are merging at this very early epoch.</p>\n<p>Because of the long time baseline of our ACS, NICMOS, IRAC, and ground-based observations, none of these sources are likely to be supernova outbursts or transiting objects. In addition, because the sources are detected in multiple bands, it is highly unlikely that they are spurious detections.</p></sec>\n<sec id=\"apj295677s4\"><label>4.</label><title>DISCUSSION</title>\n<p>Current studies of the UV luminosity function (LF) at <italic>z</italic> &sim; 2&ndash;6 suggest that lower-luminosity galaxies provide the dominant contribution to the overall luminosity density in the UV, the stellar mass density, the ionizing photon budget necessary for reionization, and the build-up of metals (Yan &amp; Windhorst <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r54\">2004</xref>; Yoshida et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r57\">2006</xref>; Beckwith et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r1\">2006</xref>; Bouwens et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r4\">2006</xref>, <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r5\">2007</xref>; Reddy et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r41\">2008</xref>; Chen et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r18\">2009</xref>). As such, clearly it is important to quantify the properties of this faint population. Unfortunately, this can be difficult to do in the field, due to the integration times required and due to crowding issues in deep IRAC images. This makes the present sample of highly magnified galaxy candidates at <italic>z</italic> &sim; 6&ndash;7 very useful for investigating the properties of lower-luminosity galaxies at high redshifts.</p>\n<sec id=\"apj295677s4-1\"><label>4.1.</label><title>Intrinsic Luminosity</title>\n<p>To determine the intrinsic properties of the high-redshift galaxy candidates identified in our search, it is necessary to model the effect of gravitational lensing on these sources. Fortunately, both clusters behind which our galaxy candidates lie have been the subject of substantial modeling efforts. Abell 1703 (<italic>z</italic> &equals; 0.258) is known to have a large number (i.e., 16) of multiply lensed galaxies and has been the subject of detailed modeling (Limousin et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r32\">2008</xref>). CL0024+16 (<italic>z</italic> &equals; 0.396) has also been the subject of several detailed efforts to model the lensing (Kneib et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r28\">2003</xref>; Jee et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r26\">2007</xref>; Natarajan et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r35\">2009</xref>; Zitrin et al. (<xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r58\">2009</xref>). We adopt the model of Zitrin et al. (<xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r58\">2009</xref>) as our fiducial lensing model, but compare with other models to gauge the approximate uncertainties. As shown in Table&nbsp;4, we adopt a magnification at our source position of 3.1 for A1703-iD1, 6.3 for CL0024-iD1, and 6.2 for CL0024-zD1. For CL0024+16, a wide range of magnification exists in the literature. The model of Natarajan et al. (<xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r35\">2009</xref>) yields a magnification of 2 and that of Jee et al. (<xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r26\">2007</xref>) predicts a magnification of 17&ndash;20. The low magnification value probably is arose because Natarajan et al. (<xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r35\">2009</xref>) assume the cluster mass profile as singular isothermal, and they used five multiple images of the same source near the center (as well as weak lensing constraints). The recent work of Zitrin et al. (<xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r58\">2009</xref>) takes advantage of high-quality, multiband ACS data and identifies 11 sets of multiple images covering a wide range of background redshift 0.5 &lt; <italic>z</italic> &lt; 4.5, so that the gradient of mass profile slope can be meaningfully constrained and hence more reliable magnification measurements are obtained. While we have adopted perhaps the most reliable magnification model, the actual magnification factors in current lensing models are sensitive to details in the mass distribution of the foreground cluster and thus subject to significant uncertainties.</p>\n<p>Adopting the photometric redshifts for A1703-iD1, CL0024-iD1, and CL0024-zD1 that we will determine in the next section (i.e., <italic>z</italic> &equals; 6.0, <italic>z</italic> &equals; 6.5, and <italic>z</italic> &equals; 6.6, respectively), we estimate that their amplification factors are approximately 3.1, 6.3, and 6.2. respectively. This implies intrinsic <italic>J</italic>-band luminosities for these sources of AB = 25.2, 27.1, and 28.0, respectively. The two fainter dropout sources in CL0024+16 are estimated to have intrinsic magnitudes fainter than L* after demagnification and would not likely have been found in even the NICMOS data to the HUDF (Bouwens et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r8\">2004b</xref>; Thompson et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r53\">2005</xref>). A1703-iD1, on the other hand, appears to be quite bright, having an intrinsic magnitude of &sim;25.</p></sec>\n<sec id=\"apj295677s4-2\"><label>4.2.</label><title>Spectral Energy Distribution and Photometric Redshift</title>\n<p>We performed fits to the multiband photometry of these three sources by using the stellar-population models of Bruzual &amp; Charlot (<xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r14\">2003</xref>). For all of the models, we assume a simple stellar population (single-burst; SSP) with a Salpeter (<xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r45\">1955</xref>) initial mass function with mass cutoffs of 0.1 and 100 <italic>M</italic><sub>&sun;</sub>. We explored models with both solar (<italic>Z</italic> &equals; 0.02) and subsolar (<italic>Z</italic> &equals; 0.0004 &equals; <italic>Z</italic><sub>&sun;</sub>&sol;50) metallicities. The models use the obscuration law of Calzetti et al. (<xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r16\">2000</xref>) and are corrected for Lyman-series line-blanketing and photoelectric absorption following the procedure of Madau (<xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r33\">1995</xref>). In the stellar-population model fits, we constrain the stellar age to be less than the age of the universe at the fit redshift (e.g., 0.8 Gyr at <italic>z</italic> &equals; 6.5).</p>\n<p>The best-fit stellar-population models are shown in Figures <xref ref-type=\"fig\" rid=\"apj295677f5\">5</xref>&ndash;<xref ref-type=\"fig\" rid=\"apj295677f7\">7</xref> and the parameters are given in Table <xref ref-type=\"table\" rid=\"apj295677t3\">3</xref>. While the nondetections are shown in these figures as 2 &sigma; upper limits, we used the measured fluxes, not the limits, to fit the SEDs. For both solar and subsolar metallicities, we found acceptable fits for the models. IRAC photometry places important constraints on the stellar-population models as it provides the rest-frame optical magnitudes for high-redshift galaxies (Labb&eacute; et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r30\">2006</xref>). Using the ratio of UV-optical luminosity one can estimate the galaxy age with considerable accuracy. Considering the uncertainties associated with the mass models themselves and in potential reddening factors, the real errors are likely somewhat higher than those listed in Table <xref ref-type=\"table\" rid=\"apj295677t3\">3</xref>.</p>\n<fig id=\"apj295677f5\" position=\"float\"><label>Figure 5.</label>\n<caption><p>Best-fit stellar-population models for A1703-iD1. With the exception of the <italic>z</italic>-band photometry, which has large uncertainty, the low-redshift SED models can also reproduce the measured fluxes blueward of the break in the SED. We still favor the high-redshift solutions (<italic>z</italic> &equals; 6.5&ndash;6.6 &plusmn; 0.4) over the low-redshift models (<italic>z</italic> &equals; 1.4 &plusmn; 0.6). The solar and subsolar models have fit reddenings of <italic>A<sub>V</sub></italic> &equals; 0.0 and 0.2, respectively.</p></sec>\n<sec id=\"apj295677s4-3\"><label>4.3.</label><title>Are CL0024-iD1 and Cl0024-zD1 Physically Related?</title>\n<p>As noted in Section <xref ref-type=\"sec\" rid=\"apj295677s3\">3</xref>, the source CL0024-zD1 is located only 2&farcs;5 from the CL0024-iD1 counterpart. For our fiducial lensing model where the amplification is 6.2 (Table <xref ref-type=\"table\" rid=\"apj295677t4\">4</xref>), they would be separated physically by only 2.2 kpc. The two sources are of similar luminosity, differing by only approximately 0.7 magnitude in both the in <italic>J</italic>- and <italic>H</italic>-bands. The SED fits suggest very similar redshifts, differing only by &Delta;<italic>z</italic> &sim; 0.1, which makes them essentially identical within the errors (see Table <xref ref-type=\"table\" rid=\"apj295677t3\">3</xref>). The formal designation indicates that one is an <italic>i</italic>-dropout and one a <italic>z</italic>-dropout&ndash;at <italic>z</italic> &sim; 6.5 they have redshifts in the transition range where small changes in flux in the bands can result in a change in their dropout designation. Given the similar redshifts, SEDs, and luminosities, and the quite small separation in projection, it is possible that the two sources are simply components of the same source with a physical separation of 2 kpc. We think it is unlikely they are multiple images of the same source since our lensing models do not predict such a split pair. Both sources show dual-core morphology (see Section <xref ref-type=\"sec\" rid=\"apj295677s4-6\">4.6</xref>), and their four components seem well aligned over a scale of 6&Prime; (see the <italic>J</italic>-band image in Figure <xref ref-type=\"fig\" rid=\"apj295677f4\">4</xref>). We have kept them as separate entities throughout our discussion but suspect that it is highly likely that they are spatially associated. To a limiting magnitude of 25.6, a survey in an unlensed field is anticipated to find &sim;1.4 candidates at <italic>z</italic> &sim; 6. We, however, find 4&ndash;5 such bright <italic>z</italic> &sim; 6&ndash;7 candidates behind lensing clusters. This effectively illustrates how lensing from foreground clusters can help us identify high-redshift galaxies at relatively bright magnitudes&ndash;which is helpful for numerous follow-up studies, including spectroscopy. The observed enhancement is expected because of the steep slope of the LF expected at higher luminosities (i.e., &minus;<italic>d</italic>(log&thinsp;<italic>d</italic>&Phi;)&sol;<italic>d</italic>log&thinsp;<italic>L</italic> &gt; 1: e.g., Broadhurst et al. (<xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r12\">1995</xref>)). Such steep slopes are seen brighter than L* where there is an exponential cut-off, but not fainter than L*. For A1703-iD1, its intrinsic <italic>z</italic>-band magnitude is approximately 25 after correcting for the 3&times; model magnification factor (see Table <xref ref-type=\"table\" rid=\"apj295677t4\">4</xref>). According to the <italic>z</italic> &sim; 6 LF, one would expect to require 40&ndash;250 arcmin<sup>2</sup> to find one such bright candidate. Since the discovery of A1703-iD1 was made over an area of approximately 7 arcmin<sup>2</sup> (after correcting for the 3&times; dilution factor), it would seem to be a rather serendipitous discovery. For the four other dropout candidates listed in Table <xref ref-type=\"table\" rid=\"apj295677t4\">4</xref>, if we assume a nominal magnification factor of 9 in the cluster field, their intrinsic magnitudes are AB 27&ndash;28. Over 2 arcmin<sup>2</sup> in the source plane, the <italic>z</italic> &sim; 6 LF predicts approximately 2&ndash;5 such galaxies. The four such galaxies found are therefore consistent, within the uncertainties, with the known distribution of high-redshift galaxies.</p>\n<fig id=\"apj295677f8\" position=\"float\"><label>Figure 8.</label>\n<caption><p><italic>i</italic>-dropout surface density. The stellar masses tell us about the past star formation rate in a galaxy and therefore its likely contribution to the reionization of the universe as well as the production of metals. The stellar masses estimated in the previous section are useful for assessing the contribution of individual galaxies to the reionization of the universe. However, what we really want to know about the galaxy population at <italic>z</italic> &sim; 6&ndash;7 is their stellar mass density&ndash;since we can use this to constrain the SFR history at <italic>z</italic> &gt; 7. It is difficult to determine this directly from the above measurements since we only have five high-redshift galaxy candidates from which to derive a mass function (and also the masses we derive will be sensitive to uncertainties in the model magnification factors: see, e.g., R. J. Bouwens et al. 2009, in preparation).</p>\n<p>A better way of estimating the total stellar mass density is to start with determinations of the UV LF at <italic>z</italic> &sim; 6&ndash;7 and then use the measurements of UV-to-optical colors of <italic>z</italic> &sim; 6&ndash;7 galaxy candidates to estimate the stellar mass function of the galaxies (given the link between rest-frame optical colors and our estimates of stellar mass). Such colors have been available for the more UV luminous galaxy candidates at <italic>z</italic> &sim; 6&ndash;7 (Eyles et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r22\">2005</xref>; Yan et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r56\">2006</xref>; Stark et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r48\">2007</xref>), but until the present have been lacking for the less UV luminous galaxy candidates at <italic>z</italic> &sim; 6&ndash;7. This is where our sample of highly magnified galaxy candidates at <italic>z</italic> &sim; 6&ndash;7 is useful since they provide a luminosity-to-mass calibration for the less luminous galaxies that have previously lacked colors.</p>\n<p>For the sake of uniformity, we will use the <italic>H</italic> &minus; 3.6&thinsp;&mu;m colors of our sample of high-redshift galaxy candidates to represent the rest-frame UV-to-optical colors. We plot this color distribution for the four strongly lensed galaxy candidates in our sample with IRAC fluxes in Figure <xref ref-type=\"fig\" rid=\"apj295677f9\">9</xref>. We compare this distribution with the colors of more luminous galaxy candidates at <italic>z</italic> &sim; 6&ndash;7 identified in the HUDF (Yan et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r55\">2005</xref>; Labb&eacute; et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r30\">2006</xref>). No significant differences are observed between the <italic>H</italic> &minus; 3.6&thinsp;&mu;m colors of our sample and the more luminous galaxy candidates Labb&eacute; et al. (<xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r30\">2006</xref>) examined in the HUDF. A t-test yields a significance of 51&percnt;, suggesting no large differences between the ages (and mass-to-light ratios) of the lower-luminosity galaxy population at high redshift and that at higher luminosities. This has obvious implications for the total stellar mass density at <italic>z</italic> &sim; 6&ndash;7 and therefore the total star formation rate density at <italic>z</italic> &gsim; 7 (Chary <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r17\">2008</xref>), since it allows us to estimate the fraction of the total stellar mass density at <italic>z</italic> &sim; 6&ndash;7 that come from high- and low-luminosity systems. Our result does not suggest that lower luminosity galaxies have substantially different M&sol;L ratios than at higher luminosities, and therefore provide no indication that lower luminosity galaxies contribute anything significantly different from what we might expect based upon their contribution to the luminosity density. It is also consistent with the hypothesis that more massive galaxies are unresolved aggregates of lower-luminosity galaxies.</p>\n<fig id=\"apj295677f9\" position=\"float\"><label>Figure 9.</label>\n<caption><p><italic>H</italic> &minus; 3.6&thinsp;&mu;m color distribution (red histogram) for the lower luminosity, highly magnified galaxy candidates at <italic>z</italic> &sim; 6.5&ndash;7.6 in our selection (Table <xref ref-type=\"table\" rid=\"apj295677t2\">2</xref>). The observed and inferred physical sizes and separations of the resolved components of the three sources reported here are given in Table <xref ref-type=\"table\" rid=\"apj295677t4\">4</xref>. For A1703-iD1, there are two knots separated by 0&farcs;45. Each of them can be fitted with a two-dimensional Gaussian with FWHM of 0&farcs;66 and 0&farcs;51, respectively. After demagnification (Table <xref ref-type=\"table\" rid=\"apj295677t4\">4</xref>), this pair of components is separated by just 0.9 kpc, suggesting a merging event that drives star formation. Both the candidates in CL0024+16, which themselves are probably related as discussed in Section <xref ref-type=\"sec\" rid=\"apj295677s4-3\">4.3</xref>, seem to consist of two components, which are best seen in the <italic>J</italic><sub>110</sub> image.</p>\n<p>It is interesting to note that the <italic>z</italic>-dropout in Abell 1689 (A1689-zD1) also has double nuclei, separated by &sim;1 kpc (Table <xref ref-type=\"table\" rid=\"apj295677t4\">4</xref>), in the source plane. Double nuclei seem to be almost ubiquitous in this sample (see Table <xref ref-type=\"table\" rid=\"apj295677t4\">4</xref>), and even A2218-iD1 (Kneib et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r27\">2004</xref>) appears to show significant substructure. One interpretation is that these galaxies may consist of multiple star-forming regions that are UV luminous. The separation between the two sub-structures seems to be sufficiently large (Table <xref ref-type=\"table\" rid=\"apj295677t4\">4</xref>) that they may be two merging systems. For A1689-zD1 or A1703-iD1 the models of Limousin et al. (<xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r31\">2007</xref>, <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r32\">2008</xref>) do not predict any counter images for these sources, and there is a lack of any luminous foreground sources that would be responsible for this lensing. Similarly as noted in Section <xref ref-type=\"sec\" rid=\"apj295677s3\">3</xref>, our models for CL0024+16 do not suggest multiple images for the sources. The complex and multiple nature of all these sources certainly suggests, as we would expect from numerical models, that interaction, merging and clumpy star formation are all aspects of the very dynamical nature of the galaxy buildup process at these redshifts.</p></sec></sec>\n<sec id=\"apj295677s5\"><label>5.</label><title>SUMMARY</title>\n<p>We have found three bright, strongly lensed <italic>i</italic>- and <italic>z</italic>-dropouts in the fields of the massive clusters Abell 1703 (see also Richard et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r44\">2009</xref>) and CL0024+16. Their large color decrements make the selection of these high-redshift galaxy candidates robust (with the exception of CL0024-zD1 for which the evidence is a little weaker), and detailed cluster mass models enable us to derive the intrinsic properties of the high-redshift galaxies. The similar redshifts, SEDs, and luminosities, and the quite small separation in projection, of the two sources in CL0024+16 suggest that they may be spatially associated with a separation of &sim;2 kpc. Using stellar-population models to fit the rest-frame UV and optical fluxes, we derive best-fit stellar masses on the order of 10<sup>9&ndash;10</sup> <italic>M</italic><sub>&sun;</sub>. Even after correcting for the lensing magnification (a factor of 3), A1703-iD1 remains intrinsically luminous. The three galaxy candidates are extended, with intrinsic sizes between 0.2 and 2 kpc, and resolved into a pair of nuclei. They, along with A1689-zD1 (Bradley et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apj295677r11\">2008</xref>), which is also double with component separation of &sim;1 kpc in the source plane, suggest that merger driven growth and star formation may be a defining characteristic of high-redshift galaxies. Bright resolved galaxies such as these will be important targets for spectroscopy with the largest ground based telescopes and future large-aperture space telescopes like the <italic>James Webb Space Telescope</italic>.</p></sec></body>\n<back>\n<ack><p>The ACS was developed under NASA contract NAS 5-32865, and this research has been supported by NASA grant NAG5-7697.
