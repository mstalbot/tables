INTRODUCTION</a></h2>\n<p>\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0The number of spectroscopically confirmed high-redshift galaxies is increasing rapidly. Steidel et al. (<a name=\"crf19\" href=\"#rf19\" id=\"crf19\">1996a</a>, <a name=\"crf18\" href=\"#rf18\" id=\"crf18\">1996b</a>) demonstrated that galaxies at redshifts <i>z</i> <img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/sim.gif\" alt=\"&#8764;\" align=\"BOTTOM\" /> 3 can be found very efficiently by deep imaging and color selection (the <i>U</i>- or <i>B</i>-band <img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/ldquo.gif\" alt=\"&#8220;\" align=\"TOP\" />dropout<img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/rdquo.gif\" alt=\"&#8221;\" align=\"TOP\" /> technique). The absorption to the blue of Ly<img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/agr.gif\" alt=\"&amp;agr;\" align=\"BOTTOM\" /> by the Ly<img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/agr.gif\" alt=\"&amp;agr;\" align=\"BOTTOM\" /> forest, and by source and intergalactic H <font size=\"-1\">I</font> beyond the Ly limit, are unique signatures of high-redshift objects (<a name=\"crf13\" href=\"#rf13\" id=\"crf13\">Madau 1995</a>; <a name=\"crf15\" href=\"#rf15\" id=\"crf15\">McMahon 1997</a>). Deep searches for Ly<img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/agr.gif\" alt=\"&amp;agr;\" align=\"BOTTOM\" /> emitters have been less successful but have recently resulted in detections at high redshift (e.g., <a name=\"crf6\" href=\"#rf6\" id=\"crf6\">Hu &amp; McMahon 1996</a>; <a name=\"crf2\" href=\"#rf2\" id=\"crf2\">Djorgovski et al. 1996</a>). Although most of the photometric searches have concentrated on galaxies at redshifts <i>z</i> = 3<img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/ndash.gif\" alt=\"&#8211;\" align=\"BOTTOM\" />4, similar searches can, in principle, be used to find even higher redshift galaxies.</p>\n<p>\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0Our serendipitous discovery of two galaxies at <i>z</i> = 4.92 reported here suggests that <i>z</i> <img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/ge.gif\" alt=\"&#8805;\" align=\"BOTTOM\" /> 5 galaxies may be found by similar photometric <img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/ldquo.gif\" alt=\"&#8220;\" align=\"TOP\" />dropout<img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/rdquo.gif\" alt=\"&#8221;\" align=\"TOP\" /> techniques. One of the galaxies is strongly gravitationally lensed and is detected as a very red arc in the <i>z</i> = 0.33 cluster CL 1358+62. This cluster was selected for detailed study of its member galaxies using <i>Hubble Space Telescope</i> (<i>HST</i>) imaging and extensive William Hershel Telescope (WHT) and Keck spectroscopy. An earlier search for arcs in this cluster was unsuccessful (<a name=\"crf10\" href=\"#rf10\" id=\"crf10\">Le F\u00e8vre et al. 1994</a>), so the presence of the arc in the <i>HST</i> image was unexpected. <i>HST</i> and Keck are able to exploit the magnification provided by the cluster lens to provide spectroscopic observations of a high-redshift galaxy at unprecedented spatial resolution.</p>\n<h2 align=\"CENTER\"><a name=\"sc2\" id=\"sc2\">\u00a72. OBSERVATIONS</a></h2>\n<p>\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0We obtained a large mosaic of multicolor WFPC2 images of the cluster for a detailed study of the cluster members. The integration time was 3600 s in each of the F606W (<img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/ldquo.gif\" alt=\"&#8220;\" align=\"TOP\" />R<img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/rdquo.gif\" alt=\"&#8221;\" align=\"TOP\" />) and F814W (<img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/ldquo.gif\" alt=\"&#8220;\" align=\"TOP\" />I<img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/rdquo.gif\" alt=\"&#8221;\" align=\"TOP\" />) filters. The data have been described in <a name=\"crf7\" href=\"#rf7\" id=\"crf7\">Kelson et al. (1997)</a> and <a name=\"crf22\" href=\"#rf22\" id=\"crf22\">van Dokkum et al. (1997)</a>. A color image of the central part of the cluster is shown in <a name=\"dfg1\" href=\"#fg1\" id=\"dfg1\">Figure 1</a> (Plate L6). Several arcs can be seen, of which the long red arc (B<img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/ndash.gif\" alt=\"&#8211;\" align=\"BOTTOM\" />C) is the brightest, lying 21<img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/arcsec.gif\" alt=\"&amp;arcsec;\" align=\"TOP\" /> \u00b1 1<img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/arcsec.gif\" alt=\"&amp;arcsec;\" align=\"TOP\" /> from the central cD galaxy. This arc is distorted by the bright elliptical galaxy north of component B. An additional faint lensed component D is located still further north. The arc is strikingly knotty, with both tangential and radial structure.</p>\n<a name=\"dfg1\" href=\"#fg1\" id=\"fg1\"><img  src=\"fg1t.gif\" align=\"BOTTOM\" /> Fig. 1</a>\n<p>\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0The symmetric distribution of the knots suggest that we are seeing a fold arc. We therefore searched for the expected counterimages. We identified a candidate counterimage 29<img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/farcs.gif\" alt=\"&amp;farcs;\" align=\"BOTTOM\" /> 5 east of the cD galaxy, marked A in <a href=\"#fg1\">Figure 1</a>. The colors of the arc and the counterimage are consistent.</p>\n<p>\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0Spectra of the arc and the counterimage were obtained at the W. M. Keck Telescope in 1996 May and June, and 1997 February. The best spectrum was taken with a long slit in February, using the Low Resolution Imaging Spectrograph (LRIS). For this LRIS spectrum, the slit was aligned with the arc (B<img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/ndash.gif\" alt=\"&#8211;\" align=\"BOTTOM\" />C), and a resolution of 4.3 \u00c5 FWHM was attained. The spectrum is shown in <a name=\"dfg2\" href=\"#fg2\" id=\"dfg2\">Figure 2</a> (Plate L7). The dominant features are the emission line at 7204 \u00c5 and the continuum drop to the blue side of this emission line. The continuum disappears further in the blue. The spectrum varies along the arc (e.g., the continuum is enhanced in the bright knot and the ratio of Ly<img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/agr.gif\" alt=\"&amp;agr;\" align=\"BOTTOM\" /> to continuum changes).</p>\n<a name=\"dfg2\" href=\"#fg2\" id=\"fg2\"><img  src=\"fg2t.gif\" align=\"BOTTOM\" /> Fig. 2</a>\n<p>\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0<a name=\"dfg3\" href=\"#fg3\" id=\"dfg3\">Figure 3</a> is a spatially averaged spectrum showing the emission line and continuum step at 7204 \u00c5 and absorption lines to the red. We mark the absorption-line features found by <a name=\"crf12\" href=\"#rf12\" id=\"crf12\">Lowenthal et al. (1997)</a> in an average of 12 high-redshift galaxies. Each absorption line can be identified in the arc spectrum. These spectral features provide a secure redshift for the source galaxy (<i>z</i> = 4.92) and allow us to identify the emission feature as Ly<img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/agr.gif\" alt=\"&amp;agr;\" align=\"BOTTOM\" />. The redshift of <i>z</i> = 4.92 is currently the highest spectroscopically measured for a galaxy or active nucleus. The narrow Ly<img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/agr.gif\" alt=\"&amp;agr;\" align=\"BOTTOM\" /> emission, the absence of other emission lines, and the broad spatial extent imply that the continuum is stellar.</p>\n<a name=\"dfg3\" href=\"#fg3\" id=\"fg3\"><img  src=\"fg3t.gif\" align=\"BOTTOM\" /> Fig. 3</a>\n<p>\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0The spectrum suggests that the continuum break across Ly<img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/agr.gif\" alt=\"&amp;agr;\" align=\"BOTTOM\" /> is about a factor of 1.9. This is an underestimate, as the break is very likely diluted by a weak contribution from the bright cluster galaxy. This galaxy contributes a flux in the <i>I</i> band similar to that of the arc. The broadband colors, and the spectrum of the bright knot at the end of the arc suggest that the true flux ratio across the break is a factor of 3 or more.</p>\n<p>\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0We also obtained spectra of the candidate counterimage in multislit mode. It was found to have an emission line at the same redshift as the arc (<a name=\"dfg4\" href=\"#fg4\" id=\"dfg4\">Fig. 4<i>b</i></a>), confirming its status as a counterimage of the arc. In what follows, we refer to the galaxy that is gravitationally lensed into these arcs as G1. To our surprise, we found an unexpected emission line from a faint, red, galaxy (<img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/ldquo.gif\" alt=\"&#8220;\" align=\"TOP\" />G2<img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/rdquo.gif\" alt=\"&#8221;\" align=\"TOP\" />) that lies 37<img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/arcsec.gif\" alt=\"&amp;arcsec;\" align=\"TOP\" /> west of the arc (<a href=\"#fg4\">Fig. 4<i>c</i></a>). It was observed by chance through a slitlet pointed at another galaxy. It has the same shape and width as the Ly<img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/agr.gif\" alt=\"&amp;agr;\" align=\"BOTTOM\" /> emission from the arc. Both Ly<img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/agr.gif\" alt=\"&amp;agr;\" align=\"BOTTOM\" /> lines have a tail to the red, and a steep cutoff in the blue. This signature has been found previously in two nearby starburst galaxies (<a name=\"crf11\" href=\"#rf11\" id=\"crf11\">Lequeux et al. 1995</a>; <a name=\"crf8\" href=\"#rf8\" id=\"crf8\">Kunth et al. 1996</a>). As these authors show, this asymmetry is caused by an outflow. Our results indicate that high-resolution spectra can be used to distinguish between Ly<img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/agr.gif\" alt=\"&amp;agr;\" align=\"BOTTOM\" /> emission and O [<font size=\"-1\">II</font>] 3727 \u00c5: at high resolution O [<font size=\"-1\">II</font>] is resolved into a symmetric doublet, without the asymmetry displayed by Ly<img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/agr.gif\" alt=\"&amp;agr;\" align=\"BOTTOM\" /> (<a href=\"#fg4\">Fig. 4</a>).</p>\n<a name=\"dfg4\" href=\"#fg4\" id=\"fg4\"><img  src=\"fg4t.gif\" align=\"BOTTOM\" /> Fig. 4</a>\n<p>\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0The wavelength peaks of the lines in object G2 and the arcs are very similar: we measure a wavelength of 7193 \u00c5 for G2, versus 7204 \u00c5 for the arcs. Object G2 has a magnitude of <i>I</i><sub>814,<i>AB</i></sub> = 25.1 and is also very red (see <a href=\"#fg1\">Fig. 1</a>). Thus, the emission is highly likely to be Ly<img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/agr.gif\" alt=\"&amp;agr;\" align=\"BOTTOM\" />, and G2 is at <i>z</i> = 4.92. After correction for lensing (see below), the projected distance between G2 and G1 is 200 <i>h</i><img  src=\"img1.gif\" align=\"MIDDLE\" alt=\"$\\mathstrut{^{-1}_{50}}$\" /> kpc, and the velocity difference is 450 km s<sup>-1</sup> (with an unknown error dominated by the outflow in both galaxies).</p>\n<h2 align=\"CENTER\"><a name=\"sc3\" id=\"sc3\">\u00a73. ANALYSIS</a></h2>\n<p>\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0The imaging and spectroscopy leave little doubt that we have found a multiply-imaged, distant galaxy. We have been able to reproduce the geometry of the arcs by modeling the cluster as an isothermal potential. An additional isothermal potential was added to model the effect of the elliptical galaxy north of the arc. The general form of the model is shown in <a name=\"dfg5\" href=\"#fg5\" id=\"dfg5\">Figure 5</a> (Plate L8). This model successfully reproduces the fold arc and the counterarc. Furthermore, the model produces only one counterimage, because the source lies outside the radial caustic. The Einstein radius of this model is 21<img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/arcsec.gif\" alt=\"&amp;arcsec;\" align=\"TOP\" />. For an isothermal model, this implies a velocity dispersion of 970 (990) km s<sup>-1</sup> for <i>q</i><sub>0</sub> = 0.5 (0.05), which is similar to the observed dispersion of 1080 km s<sup>-1</sup> for the galaxies (<a name=\"crf50\" href=\"#rf50\" id=\"crf50\">Fisher et al. 1997</a>).</p>\n<a name=\"dfg5\" href=\"#fg5\" id=\"fg5\"><img  src=\"fg5t.gif\" align=\"BOTTOM\" /> Fig. 5</a>\n<p>\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0We use this lens model to reconstruct the intensity distribution of the source galaxy, G1. The two parts of the fold arc, and the counterimage were reconstructed separately. The reconstructions are shown in <a href=\"#fg1\">Figure 1</a> with the color information and with higher contrast in <a href=\"#fg5\">Figure 5</a>. The resolution varies from one reconstruction to the next but is particularly high for the eastern part of the fold arc where the magnification is large (5<img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/ndash.gif\" alt=\"&#8211;\" align=\"BOTTOM\" />11\u00d7). The morphology is a strong function of the resolution. At the lowest resolution (with 0<img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/farcs.gif\" alt=\"&amp;farcs;\" align=\"BOTTOM\" /> 05 \u00d7 0<img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/farcs.gif\" alt=\"&amp;farcs;\" align=\"BOTTOM\" /> 1 pixels in the source plane), the galaxy appears to consist of a bright knot and a diffuse extended structure. These features contribute roughly equally to the total flux. At the highest resolution, half of the flux of the extended structure is resolved into small knots. The bright knot is very small. It is extended in a Planetary Camera (PC) image, but this is partly due to the PC point-spread function. After deconvolution and reconstruction, we find that the half-light radius of the knot is 22 mas or <img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/ap.gif\" alt=\"&amp;ap;\" align=\"BOTTOM\" />130 <i>h</i><img  src=\"img1.gif\" align=\"MIDDLE\" alt=\"$\\mathstrut{^{-1}_{50}}$\" /> pc (<i>q</i><sub>0</sub> = 0.5).</p>\n<p>\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0Nearby starburst galaxies show comparable irregular morphologies in the UV (<a name=\"crf16\" href=\"#rf16\" id=\"crf16\">Meurer et al. 1995</a>) with dense knots of star formation. <a href=\"#rf16\">Meurer et al. 1995</a> refer to these knots as <img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/ldquo.gif\" alt=\"&#8220;\" align=\"TOP\" />super<img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/ndash.gif\" alt=\"&#8211;\" align=\"BOTTOM\" />star clusters<img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/rdquo.gif\" alt=\"&#8221;\" align=\"TOP\" /> and suggest that they are possibly the progenitors of globular clusters. The dense knots can contribute between 20% to 50% of the UV flux. The bright knot in G1 is much more luminous than those in nearby starburst systems, but otherwise the similarity is striking.</p>\n<h3 align=\"CENTER\"><a name=\"sc3.1\" id=\"sc3.1\">\u00a73.1. <i>Star Formation at z = 5</i></a></h3>\n<p>\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0The lens model implies that the unlensed magnitudes of G1 and G2 are <i>I</i><sub>814,<i>AB</i></sub> <img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/ap.gif\" alt=\"&amp;ap;\" align=\"BOTTOM\" /> 24 and 25.4, respectively. The corresponding star formation rates (SFRs) are 36 and 9 <i>M</i><sub><img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/odot.gif\" alt=\"&amp;odot;\" align=\"BOTTOM\" /></sub> yr<sup>-1</sup> (<i>q</i><sub>0</sub> = 0.5). This is based on model predictions by <a name=\"crf1\" href=\"#rf1\" id=\"crf1\">Bruzual &amp; Charlot (1993)</a> for a stellar population with continuous star formation at an age of 10<sup>8</sup> yr and a Salpeter initial mass function. An instantaneous burst model requires a minimum mass of 3 \u00d7 10<sup>8</sup> <i>M</i><sub><img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/odot.gif\" alt=\"&amp;odot;\" align=\"BOTTOM\" /></sub>. If dust is present, the above SFRs would increase, possibly substantially (<a name=\"crf17\" href=\"#rf17\" id=\"crf17\">Meurer et al. 1997</a>). Even so, the inferred SFR is very high. Over a period of 10<sup>8</sup> yr, which is only 1<img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/solm0.gif\" alt=\"&amp;solm0;\" align=\"BOTTOM\" />6 of the Hubble time at <i>z</i> = 4.92, 20%<img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/ndash.gif\" alt=\"&#8211;\" align=\"BOTTOM\" />40% of the mass of the Galactic bulge can be formed.</p>\n<p>\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0Meurer et al. (<a href=\"#rf16\">1995</a>, <a href=\"#rf17\">1997</a>) suggest that there is a natural upper limit to the surface brightness of starbursts. We find that the mean surface brightness of G1 is low at 1.4 \u00d7 10<sup>10</sup> <i>L</i><sub><img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/odot.gif\" alt=\"&amp;odot;\" align=\"BOTTOM\" /></sub> kpc<sup>-2</sup>. However, the surface brightness of the knot in G1 is much higher, at 3 \u00d7 10<sup>12</sup> <i>L</i><sub><img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/odot.gif\" alt=\"&amp;odot;\" align=\"BOTTOM\" /></sub> kpc<sup>-2</sup>. This is significantly higher than the 90th percentile upper limit of 2 \u00d7 10<sup>11</sup> <i>L</i><sub><img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/odot.gif\" alt=\"&amp;odot;\" align=\"BOTTOM\" /></sub> kpc<sup>-2</sup> derived by <a href=\"#rf17\">Meurer et al. (1997)</a>, although it is close to the highest value in their sample. The bright knot in G1 may appear extreme because G1 is unique amongst high-redshift galaxies in being mapped with very high spatial resolution (from the magnification provided by the cluster lens). At the usual WFPC2 resolution, the knot in G1 is undersampled. This undersampling may pose a problem for size estimates based on WFPC2 observations of unlensed high-redshift galaxies.</p>\n<p>\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0The bright knot in G1 is too massive to evolve into a typical globular cluster. If the knot remains bound, it is more likely to end up in the nucleus of the galaxy, and it is possibly one of the progenitors of a bulge. A dynamical mass estimate of the knot would be valuable.</p>\n<h3 align=\"CENTER\"><a name=\"sc3.2\" id=\"sc3.2\">\u00a73.2. <i>Velocities and Spatial Structure in the Spectrum</i></a></h3>\n<p>\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0The arc is the first high-redshift galaxy for which it is possible to obtain spatially-resolved spectral information (<a name=\"dfg6\" href=\"#fg6\" id=\"dfg6\">Fig. 6</a>). The peak of the Ly<img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/agr.gif\" alt=\"&amp;agr;\" align=\"BOTTOM\" /> emission varies by about 7 \u00c5. This variation is real and is larger than expected from positional variations of the knots on the slit. The Si <font size=\"-1\">II</font> 1260 \u00c5 line is blueshifted with respect to the Ly<img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/agr.gif\" alt=\"&amp;agr;\" align=\"BOTTOM\" /> peak by 300 km s<sup>-1</sup>, and the velocity variation is on the order of 300 km s<sup>-1</sup>. The width of the Si <font size=\"-1\">II</font> line is approximately constant at 12 \u00c5 (or 2 \u00c5 in the rest frame), which is equivalent to a velocity dispersion of 200 km s<sup>-1</sup>. However, these velocities should <i>not</i> be interpreted as indicative of the gravitational potential of G1.</p>\n<a name=\"dfg6\" href=\"#fg6\" id=\"fg6\"><img  src=\"fg6t.gif\" align=\"BOTTOM\" /> Fig. 6</a>\n<p>\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0The blueshifted Si <font size=\"-1\">II</font> absorption and the asymmetric Ly<img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/agr.gif\" alt=\"&amp;agr;\" align=\"BOTTOM\" /> emission are naturally explained by an outflow model. Outflowing neutral and ionized material accounts for the Si <font size=\"-1\">II</font> absorption and absorbs the blue part of the Ly<img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/agr.gif\" alt=\"&amp;agr;\" align=\"BOTTOM\" /> emission line. The low continuum blueward of Ly<img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/agr.gif\" alt=\"&amp;agr;\" align=\"BOTTOM\" /> (<a href=\"#fg2\">Fig. 2</a>) is consistent with the presence of a significant, blueshifted H <font size=\"-1\">I</font> column. G1 is thus similar to Mrk 33, a nearby starburst galaxy. <a href=\"#rf11\">Lequeux et al. (1995)</a> observed the same blueshifted interstellar absorption lines and asymmetric Ly<img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/agr.gif\" alt=\"&amp;agr;\" align=\"BOTTOM\" /> profile in Mrk 33. They verified that the interstellar absorption lines were blueshifted with respect to the bulk of the galaxy. Another such case was presented by <a href=\"#rf8\">Kunth et al. (1996)</a>. Furthermore, <a name=\"crf5\" href=\"#rf5\" id=\"crf5\">Heckman &amp; Leitherer (1997)</a> found unambiguous evidence that UV absorption lines in NGC 1705 are produced by outflowing material. In general, outflows are present in most luminous starbursts at low redshifts (e.g., <a name=\"crf4\" href=\"#rf4\" id=\"crf4\">Heckman, Armus, &amp; Miley 1990</a>).</p>\n<p>\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0Outflows may also be the norm for high-redshift galaxies: G2 has the same asymmetric Ly<img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/agr.gif\" alt=\"&amp;agr;\" align=\"BOTTOM\" /> profile, and the Ly<img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/agr.gif\" alt=\"&amp;agr;\" align=\"BOTTOM\" /> emitter found by <a href=\"#rf2\">Djorgovski et al. (1996)</a> has a red tail. Furthermore, the average spectrum of 12 high-redshift galaxies obtained by <a href=\"#rf12\">Lowenthal et al. (1997)</a> shows a velocity offset between the Ly<img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/agr.gif\" alt=\"&amp;agr;\" align=\"BOTTOM\" /> emission, and the Si <font size=\"-1\">II</font> absorption. <a name=\"crf21\" href=\"#rf21\" id=\"crf21\">Trager et al. (1997)</a> found a similar blueshift for two galaxies at <i>z</i> = 4.</p>\n<p>\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0The Ly<img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/agr.gif\" alt=\"&amp;agr;\" align=\"BOTTOM\" /> emission may also be redshifted with respect to the systemic velocity because of absorption by the Ly<img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/agr.gif\" alt=\"&amp;agr;\" align=\"BOTTOM\" /> forest. This does not appear to be the dominant effect for this galaxy, as the high-resolution spectrum in <a href=\"#fg4\">Figure 4<i>a</i></a> shows a very strong drop in the blue. Furthermore, the blue edge of the line shows velocity variations across the slit. The case is less clear for other high-redshift galaxies, where the spectra are typically at lower resolution.</p>\n<p>\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0The apparent ubiquity of outflows in galaxies with substantial SFRs, casts doubt on the suggestion by <a href=\"#rf18\">Steidel et al. (1996b)</a> that regular rotation causes the broadening of the interstellar absorption lines in high-redshift galaxies. This is unfortunate since it would be very useful to find a method of measuring the mass of high-redshift galaxies. The large width (200 km s<sup>-1</sup>) of the Si <font size=\"-1\">II</font> line toward the bright knot in the galaxy is another argument against a rotational origin for the observed motions. It is hard to reconcile a systematic velocity variation of this magnitude across the knot with the small size of the knot. The velocity variations in the Ly<img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/agr.gif\" alt=\"&amp;agr;\" align=\"BOTTOM\" /> and Si <font size=\"-1\">II</font> across the arc are probably due to differences in the outflow velocity and absorption column density. The bottom line is that wavelength gradients in Ly<img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/agr.gif\" alt=\"&amp;agr;\" align=\"BOTTOM\" /> or the interstellar absorption lines cannot reliably be used to infer reliably the mass of such star-forming galaxies.</p>\n<h2 align=\"CENTER\"><a name=\"sc4\" id=\"sc4\">\u00a74. CONCLUSIONS</a></h2>\n<p>\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0We have discovered a red arc and a counterimage of a distant galaxy that is gravitationally lensed by the cluster CL 1358+62 at <i>z</i> = 0.33. The lensed galaxy and a second companion galaxy have secure redshifts of <i>z</i> = 4.92. The arc has an irregular structure, and image reconstructions show that most of the flux comes from very small knots. The morphology of the reconstructed galaxy is very similar to that of nearby starburst galaxies in the UV (<a href=\"#rf16\">Meurer et al. 1995</a>), which are also very irregular with dense star-forming knots. The surface brightness of the brightest knot in the lensed galaxy is higher than the 90th percentile upper limit derived by <a href=\"#rf17\">Meurer et al. (1997)</a> for nearby and distant starbursts. The mass and size of this bright knot suggest that it might be a <img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/ldquo.gif\" alt=\"&#8220;\" align=\"TOP\" />bulge-building<img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/rdquo.gif\" alt=\"&#8221;\" align=\"TOP\" /> subclump. It remains to be seen whether this lensed galaxy (G1) is typical of the population of starbursting galaxies at <i>z</i> <img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/sim.gif\" alt=\"&#8764;\" align=\"BOTTOM\" /> 5.</p>\n<p>\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0The irregular structure of G1 can be contrasted with the <i>z</i> = 3 galaxies studied by <a name=\"crf3\" href=\"#rf3\" id=\"crf3\">Giavalisco et al. (1996)</a>. These authors found that many of those galaxies are compact with little substructure. The high-redshift galaxies in the Hubble Deep Field are more similar to our reconstructed images of G1 (e.g., <a href=\"#rf12\">Lowenthal et al. 1997</a>; <a href=\"#rf19\">Steidel et al. 1996a</a>). However, the morphology of G1 is a strong function of resolution: at the lowest resolution, half of the flux appears to originate from a smooth, extended component. Half the flux of this component is resolved into knots at the highest resolution. Other high-redshift galaxies might display similar irregular morphologies if they were to be imaged at such high resolution.</p>\n<p>\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0Our results, and the data presented by others, indicate that radial outflows dominate the kinematics of the absorption-line gas in high-redshift galaxies. These radial outflows are a general feature of luminous starburst galaxies in the nearby universe. Radial outflows of hundreds of kilometers per second can quickly disperse the reservoir of gas from which the stars formed. Thus, the rest-UV appearance of these galaxies is likely to be dominated by short-lived areas of intense star formation. IR observations are needed to characterize the overall morphology, since the UV structure will not be representative of the distribution of older stars in these galaxies.</p>\n<p>\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0Our serendipitous discovery of the two galaxies at <i>z</i> = 4.92 raises the question of the surface density of such objects. Their unlensed <i>I</i><sub>814,<i>AB</i></sub> magnitudes are estimated to be 24 and 25.4. The results by <a name=\"crf9\" href=\"#rf9\" id=\"crf9\">Lanzetta, Yahil, &amp; Fern\u00e1ndez-Soto (1996)</a> suggest an upper limit of <i>z</i> &gt; 4 galaxies of 0.4 arcmin<sup>-2</sup> for <i>I</i><sub>814,<i>AB</i></sub> &lt; 26. <a name=\"crf14\" href=\"#rf14\" id=\"crf14\">Madau et al. (1996)</a> found <i>B</i> dropout candidates in the Hubble Deep Field with <i>I</i><sub>814,<i>AB</i></sub> magnitudes of 26 or higher. The <i>I</i><sub>606,<i>AB</i></sub> - <i>I</i><sub>814,<i>AB</i></sub> color of the arc is in good agreement with the models of high-redshift starbursts (<a href=\"#rf14\">Madau et al. 1996</a>), which indicates that more candidates might be found by shifting the dropout techniques further into the red. Finding such objects with emission-line searches may be more difficult. While the unlensed Ly<img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/agr.gif\" alt=\"&amp;agr;\" align=\"BOTTOM\" /> flux of the arc is comparable to the fluxes for the Ly<img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/agr.gif\" alt=\"&amp;agr;\" align=\"BOTTOM\" /> selected galaxies found by <a href=\"#rf6\">Hu &amp; McMahon (1996)</a>, it is comparable to the rms noise in the deep field searches for Ly<img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/agr.gif\" alt=\"&amp;agr;\" align=\"BOTTOM\" /> (e.g., <a name=\"crf20\" href=\"#rf20\" id=\"crf20\">Thompson, Djorgovski, &amp; Trauger 1995</a>).</p>\n<h2 align=\"CENTER\"><a name=\"ack\" id=\"ack\">ACKNOWLEDGMENTS</a></h2>\n<p>\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0We thank Dan Fabricant and Tim Heckman for their comments on the manuscript. Keck spectra of the arc show Ly<img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/agr.gif\" alt=\"&amp;agr;\" align=\"BOTTOM\" /> emission at 7204 \u00c5, a continuum drop blueward of the line, and several absorption lines to the red. We identify the arc as a gravitationally lensed galaxy at a redshift of <i>z</i> = 4.92. It is the highest redshift object currently known. A gravitational lens model was used to reconstruct images of the high-redshift galaxy. The reconstructed image is asymmetric, containing a bright knot and a patch of extended emission 0<img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/farcs.gif\" alt=\"&amp;farcs;\" align=\"BOTTOM\" />4 from the knot. The effective radius of the bright knot is 0<img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/farcs.gif\" alt=\"&amp;farcs;\" align=\"BOTTOM\" />022 or 130 <i>h</i><img  src=\"img1.gif\" align=\"MIDDLE\" alt=\"$\\mathstrut{^{-1}_{50}}$\" /> pc. The extended patch is partially resolved into compact regions of star formation. The reconstructed galaxy has <i>I</i><sub><i>AB</i></sub> = 24, giving a bolometric luminosity of about 3 \u00d7 10<sup>11</sup> <i>L</i><sub><img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/odot.gif\" alt=\"&amp;odot;\" align=\"BOTTOM\" /></sub>. This can be produced by a star formation rate of 36 <i>h</i><img  src=\"img2.gif\" align=\"MIDDLE\" alt=\"$\\mathstrut{^{-2}_{50}}$\" /> <i>M</i><sub><img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/odot.gif\" alt=\"&amp;odot;\" align=\"BOTTOM\" /></sub> yr<sup>-1</sup> (<i>q</i><sub>0</sub> = 0.5) or by an instantaneous starburst of 3 \u00d7 10<sup>8</sup> <i>M</i><sub><img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/odot.gif\" alt=\"&amp;odot;\" align=\"BOTTOM\" /></sub>. The spectral lines show velocity variations on the order of 300 km s<sup>-1</sup> along the arc. The Si <font size=\"-1\">II</font> line is blueshifted with respect to the Ly<img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/agr.gif\" alt=\"&amp;agr;\" align=\"BOTTOM\" /> emission, and the Ly<img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/agr.gif\" alt=\"&amp;agr;\" align=\"BOTTOM\" /> emission line is asymmetric with a red tail. These spectral features are naturally explained by an outflow model, in which the blue side of the Ly<img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/agr.gif\" alt=\"&amp;agr;\" align=\"BOTTOM\" /> line has been absorbed by outflowing neutral H <font size=\"-1\">I</font>. Evidence from other sources indicates that outflows are common in starburst galaxies at high and low redshift. We have discovered a companion galaxy with a radial velocity only 450 km s<sup>-1</sup> different than that of the arc. The serendipitous discovery of these two galaxies suggests that systematic searches may uncover galaxies at even higher redshifts.</p>\n<p><a name=\"keywords\" id=\"keywords\"><i>Subject headings:</i></a> galaxies: clusters: individual (CL 1358+62)<img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/mdash.gif\" alt=\"&#8212;\" align=\"BOTTOM\" />galaxies: evolution<img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/mdash.gif\" alt=\"&#8212;\" align=\"BOTTOM\" />galaxies: formation<img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/mdash.gif\" alt=\"&#8212;\" align=\"BOTTOM\" />galaxies: starburst<img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/mdash.gif\" alt=\"&#8212;\" align=\"BOTTOM\" />gravitational lensing</p>\n<hr />\n<h3>FOOTNOTES</h3>\n<p>\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0<a href=\"#cfn1\" name=\"fn1\" id=\"fn1\"><sup>1</sup></a> Based on observations taken with the NASA<img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/solm0.gif\" alt=\"&amp;solm0;\" align=\"BOTTOM\" />ESA <i>Hubble Space Telescope</i> obtained at the Space Telescope Science Institute, which is operated by AURA under NASA contract NAS5-26555, and observations obtained at the W. INTRODUCTION</a></h2>\n<p>\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0The number of spectroscopically confirmed high-redshift galaxies is increasing rapidly. Steidel et al. (<a name=\"crf19\" href=\"#rf19\" id=\"crf19\">1996a</a>, <a name=\"crf18\" href=\"#rf18\" id=\"crf18\">1996b</a>) demonstrated that galaxies at redshifts <i>z</i> <img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/sim.gif\" alt=\"&#8764;\" align=\"BOTTOM\" /> 3 can be found very efficiently by deep imaging and color selection (the <i>U</i>- or <i>B</i>-band <img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/ldquo.gif\" alt=\"&#8220;\" align=\"TOP\" />dropout<img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/rdquo.gif\" alt=\"&#8221;\" align=\"TOP\" /> technique). The absorption to the blue of Ly<img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/agr.gif\" alt=\"&amp;agr;\" align=\"BOTTOM\" /> by the Ly<img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/agr.gif\" alt=\"&amp;agr;\" align=\"BOTTOM\" /> forest, and by source and intergalactic H <font size=\"-1\">I</font> beyond the Ly limit, are unique signatures of high-redshift objects (<a name=\"crf13\" href=\"#rf13\" id=\"crf13\">Madau 1995</a>; <a name=\"crf15\" href=\"#rf15\" id=\"crf15\">McMahon 1997</a>). Deep searches for Ly<img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/agr.gif\" alt=\"&amp;agr;\" align=\"BOTTOM\" /> emitters have been less successful but have recently resulted in detections at high redshift (e.g., <a name=\"crf6\" href=\"#rf6\" id=\"crf6\">Hu &amp; McMahon 1996</a>; <a name=\"crf2\" href=\"#rf2\" id=\"crf2\">Djorgovski et al. 1996</a>). Although most of the photometric searches have concentrated on galaxies at redshifts <i>z</i> = 3<img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/ndash.gif\" alt=\"&#8211;\" align=\"BOTTOM\" />4, similar searches can, in principle, be used to find even higher redshift galaxies.</p>\n<p>\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0Our serendipitous discovery of two galaxies at <i>z</i> = 4.92 reported here suggests that <i>z</i> <img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/ge.gif\" alt=\"&#8805;\" align=\"BOTTOM\" /> 5 galaxies may be found by similar photometric <img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/ldquo.gif\" alt=\"&#8220;\" align=\"TOP\" />dropout<img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/rdquo.gif\" alt=\"&#8221;\" align=\"TOP\" /> techniques. One of the galaxies is strongly gravitationally lensed and is detected as a very red arc in the <i>z</i> = 0.33 cluster CL 1358+62. This cluster was selected for detailed study of its member galaxies using <i>Hubble Space Telescope</i> (<i>HST</i>) imaging and extensive William Hershel Telescope (WHT) and Keck spectroscopy. An earlier search for arcs in this cluster was unsuccessful (<a name=\"crf10\" href=\"#rf10\" id=\"crf10\">Le F\u00e8vre et al. 1994</a>), so the presence of the arc in the <i>HST</i> image was unexpected. <i>HST</i> and Keck are able to exploit the magnification provided by the cluster lens to provide spectroscopic observations of a high-redshift galaxy at unprecedented spatial resolution.</p>\n<h2 align=\"CENTER\"><a name=\"sc2\" id=\"sc2\">\u00a72. OBSERVATIONS</a></h2>\n<p>\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0We obtained a large mosaic of multicolor WFPC2 images of the cluster for a detailed study of the cluster members. The integration time was 3600 s in each of the F606W (<img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/ldquo.gif\" alt=\"&#8220;\" align=\"TOP\" />R<img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/rdquo.gif\" alt=\"&#8221;\" align=\"TOP\" />) and F814W (<img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/ldquo.gif\" alt=\"&#8220;\" align=\"TOP\" />I<img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/rdquo.gif\" alt=\"&#8221;\" align=\"TOP\" />) filters. The data have been described in <a name=\"crf7\" href=\"#rf7\" id=\"crf7\">Kelson et al. (1997)</a> and <a name=\"crf22\" href=\"#rf22\" id=\"crf22\">van Dokkum et al. (1997)</a>. A color image of the central part of the cluster is shown in <a name=\"dfg1\" href=\"#fg1\" id=\"dfg1\">Figure 1</a> (Plate L6). Several arcs can be seen, of which the long red arc (B<img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/ndash.gif\" alt=\"&#8211;\" align=\"BOTTOM\" />C) is the brightest, lying 21<img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/arcsec.gif\" alt=\"&amp;arcsec;\" align=\"TOP\" /> \u00b1 1<img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/arcsec.gif\" alt=\"&amp;arcsec;\" align=\"TOP\" /> from the central cD galaxy. This arc is distorted by the bright elliptical galaxy north of component B. An additional faint lensed component D is located still further north. The arc is strikingly knotty, with both tangential and radial structure.</p>\n<a name=\"dfg1\" href=\"#fg1\" id=\"fg1\"><img  src=\"fg1t.gif\" align=\"BOTTOM\" /> Fig. 1</a>\n<p>\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0The symmetric distribution of the knots suggest that we are seeing a fold arc. We therefore searched for the expected counterimages. We identified a candidate counterimage 29<img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/farcs.gif\" alt=\"&amp;farcs;\" align=\"BOTTOM\" /> 5 east of the cD galaxy, marked A in <a href=\"#fg1\">Figure 1</a>. The colors of the arc and the counterimage are consistent.</p>\n<p>\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0Spectra of the arc and the counterimage were obtained at the W. M. Keck Telescope in 1996 May and June, and 1997 February. The best spectrum was taken with a long slit in February, using the Low Resolution Imaging Spectrograph (LRIS). For this LRIS spectrum, the slit was aligned with the arc (B<img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/ndash.gif\" alt=\"&#8211;\" align=\"BOTTOM\" />C), and a resolution of 4.3 \u00c5 FWHM was attained. The spectrum is shown in <a name=\"dfg2\" href=\"#fg2\" id=\"dfg2\">Figure 2</a> (Plate L7). The dominant features are the emission line at 7204 \u00c5 and the continuum drop to the blue side of this emission line. The continuum disappears further in the blue. The spectrum varies along the arc (e.g., the continuum is enhanced in the bright knot and the ratio of Ly<img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/agr.gif\" alt=\"&amp;agr;\" align=\"BOTTOM\" /> to continuum changes).</p>\n<a name=\"dfg2\" href=\"#fg2\" id=\"fg2\"><img  src=\"fg2t.gif\" align=\"BOTTOM\" /> Fig. 2</a>\n<p>\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0<a name=\"dfg3\" href=\"#fg3\" id=\"dfg3\">Figure 3</a> is a spatially averaged spectrum showing the emission line and continuum step at 7204 \u00c5 and absorption lines to the red. We mark the absorption-line features found by <a name=\"crf12\" href=\"#rf12\" id=\"crf12\">Lowenthal et al. (1997)</a> in an average of 12 high-redshift galaxies. Each absorption line can be identified in the arc spectrum. These spectral features provide a secure redshift for the source galaxy (<i>z</i> = 4.92) and allow us to identify the emission feature as Ly<img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/agr.gif\" alt=\"&amp;agr;\" align=\"BOTTOM\" />. The redshift of <i>z</i> = 4.92 is currently the highest spectroscopically measured for a galaxy or active nucleus. The narrow Ly<img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/agr.gif\" alt=\"&amp;agr;\" align=\"BOTTOM\" /> emission, the absence of other emission lines, and the broad spatial extent imply that the continuum is stellar.</p>\n<a name=\"dfg3\" href=\"#fg3\" id=\"fg3\"><img  src=\"fg3t.gif\" align=\"BOTTOM\" /> Fig. 3</a>\n<p>\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0The spectrum suggests that the continuum break across Ly<img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/agr.gif\" alt=\"&amp;agr;\" align=\"BOTTOM\" /> is about a factor of 1.9. This is an underestimate, as the break is very likely diluted by a weak contribution from the bright cluster galaxy. This galaxy contributes a flux in the <i>I</i> band similar to that of the arc. The broadband colors, and the spectrum of the bright knot at the end of the arc suggest that the true flux ratio across the break is a factor of 3 or more.</p>\n<p>\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0We also obtained spectra of the candidate counterimage in multislit mode. It was found to have an emission line at the same redshift as the arc (<a name=\"dfg4\" href=\"#fg4\" id=\"dfg4\">Fig. 4<i>b</i></a>), confirming its status as a counterimage of the arc. In what follows, we refer to the galaxy that is gravitationally lensed into these arcs as G1. To our surprise, we found an unexpected emission line from a faint, red, galaxy (<img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/ldquo.gif\" alt=\"&#8220;\" align=\"TOP\" />G2<img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/rdquo.gif\" alt=\"&#8221;\" align=\"TOP\" />) that lies 37<img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/arcsec.gif\" alt=\"&amp;arcsec;\" align=\"TOP\" /> west of the arc (<a href=\"#fg4\">Fig. 4<i>c</i></a>). It was observed by chance through a slitlet pointed at another galaxy. It has the same shape and width as the Ly<img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/agr.gif\" alt=\"&amp;agr;\" align=\"BOTTOM\" /> emission from the arc. Both Ly<img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/agr.gif\" alt=\"&amp;agr;\" align=\"BOTTOM\" /> lines have a tail to the red, and a steep cutoff in the blue. This signature has been found previously in two nearby starburst galaxies (<a name=\"crf11\" href=\"#rf11\" id=\"crf11\">Lequeux et al. 1995</a>; <a name=\"crf8\" href=\"#rf8\" id=\"crf8\">Kunth et al. 1996</a>). As these authors show, this asymmetry is caused by an outflow. Our results indicate that high-resolution spectra can be used to distinguish between Ly<img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/agr.gif\" alt=\"&amp;agr;\" align=\"BOTTOM\" /> emission and O [<font size=\"-1\">II</font>] 3727 \u00c5: at high resolution O [<font size=\"-1\">II</font>] is resolved into a symmetric doublet, without the asymmetry displayed by Ly<img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/agr.gif\" alt=\"&amp;agr;\" align=\"BOTTOM\" /> (<a href=\"#fg4\">Fig. 4</a>).</p>\n<a name=\"dfg4\" href=\"#fg4\" id=\"fg4\"><img  src=\"fg4t.gif\" align=\"BOTTOM\" /> Fig. 4</a>\n<p>\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0The wavelength peaks of the lines in object G2 and the arcs are very similar: we measure a wavelength of 7193 \u00c5 for G2, versus 7204 \u00c5 for the arcs. Object G2 has a magnitude of <i>I</i><sub>814,<i>AB</i></sub> = 25.1 and is also very red (see <a href=\"#fg1\">Fig. 1</a>). Thus, the emission is highly likely to be Ly<img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/agr.gif\" alt=\"&amp;agr;\" align=\"BOTTOM\" />, and G2 is at <i>z</i> = 4.92. After correction for lensing (see below), the projected distance between G2 and G1 is 200 <i>h</i><img  src=\"img1.gif\" align=\"MIDDLE\" alt=\"$\\mathstrut{^{-1}_{50}}$\" /> kpc, and the velocity difference is 450 km s<sup>-1</sup> (with an unknown error dominated by the outflow in both galaxies).</p>\n<h2 align=\"CENTER\"><a name=\"sc3\" id=\"sc3\">\u00a73. ANALYSIS</a></h2>\n<p>\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0The imaging and spectroscopy leave little doubt that we have found a multiply-imaged, distant galaxy. We have been able to reproduce the geometry of the arcs by modeling the cluster as an isothermal potential. An additional isothermal potential was added to model the effect of the elliptical galaxy north of the arc. The general form of the model is shown in <a name=\"dfg5\" href=\"#fg5\" id=\"dfg5\">Figure 5</a> (Plate L8). This model successfully reproduces the fold arc and the counterarc. Furthermore, the model produces only one counterimage, because the source lies outside the radial caustic. The Einstein radius of this model is 21<img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/arcsec.gif\" alt=\"&amp;arcsec;\" align=\"TOP\" />. For an isothermal model, this implies a velocity dispersion of 970 (990) km s<sup>-1</sup> for <i>q</i><sub>0</sub> = 0.5 (0.05), which is similar to the observed dispersion of 1080 km s<sup>-1</sup> for the galaxies (<a name=\"crf50\" href=\"#rf50\" id=\"crf50\">Fisher et al. 1997</a>).</p>\n<a name=\"dfg5\" href=\"#fg5\" id=\"fg5\"><img  src=\"fg5t.gif\" align=\"BOTTOM\" /> Fig. 5</a>\n<p>\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0We use this lens model to reconstruct the intensity distribution of the source galaxy, G1. The two parts of the fold arc, and the counterimage were reconstructed separately. The reconstructions are shown in <a href=\"#fg1\">Figure 1</a> with the color information and with higher contrast in <a href=\"#fg5\">Figure 5</a>. The resolution varies from one reconstruction to the next but is particularly high for the eastern part of the fold arc where the magnification is large (5<img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/ndash.gif\" alt=\"&#8211;\" align=\"BOTTOM\" />11\u00d7). The morphology is a strong function of the resolution. At the lowest resolution (with 0<img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/farcs.gif\" alt=\"&amp;farcs;\" align=\"BOTTOM\" /> 05 \u00d7 0<img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/farcs.gif\" alt=\"&amp;farcs;\" align=\"BOTTOM\" /> 1 pixels in the source plane), the galaxy appears to consist of a bright knot and a diffuse extended structure. These features contribute roughly equally to the total flux. At the highest resolution, half of the flux of the extended structure is resolved into small knots. The bright knot is very small. It is extended in a Planetary Camera (PC) image, but this is partly due to the PC point-spread function. After deconvolution and reconstruction, we find that the half-light radius of the knot is 22 mas or <img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/ap.gif\" alt=\"&amp;ap;\" align=\"BOTTOM\" />130 <i>h</i><img  src=\"img1.gif\" align=\"MIDDLE\" alt=\"$\\mathstrut{^{-1}_{50}}$\" /> pc (<i>q</i><sub>0</sub> = 0.5).</p>\n<p>\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0Nearby starburst galaxies show comparable irregular morphologies in the UV (<a name=\"crf16\" href=\"#rf16\" id=\"crf16\">Meurer et al. 1995</a>) with dense knots of star formation. <a href=\"#rf16\">Meurer et al. 1995</a> refer to these knots as <img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/ldquo.gif\" alt=\"&#8220;\" align=\"TOP\" />super<img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/ndash.gif\" alt=\"&#8211;\" align=\"BOTTOM\" />star clusters<img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/rdquo.gif\" alt=\"&#8221;\" align=\"TOP\" /> and suggest that they are possibly the progenitors of globular clusters. The dense knots can contribute between 20% to 50% of the UV flux. The bright knot in G1 is much more luminous than those in nearby starburst systems, but otherwise the similarity is striking.</p>\n<h3 align=\"CENTER\"><a name=\"sc3.1\" id=\"sc3.1\">\u00a73.1. <i>Star Formation at z = 5</i></a></h3>\n<p>\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0The lens model implies that the unlensed magnitudes of G1 and G2 are <i>I</i><sub>814,<i>AB</i></sub> <img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/ap.gif\" alt=\"&amp;ap;\" align=\"BOTTOM\" /> 24 and 25.4, respectively. The corresponding star formation rates (SFRs) are 36 and 9 <i>M</i><sub><img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/odot.gif\" alt=\"&amp;odot;\" align=\"BOTTOM\" /></sub> yr<sup>-1</sup> (<i>q</i><sub>0</sub> = 0.5). This is based on model predictions by <a name=\"crf1\" href=\"#rf1\" id=\"crf1\">Bruzual &amp; Charlot (1993)</a> for a stellar population with continuous star formation at an age of 10<sup>8</sup> yr and a Salpeter initial mass function. An instantaneous burst model requires a minimum mass of 3 \u00d7 10<sup>8</sup> <i>M</i><sub><img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/odot.gif\" alt=\"&amp;odot;\" align=\"BOTTOM\" /></sub>. If dust is present, the above SFRs would increase, possibly substantially (<a name=\"crf17\" href=\"#rf17\" id=\"crf17\">Meurer et al. 1997</a>). Even so, the inferred SFR is very high. Over a period of 10<sup>8</sup> yr, which is only 1<img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/solm0.gif\" alt=\"&amp;solm0;\" align=\"BOTTOM\" />6 of the Hubble time at <i>z</i> = 4.92, 20%<img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/ndash.gif\" alt=\"&#8211;\" align=\"BOTTOM\" />40% of the mass of the Galactic bulge can be formed.</p>\n<p>\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0Meurer et al. (<a href=\"#rf16\">1995</a>, <a href=\"#rf17\">1997</a>) suggest that there is a natural upper limit to the surface brightness of starbursts. We find that the mean surface brightness of G1 is low at 1.4 \u00d7 10<sup>10</sup> <i>L</i><sub><img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/odot.gif\" alt=\"&amp;odot;\" align=\"BOTTOM\" /></sub> kpc<sup>-2</sup>. However, the surface brightness of the knot in G1 is much higher, at 3 \u00d7 10<sup>12</sup> <i>L</i><sub><img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/odot.gif\" alt=\"&amp;odot;\" align=\"BOTTOM\" /></sub> kpc<sup>-2</sup>. This is significantly higher than the 90th percentile upper limit of 2 \u00d7 10<sup>11</sup> <i>L</i><sub><img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/odot.gif\" alt=\"&amp;odot;\" align=\"BOTTOM\" /></sub> kpc<sup>-2</sup> derived by <a href=\"#rf17\">Meurer et al. (1997)</a>, although it is close to the highest value in their sample. The bright knot in G1 may appear extreme because G1 is unique amongst high-redshift galaxies in being mapped with very high spatial resolution (from the magnification provided by the cluster lens). At the usual WFPC2 resolution, the knot in G1 is undersampled. This undersampling may pose a problem for size estimates based on WFPC2 observations of unlensed high-redshift galaxies.</p>\n<p>\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0The bright knot in G1 is too massive to evolve into a typical globular cluster. If the knot remains bound, it is more likely to end up in the nucleus of the galaxy, and it is possibly one of the progenitors of a bulge. A dynamical mass estimate of the knot would be valuable.</p>\n<h3 align=\"CENTER\"><a name=\"sc3.2\" id=\"sc3.2\">\u00a73.2. <i>Velocities and Spatial Structure in the Spectrum</i></a></h3>\n<p>\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0The arc is the first high-redshift galaxy for which it is possible to obtain spatially-resolved spectral information (<a name=\"dfg6\" href=\"#fg6\" id=\"dfg6\">Fig. 6</a>). The peak of the Ly<img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/agr.gif\" alt=\"&amp;agr;\" align=\"BOTTOM\" /> emission varies by about 7 \u00c5. This variation is real and is larger than expected from positional variations of the knots on the slit. The Si <font size=\"-1\">II</font> 1260 \u00c5 line is blueshifted with respect to the Ly<img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/agr.gif\" alt=\"&amp;agr;\" align=\"BOTTOM\" /> peak by 300 km s<sup>-1</sup>, and the velocity variation is on the order of 300 km s<sup>-1</sup>. The width of the Si <font size=\"-1\">II</font> line is approximately constant at 12 \u00c5 (or 2 \u00c5 in the rest frame), which is equivalent to a velocity dispersion of 200 km s<sup>-1</sup>. However, these velocities should <i>not</i> be interpreted as indicative of the gravitational potential of G1.</p>\n<a name=\"dfg6\" href=\"#fg6\" id=\"fg6\"><img  src=\"fg6t.gif\" align=\"BOTTOM\" /> Fig. 6</a>\n<p>\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0The blueshifted Si <font size=\"-1\">II</font> absorption and the asymmetric Ly<img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/agr.gif\" alt=\"&amp;agr;\" align=\"BOTTOM\" /> emission are naturally explained by an outflow model. Outflowing neutral and ionized material accounts for the Si <font size=\"-1\">II</font> absorption and absorbs the blue part of the Ly<img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/agr.gif\" alt=\"&amp;agr;\" align=\"BOTTOM\" /> emission line. The low continuum blueward of Ly<img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/agr.gif\" alt=\"&amp;agr;\" align=\"BOTTOM\" /> (<a href=\"#fg2\">Fig. 2</a>) is consistent with the presence of a significant, blueshifted H <font size=\"-1\">I</font> column. G1 is thus similar to Mrk 33, a nearby starburst galaxy. <a href=\"#rf11\">Lequeux et al. (1995)</a> observed the same blueshifted interstellar absorption lines and asymmetric Ly<img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/agr.gif\" alt=\"&amp;agr;\" align=\"BOTTOM\" /> profile in Mrk 33. They verified that the interstellar absorption lines were blueshifted with respect to the bulk of the galaxy. Another such case was presented by <a href=\"#rf8\">Kunth et al. (1996)</a>. Furthermore, <a name=\"crf5\" href=\"#rf5\" id=\"crf5\">Heckman &amp; Leitherer (1997)</a> found unambiguous evidence that UV absorption lines in NGC 1705 are produced by outflowing material. In general, outflows are present in most luminous starbursts at low redshifts (e.g., <a name=\"crf4\" href=\"#rf4\" id=\"crf4\">Heckman, Armus, &amp; Miley 1990</a>).</p>\n<p>\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0Outflows may also be the norm for high-redshift galaxies: G2 has the same asymmetric Ly<img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/agr.gif\" alt=\"&amp;agr;\" align=\"BOTTOM\" /> profile, and the Ly<img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/agr.gif\" alt=\"&amp;agr;\" align=\"BOTTOM\" /> emitter found by <a href=\"#rf2\">Djorgovski et al. (1996)</a> has a red tail. Furthermore, the average spectrum of 12 high-redshift galaxies obtained by <a href=\"#rf12\">Lowenthal et al. (1997)</a> shows a velocity offset between the Ly<img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/agr.gif\" alt=\"&amp;agr;\" align=\"BOTTOM\" /> emission, and the Si <font size=\"-1\">II</font> absorption. <a name=\"crf21\" href=\"#rf21\" id=\"crf21\">Trager et al. (1997)</a> found a similar blueshift for two galaxies at <i>z</i> = 4.</p>\n<p>\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0The Ly<img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/agr.gif\" alt=\"&amp;agr;\" align=\"BOTTOM\" /> emission may also be redshifted with respect to the systemic velocity because of absorption by the Ly<img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/agr.gif\" alt=\"&amp;agr;\" align=\"BOTTOM\" /> forest. This does not appear to be the dominant effect for this galaxy, as the high-resolution spectrum in <a href=\"#fg4\">Figure 4<i>a</i></a> shows a very strong drop in the blue. Furthermore, the blue edge of the line shows velocity variations across the slit. The case is less clear for other high-redshift galaxies, where the spectra are typically at lower resolution.</p>\n<p>\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0The apparent ubiquity of outflows in galaxies with substantial SFRs, casts doubt on the suggestion by <a href=\"#rf18\">Steidel et al. (1996b)</a> that regular rotation causes the broadening of the interstellar absorption lines in high-redshift galaxies. This is unfortunate since it would be very useful to find a method of measuring the mass of high-redshift galaxies. The large width (200 km s<sup>-1</sup>) of the Si <font size=\"-1\">II</font> line toward the bright knot in the galaxy is another argument against a rotational origin for the observed motions. It is hard to reconcile a systematic velocity variation of this magnitude across the knot with the small size of the knot. The velocity variations in the Ly<img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/agr.gif\" alt=\"&amp;agr;\" align=\"BOTTOM\" /> and Si <font size=\"-1\">II</font> across the arc are probably due to differences in the outflow velocity and absorption column density. The bottom line is that wavelength gradients in Ly<img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/agr.gif\" alt=\"&amp;agr;\" align=\"BOTTOM\" /> or the interstellar absorption lines cannot reliably be used to infer reliably the mass of such star-forming galaxies.</p>\n<h2 align=\"CENTER\"><a name=\"sc4\" id=\"sc4\">\u00a74. CONCLUSIONS</a></h2>\n<p>\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0We have discovered a red arc and a counterimage of a distant galaxy that is gravitationally lensed by the cluster CL 1358+62 at <i>z</i> = 0.33. The lensed galaxy and a second companion galaxy have secure redshifts of <i>z</i> = 4.92. The arc has an irregular structure, and image reconstructions show that most of the flux comes from very small knots. The morphology of the reconstructed galaxy is very similar to that of nearby starburst galaxies in the UV (<a href=\"#rf16\">Meurer et al. 1995</a>), which are also very irregular with dense star-forming knots. The surface brightness of the brightest knot in the lensed galaxy is higher than the 90th percentile upper limit derived by <a href=\"#rf17\">Meurer et al. (1997)</a> for nearby and distant starbursts. The mass and size of this bright knot suggest that it might be a <img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/ldquo.gif\" alt=\"&#8220;\" align=\"TOP\" />bulge-building<img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/rdquo.gif\" alt=\"&#8221;\" align=\"TOP\" /> subclump. It remains to be seen whether this lensed galaxy (G1) is typical of the population of starbursting galaxies at <i>z</i> <img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/sim.gif\" alt=\"&#8764;\" align=\"BOTTOM\" /> 5.</p>\n<p>\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0The irregular structure of G1 can be contrasted with the <i>z</i> = 3 galaxies studied by <a name=\"crf3\" href=\"#rf3\" id=\"crf3\">Giavalisco et al. (1996)</a>. These authors found that many of those galaxies are compact with little substructure. The high-redshift galaxies in the Hubble Deep Field are more similar to our reconstructed images of G1 (e.g., <a href=\"#rf12\">Lowenthal et al. 1997</a>; <a href=\"#rf19\">Steidel et al. 1996a</a>). However, the morphology of G1 is a strong function of resolution: at the lowest resolution, half of the flux appears to originate from a smooth, extended component. Half the flux of this component is resolved into knots at the highest resolution. Other high-redshift galaxies might display similar irregular morphologies if they were to be imaged at such high resolution.</p>\n<p>\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0Our results, and the data presented by others, indicate that radial outflows dominate the kinematics of the absorption-line gas in high-redshift galaxies. These radial outflows are a general feature of luminous starburst galaxies in the nearby universe. Radial outflows of hundreds of kilometers per second can quickly disperse the reservoir of gas from which the stars formed. Thus, the rest-UV appearance of these galaxies is likely to be dominated by short-lived areas of intense star formation. IR observations are needed to characterize the overall morphology, since the UV structure will not be representative of the distribution of older stars in these galaxies.</p>\n<p>\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0Our serendipitous discovery of the two galaxies at <i>z</i> = 4.92 raises the question of the surface density of such objects. Their unlensed <i>I</i><sub>814,<i>AB</i></sub> magnitudes are estimated to be 24 and 25.4. The results by <a name=\"crf9\" href=\"#rf9\" id=\"crf9\">Lanzetta, Yahil, &amp; Fern\u00e1ndez-Soto (1996)</a> suggest an upper limit of <i>z</i> &gt; 4 galaxies of 0.4 arcmin<sup>-2</sup> for <i>I</i><sub>814,<i>AB</i></sub> &lt; 26. <a name=\"crf14\" href=\"#rf14\" id=\"crf14\">Madau et al. (1996)</a> found <i>B</i> dropout candidates in the Hubble Deep Field with <i>I</i><sub>814,<i>AB</i></sub> magnitudes of 26 or higher. The <i>I</i><sub>606,<i>AB</i></sub> - <i>I</i><sub>814,<i>AB</i></sub> color of the arc is in good agreement with the models of high-redshift starbursts (<a href=\"#rf14\">Madau et al. 1996</a>), which indicates that more candidates might be found by shifting the dropout techniques further into the red. Finding such objects with emission-line searches may be more difficult. While the unlensed Ly<img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/agr.gif\" alt=\"&amp;agr;\" align=\"BOTTOM\" /> flux of the arc is comparable to the fluxes for the Ly<img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/agr.gif\" alt=\"&amp;agr;\" align=\"BOTTOM\" /> selected galaxies found by <a href=\"#rf6\">Hu &amp; McMahon (1996)</a>, it is comparable to the rms noise in the deep field searches for Ly<img  src=\"/ApJ/icons/entities/agr.gif\" alt=\"&amp;agr;\" align=\"BOTTOM\" /> (e.g., <a name=\"crf20\" href=\"#rf20\" id=\"crf20\">Thompson, Djorgovski, &amp; Trauger 1995</a>).</p>\n<h2 align=\"CENTER\"><a name=\"ack\" id=\"ack\">ACKNOWLEDGMENTS</a></h2>\n<p>\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0We thank Dan Fabricant and Tim Heckman for their comments on the manuscript.
