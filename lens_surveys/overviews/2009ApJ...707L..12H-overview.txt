Images taken by the <italic>Hubble Space Telescope</italic> using the Advanced Camera for Surveys reveal a complex lensing system that produces a prominent, highly magnified arc and a triplet of smaller arcs grouped tightly around a spectroscopically confirmed cluster member. Spectroscopic observations using the Faint Object Camera and Spectrograph on <italic>Subaru</italic> confirm strong Ly&alpha; emission in the source galaxy and provide the redshifts for more than 21 cluster members with a velocity dispersion of 807 &plusmn; 185&thinsp;km&thinsp;s<sup>&minus;1</sup>. Assuming a singular isothermal sphere profile, the mass within the Einstein ring (7.13 &plusmn; &thinsp;0&farcs;38) corresponds to a central velocity dispersion of 686<sup>&plus;15</sup><sub>&minus;19</sub>&thinsp;km&thinsp;s<sup>&minus;1</sup> for the cluster, consistent with the value estimated from cluster member redshifts. Our mass profile estimate from combining strong lensing and dynamical analyses is in good agreement with both X-ray and weak lensing results.</p></abstract>\n<kwd-group kwd-group-type=\"AAS\">\n<kwd>galaxies: clusters: general</kwd>\n<kwd>galaxies: clusters: individual (WARPS J1415.1+36)</kwd>\n<kwd>gravitational lensing</kwd>\n</kwd-group>\n<custom-meta-wrap>\n<custom-meta><meta-name>ccc</meta-name><meta-value>0004-637X/09/010012+05&#x0024;30.00</meta-value></custom-meta>\n<custom-meta><meta-name>printed</meta-name><meta-value>Printed in the U.S.A.</meta-value></custom-meta>\n</custom-meta-wrap>\n</article-meta>\n</front>\n<body>\n<sec id=\"apjl327453s1\">\n<label>1.</label><title>INTRODUCTION</title>\n<p>Surveys like SpARCS (Wilson et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjl327453r38\">2008</xref>), SPT (Ruhl et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjl327453r33\">2004</xref>), the Red-Sequence Cluster Surveys (RCS, Gladders <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjl327453r16\">2004</xref> and RCS-2, Gladders &amp; Yee <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjl327453r18\">2005</xref>; Yee et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjl327453r39\">2007</xref>), and the IRAC Shallow Survey (Eisenhardt et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjl327453r12\">2008</xref>) will discover thousands of galaxy clusters out to redshifts beyond <italic>z</italic> &equals; 1. These large surveys will be used to probe dark energy and the underlying cosmology through direct measurements of the evolution of the cluster mass function. To fully utilize these data, it will be essential to derive reliable mass estimates from the cluster properties measured in these surveys.</p>\n<p>Great effort has been dedicated to the development of cluster mass-observable relations in targeted cluster observations. Sunyaev&ndash;Zel&apos;dovich (SZ) and X-ray observations probe the hot ionized intracluster gas and have been used to derive masses (LaRoque et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjl327453r27\">2006</xref>; Allen et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjl327453r1\">2008</xref>) and scaling relations that tie mass to the observed SZ quantities (Bonamente et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjl327453r6\">2008</xref>). Hicks et al. (<xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjl327453r21\">2006</xref>) derive scaling relations between observed X-ray properties and the optical richness of clusters while Evrard et al. (<xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjl327453r13\">2008</xref>) model the relationship between the velocity dispersion of cluster galaxies and cluster mass. Weak lensing measurements have been used to calibrate optical richness measurements from a large sample of clusters from the Sloan Digital Sky Survey (Koester et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjl327453r25\">2007</xref>; Johnston et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjl327453r23\">2007</xref>) and to calibrate X-ray measurements through the mass&ndash;temperature relations (Hoekstra <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjl327453r22\">2007</xref>; Bardeau et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjl327453r2\">2007</xref>; Berg&eacute; et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjl327453r4\">2008</xref>). Strong lensing measurements typically have much smaller errors compared with the other methods but are restricted to the central region of clusters and are rare. Nevertheless, strong lensing systems have been studied in small samples of clusters (Comerford et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjl327453r9\">2006</xref>; Hennawi et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjl327453r20\">2008</xref>).</p>\n<p>Large cluster surveys out to <italic>z</italic>&sim; 1 have the potential to constrain the dark energy equation of state (e.g., Voit <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjl327453r37\">2005</xref>), provided that the mass-observable relations are well calibrated. Full exploitation of the more distant clusters requires deep space-based observations. As a result, mass-observable relations have been studied in far fewer clusters at <italic>z</italic> &gt; 1 relative to low-redshift clusters. In a 219-orbit program (Program number 10496, PI: Perlmutter) to search for supernovae (SNe) with the <italic>Hubble Space Telescope</italic> (<italic>HST</italic>), we observed 25 of the highest redshift galaxy clusters known at the time (Dawson et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjl327453r10\">2009</xref>). These images support a rich program of cluster studies including the calibration of mass proxies at <italic>z</italic> &gt; 1. One particular cluster from this program, selected from the Wide Angle <italic>ROSAT</italic> Pointed Survey, WARPS J1415.1+3612 at <italic>z</italic> &equals; 1.026 (Perlman et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjl327453r32\">2002</xref>), has already been studied in X-ray (Maughan et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjl327453r28\">2006</xref>; Allen et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjl327453r1\">2008</xref>) and SZ (Muchovej et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjl327453r29\">2007</xref>) observations. We now have deep images from the Advanced Camera for Surveys (ACS) and spectroscopy from the Faint Object Camera and Spectrograph (FOCAS: Kashikawa et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjl327453r24\">2002</xref>) on <italic>Subaru</italic> reveals a pronounced strong lensing arc of a source Ly&alpha; emitting galaxy at <italic>z</italic> &equals; 3.90. The new data from this program combined with previous and ongoing measurements enable a multi-probe analysis of the cluster mass-observable relation for this high-redshift cluster.</p>\n<p>Here, we present an analysis of the strong lensing and dynamical mass of WARPS J1415.1+3612. The Letter is organized as follows: we describe the observations and data in Section <xref ref-type=\"sec\" rid=\"apjl327453s2\">2</xref>, mass estimates are derived and compared in Section <xref ref-type=\"sec\" rid=\"apjl327453s3\">3</xref>, and the summary is found in Section <xref ref-type=\"sec\" rid=\"apjl327453s4\">4</xref>. Throughout this Letter, we use AB magnitudes and assume a cosmology with &Omega;<sub><italic>M</italic></sub> &equals; 0.3, &Omega;<sub>&Lambda;</sub> &equals; 0.7, and <italic>h</italic> &equals; 0.7.</p>\n</sec>\n<sec id=\"apjl327453s2\">\n<label>2.</label><title>OBSERVATIONS</title>\n<sec id=\"apjl327453s2-1\">\n<label>2.1.</label><title>ACS Images</title>\n<p>WARPS J1415.1+3612 was observed seven times with ACS from 2005 November through 2006 April for a total integration of 2425 s in the F775W filter and 9920 s in the F850LP filter, hereafter <italic>i</italic><sub>775</sub> and <italic>z</italic><sub>850</sub>. Object G in Figure <xref ref-type=\"fig\" rid=\"apjl327453f1\">1</xref> has the same color as arcs A&ndash;E and lies near the Einstein ring but spectroscopic data are inconclusive as to whether it has the same redshift as the confirmed arcs.</p>\n</sec>\n</sec>\n<sec id=\"apjl327453s3\">\n<label>3.</label><title>DISCUSSION</title>\n<p>The total mass inside the Einstein ring of <italic>r<sub>E</sub></italic> &equals; 57.5 &plusmn; 3.1 kpc is 1.96<sup>&plus;0.22</sup><sub>&minus;0.20</sub> &times; 10<sup>13</sup>&thinsp;<italic>M</italic><sub>&sun;</sub> (e.g., Narayan &amp; Bartelmann <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjl327453r30\">1996</xref>). If we assume a singular isothermal sphere (SIS) profile for the central region of the cluster, this mass corresponds to a rest-frame velocity dispersion of 686<sup>&plus;15</sup><sub>&minus;19</sub>&thinsp;km&thinsp;s<sup>&minus;1</sup>, consistent with the value estimated from cluster member redshifts. We can estimate the magnification factor for arc A by using &mu; &equals; &verbar;1&sol;(1 &minus; <italic>r<sub>E</sub></italic>&sol;<italic>r</italic>)&verbar; (e.g., Narayan &amp; Bartelmann <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjl327453r30\">1996</xref>) for the SIS profile, where <italic>r</italic> is the distance of the image from the lens center. If we take <italic>r<sub>E</sub></italic> &equals; 7&farcs;13 (or 57.5 kpc), &mu; &sim; 18 for arc A. This implies that the unmagnified <italic>i</italic><sub>775</sub> magnitude for arc A is &sim; 26.6.</p>\n<p>To get a qualitative estimate on the change in the derived strong lensing mass caused by relaxing our assumption of a spherically symmetric mass distribution, we construct elliptical Navarro&ndash;Frenk&ndash;White profile (NFW) models (Glose &amp; Kneib <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjl327453r19\">2002</xref>) with a critical curve that passes through at least part of arcs A and E and passes between arc C and the lensing galaxy (cluster member no. 13). The model with the largest ellipticity that still meets these requirements has an ellipticity of 0.1. The mass enclosed within its critical curve is 1.79 &times; 10<sup>13</sup>&thinsp;<italic>M</italic><sub>&sun;</sub>, within the errors of the spherical models.</p>\n<p>The dynamical mass is estimated from the redshifts of the 21 cluster members with <italic>Q</italic> &gt; 1 in Table <xref ref-type=\"table\" rid=\"apjl327453t1\">1</xref> using the virial scaling relation from the simulations of Evrard et al. The constants &sigma;<sub>DM,15</sub> &equals; 1082.9 &plusmn; 4.0&thinsp;km&thinsp;s<sup>&minus;1</sup> and &alpha; &equals; 0.3361 &plusmn; 0.0026 are determined from the simulations of Evrard et al. (<xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjl327453r13\">2008</xref>). Assuming that the velocity bias <italic>b<sub>v</sub></italic> &equals; &sigma;<sub>v,gal</sub>&sol;&sigma;<sub>DM</sub> &equals; 1, we find <italic>M</italic><sub>200</sub> &equals; 3.33<sup>&plus;2.83</sup><sub>&minus;1.80</sub> &times; 10<sup>14</sup>&thinsp;<italic>M</italic><sub>&sun;</sub> and <italic>r</italic><sub>200</sub> &equals; 0.97 &plusmn; 0.22 Mpc. Thus, the mass to light ratio is &sim;114&thinsp;<italic>M</italic><sub>&sun;</sub>&sol;<italic>L</italic><sub><italic>B</italic>&odot;</sub>. In Figure <xref ref-type=\"fig\" rid=\"apjl327453f2\">2</xref>, we fit an NFW profile (Navarro et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjl327453r31\">1997</xref>) to the total cluster mass using the strong lensing and dynamical mass estimates. The model has a concentration parameter <italic>c</italic> &equals; 4.96<sup>&plus;1.81</sup><sub>&minus;1.03</sub> and a core radius <italic>r<sub>s</sub></italic> &equals; 0.179<sup>&plus;0.078</sup><sub>&minus;0.055</sub> Mpc, where uncertainties are computed via Monte Carlo simulation.</p>\n<fig id=\"apjl327453f2\" position=\"float\">\n<label>Figure 2.</label>\n<caption><p>Comparison of different mass estimates for WARPS J1415+36. Using the same deep ACS images, M. J. Jee et al. (2009, in preparation) derive the weak lensing mass assuming an NFW profile with a mass&ndash;concentration relation from Bullock et al. (<xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjl327453r8\">2001</xref>). The X-ray mass <italic>M</italic><sub>2500(<italic>z</italic>)</sub> (Maughan et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjl327453r28\">2006</xref>) is determined from a best-fit two-dimensional elliptical &beta;-model analyzed at <italic>r</italic><sub>2500(<italic>z</italic>)</sub>. The SZ value of <italic>M</italic><sub>2500(<italic>z</italic>)</sub> is derived from a spherical, isothermal &beta;-model fit to 30 GHz data from the SZ Array (Muchovej et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjl327453r29\">2007</xref>). In the X-ray and the SZ mass estimate, &Delta;(<italic>z</italic>) is a redshift-dependent density contrast as described in, e.g., Maughan et al. (<xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjl327453r28\">2006</xref>). Both the weak lensing mass and the X-ray mass are consistent with our results. However, the SZ mass is lower than the prediction of our model by roughly a factor of 2. In Muchovej et al. (<xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjl327453r29\">2007</xref>), it is noted that the SZ mass estimate is marginally lower than the X-ray value, likely due to low signal-to-noise ratio (S&sol;N) in SZ measurements, uncertainties in the absolute calibration, and possible systematic errors from the model assumptions. Additional observations with the SZA at 30 GHz and 90 GHz should improve the constraints on the SZ mass estimate and shed light on the apparent discrepancy.</p>\n<p>Using numerical simulations, Dolag et al. (<xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjl327453r11\">2004</xref>) predict the dependence of the average halo concentration parameter on the cluster redshift and virial mass\n<disp-formula id=\"apjl327453fd2\"><label>2</label><tex-math><?CDATA\n\\begin{equation}\nc(M, z) = {c_{0}\\over {1+z}} \\left({M\\over {M_{*}}}\\right)^{\\gamma }.\n\\end{equation}\n?></tex-math>\n<graphic xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" xlink:href=\"apjl327453fd2.gif\"/></disp-formula>Shaw et al. (<xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjl327453r34\">2006</xref>) find the best-fit parameters to be <italic>c</italic><sub>0</sub> &equals; 6.47 &plusmn; 0.03 and &gamma; &equals; &minus;0.12 &plusmn; 0.03 from <italic>N</italic>-body simulations. Here, <italic>M</italic> is the virial mass and <italic>M</italic><sub>&ast;</sub> &equals; 11.0 &times; 10<sup>14</sup>&thinsp;<italic>M</italic><sub>&sun;</sub>(Shaw et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjl327453r34\">2006</xref>). If we assume the dynamical mass (<italic>M</italic><sub>200</sub> &equals; 3.33 &times; 10<sup>14</sup>&thinsp;<italic>M</italic><sub>&sun;</sub>) to be the virial mass, Equation (<xref ref-type=\"disp-formula\" rid=\"apjl327453fd2\">2</xref>) predicts the average concentration to be <italic>c</italic> &equals; 3.70<sup>&plus;0.62&plus;1.9</sup><sub>&minus;0.34 &minus; 1.3</sub>, where the first set of errors is from the errors in the best-fit parameters (<italic>c</italic><sub>0</sub> and &gamma;) and the second set is from the scatter about the average (Bullock et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjl327453r8\">2001</xref>). Our concentration of <italic>c</italic> = 4.96 is higher than the predicted value of <italic>c</italic> but is well within its errors.</p>\n<p>We now examine the strong lensing arcs B, C, and D around cluster member no. 13. The radius of the Einstein ring for the lensing galaxy is estimated by the distance between the galaxy and arc C, 0&farcs;65<sup>&plus;0&farcs;35</sup><sub>&minus;0&farcs;30</sub>. The upper and lower bounds are set by the distances from the lensing galaxy to arcs D and B, respectively. Assuming that the deflection angle due to the cluster potential does not vary appreciably for arcs B, C, and D, the mass enclosed within the Einstein radius is estimated to be 1.65<sup>&plus;2.25</sup><sub>&minus;1.17</sub> &times; 10<sup>11</sup> <italic>M</italic><sub>&sun;</sub>.</p>\n</sec>\n<sec id=\"apjl327453s4\">\n<label>4.</label><title>SUMMARY</title>\n<p>The cluster WARPS J1415+36 at <italic>z</italic><sub>lens</sub> &equals; 1.026 is one of the most distant strong lensing clusters reported (Thompson et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjl327453r36\">2001</xref>; Gladders et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjl327453r17\">2003</xref>; Gilbank et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjl327453r15\">2008</xref>). Although the SZ mass estimate is not consistent with the rest of the data for this cluster, this work suggests that the mass-observable relations derived from numerical simulations and observations of strong lensing, weak lensing, dynamical mass, and X-ray mass still hold for clusters at redshifts at <italic>z</italic> &sim; 1.</p>\n<p>Recently Gilbank et al. (<xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjl327453r15\">2008</xref>) report multi-probe mass estimates for the strong lensing cluster, RCS2319, at <italic>z</italic> &equals; 0.91. Future surveys will soon reveal many new high-redshift, strong lensing systems and provide deep, multiwavelength images of these clusters. With the corresponding improvements in mass-observable relations in the decelerating regime, galaxy clusters will soon become an even more effective tool for cosmology.</p>\n</sec>\n</body>\n<back>\n<ack>\n<p>Financial support for this work was provided by NASA through program GO-10496 from the Space Telescope Science Institute, which is operated by AURA, Inc., under NASA contract NAS 5-26555. Images taken by the <italic>Hubble Space Telescope</italic> using the Advanced Camera for Surveys reveal a complex lensing system that produces a prominent, highly magnified arc and a triplet of smaller arcs grouped tightly around a spectroscopically confirmed cluster member. Spectroscopic observations using the Faint Object Camera and Spectrograph on <italic>Subaru</italic> confirm strong Ly&alpha; emission in the source galaxy and provide the redshifts for more than 21 cluster members with a velocity dispersion of 807 &plusmn; 185&thinsp;km&thinsp;s<sup>&minus;1</sup>. Assuming a singular isothermal sphere profile, the mass within the Einstein ring (7.13 &plusmn; &thinsp;0&farcs;38) corresponds to a central velocity dispersion of 686<sup>&plus;15</sup><sub>&minus;19</sub>&thinsp;km&thinsp;s<sup>&minus;1</sup> for the cluster, consistent with the value estimated from cluster member redshifts. Our mass profile estimate from combining strong lensing and dynamical analyses is in good agreement with both X-ray and weak lensing results.</p></abstract>\n<kwd-group kwd-group-type=\"AAS\">\n<kwd>galaxies: clusters: general</kwd>\n<kwd>galaxies: clusters: individual (WARPS J1415.1+36)</kwd>\n<kwd>gravitational lensing</kwd>\n</kwd-group>\n<custom-meta-wrap>\n<custom-meta><meta-name>ccc</meta-name><meta-value>0004-637X/09/010012+05&#x0024;30.00</meta-value></custom-meta>\n<custom-meta><meta-name>printed</meta-name><meta-value>Printed in the U.S.A.</meta-value></custom-meta>\n</custom-meta-wrap>\n</article-meta>\n</front>\n<body>\n<sec id=\"apjl327453s1\">\n<label>1.</label><title>INTRODUCTION</title>\n<p>Surveys like SpARCS (Wilson et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjl327453r38\">2008</xref>), SPT (Ruhl et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjl327453r33\">2004</xref>), the Red-Sequence Cluster Surveys (RCS, Gladders <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjl327453r16\">2004</xref> and RCS-2, Gladders &amp; Yee <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjl327453r18\">2005</xref>; Yee et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjl327453r39\">2007</xref>), and the IRAC Shallow Survey (Eisenhardt et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjl327453r12\">2008</xref>) will discover thousands of galaxy clusters out to redshifts beyond <italic>z</italic> &equals; 1. These large surveys will be used to probe dark energy and the underlying cosmology through direct measurements of the evolution of the cluster mass function. To fully utilize these data, it will be essential to derive reliable mass estimates from the cluster properties measured in these surveys.</p>\n<p>Great effort has been dedicated to the development of cluster mass-observable relations in targeted cluster observations. Sunyaev&ndash;Zel&apos;dovich (SZ) and X-ray observations probe the hot ionized intracluster gas and have been used to derive masses (LaRoque et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjl327453r27\">2006</xref>; Allen et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjl327453r1\">2008</xref>) and scaling relations that tie mass to the observed SZ quantities (Bonamente et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjl327453r6\">2008</xref>). Hicks et al. (<xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjl327453r21\">2006</xref>) derive scaling relations between observed X-ray properties and the optical richness of clusters while Evrard et al. (<xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjl327453r13\">2008</xref>) model the relationship between the velocity dispersion of cluster galaxies and cluster mass. Weak lensing measurements have been used to calibrate optical richness measurements from a large sample of clusters from the Sloan Digital Sky Survey (Koester et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjl327453r25\">2007</xref>; Johnston et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjl327453r23\">2007</xref>) and to calibrate X-ray measurements through the mass&ndash;temperature relations (Hoekstra <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjl327453r22\">2007</xref>; Bardeau et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjl327453r2\">2007</xref>; Berg&eacute; et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjl327453r4\">2008</xref>). Strong lensing measurements typically have much smaller errors compared with the other methods but are restricted to the central region of clusters and are rare. Nevertheless, strong lensing systems have been studied in small samples of clusters (Comerford et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjl327453r9\">2006</xref>; Hennawi et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjl327453r20\">2008</xref>).</p>\n<p>Large cluster surveys out to <italic>z</italic>&sim; 1 have the potential to constrain the dark energy equation of state (e.g., Voit <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjl327453r37\">2005</xref>), provided that the mass-observable relations are well calibrated. Full exploitation of the more distant clusters requires deep space-based observations. As a result, mass-observable relations have been studied in far fewer clusters at <italic>z</italic> &gt; 1 relative to low-redshift clusters. In a 219-orbit program (Program number 10496, PI: Perlmutter) to search for supernovae (SNe) with the <italic>Hubble Space Telescope</italic> (<italic>HST</italic>), we observed 25 of the highest redshift galaxy clusters known at the time (Dawson et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjl327453r10\">2009</xref>). These images support a rich program of cluster studies including the calibration of mass proxies at <italic>z</italic> &gt; 1. One particular cluster from this program, selected from the Wide Angle <italic>ROSAT</italic> Pointed Survey, WARPS J1415.1+3612 at <italic>z</italic> &equals; 1.026 (Perlman et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjl327453r32\">2002</xref>), has already been studied in X-ray (Maughan et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjl327453r28\">2006</xref>; Allen et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjl327453r1\">2008</xref>) and SZ (Muchovej et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjl327453r29\">2007</xref>) observations. We now have deep images from the Advanced Camera for Surveys (ACS) and spectroscopy from the Faint Object Camera and Spectrograph (FOCAS: Kashikawa et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjl327453r24\">2002</xref>) on <italic>Subaru</italic> reveals a pronounced strong lensing arc of a source Ly&alpha; emitting galaxy at <italic>z</italic> &equals; 3.90. The new data from this program combined with previous and ongoing measurements enable a multi-probe analysis of the cluster mass-observable relation for this high-redshift cluster.</p>\n<p>Here, we present an analysis of the strong lensing and dynamical mass of WARPS J1415.1+3612. The Letter is organized as follows: we describe the observations and data in Section <xref ref-type=\"sec\" rid=\"apjl327453s2\">2</xref>, mass estimates are derived and compared in Section <xref ref-type=\"sec\" rid=\"apjl327453s3\">3</xref>, and the summary is found in Section <xref ref-type=\"sec\" rid=\"apjl327453s4\">4</xref>. Throughout this Letter, we use AB magnitudes and assume a cosmology with &Omega;<sub><italic>M</italic></sub> &equals; 0.3, &Omega;<sub>&Lambda;</sub> &equals; 0.7, and <italic>h</italic> &equals; 0.7.</p>\n</sec>\n<sec id=\"apjl327453s2\">\n<label>2.</label><title>OBSERVATIONS</title>\n<sec id=\"apjl327453s2-1\">\n<label>2.1.</label><title>ACS Images</title>\n<p>WARPS J1415.1+3612 was observed seven times with ACS from 2005 November through 2006 April for a total integration of 2425 s in the F775W filter and 9920 s in the F850LP filter, hereafter <italic>i</italic><sub>775</sub> and <italic>z</italic><sub>850</sub>. Individual exposures were co-added using MultiDrizzle (Fruchter &amp; Hook <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjl327453r14\">2002</xref>) at a resolution of 0&farcs;05 pixel<sup>&minus;1</sup>. We use 25.678 and 24.867 as zero points for <italic>i</italic><sub>775</sub> and <italic>z</italic><sub>850</sub>, respectively (Sirianni et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjl327453r35\">2005</xref>).</p>\n<p>The deep ACS images revealed a complex strong lensing system near the cluster core. The composite color image from the <italic>i</italic><sub>775</sub> and <italic>z</italic><sub>850</sub> data is shown in Figure <xref ref-type=\"fig\" rid=\"apjl327453f1\">1</xref>. The arc system consists of at least five images. The main arc (A) is 6&farcs;75 (54.4 kpc at the cluster redshift) from the center of the cluster, which we take to be the position of the brightest cluster galaxy (BCG). To the SW of the main arc, there is a triplet of arcs (B, C, and D) around a spectroscopically confirmed cluster member (no. 13). About 5&Prime; south of the triplet, there is a fifth arc (E). The total <italic>i</italic><sub>775</sub> isophotal magnitudes for the arcs A&ndash;E are 23.44, 25.75, 25.46, 25.13, and 25.62, respectively. We use the <italic>i</italic><sub>775</sub> magnitudes because they suffer less contamination than the <italic>z</italic><sub>850</sub> magnitudes from the much redder elliptical cluster members. The magnitudes for arcs B, C, and D are obtained after the lensing galaxy has been subtracted using the b-spline model of Bolton et al. (<xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjl327453r5\">2006</xref>). These five images lie very close to an imaginary arc centered on the BCG that subtends slightly more than 90&deg;. The radius of the Einstein ring for the cluster potential is taken to be the average of the two circles mentioned in Figure <xref ref-type=\"fig\" rid=\"apjl327453f1\">1</xref>, 7.13 &plusmn; 0&farcs;38. The uncertainty in the radius is determined from the difference between the two circles and the average.</p>\n<fig id=\"apjl327453f1\" position=\"float\">\n<label>Figure 1.</label>\n<caption><p>Top: central region of WARPS J1415.1+3612. We apply a <italic>K</italic>-correction to transform <italic>z</italic><sub>850</sub> to rest-frame <italic>B</italic> magnitude. The total luminosity is determined to be <italic>L<sub>B</sub></italic> &equals; 2.92 &plusmn; 0.88 &times; 10<sup>12</sup>&thinsp;<italic>L</italic><sub><italic>B</italic>&odot;</sub>.</p>\n</sec>\n<sec id=\"apjl327453s2-2\">\n<label>2.2.</label><title><italic>Subaru</italic> Spectroscopy</title>\n<p>Spectroscopic observations were made with the FOCAS spectrograph on the <italic>Subaru</italic> telescope. SN candidates provided the primary targets for spectroscopic observations. The strong lensing arcs described in Section <xref ref-type=\"sec\" rid=\"apjl327453s2-1\">2.1</xref> and galaxies with <italic>i</italic><sub>775</sub>&ndash;<italic>z</italic><sub>850</sub> color consistent with early-type galaxies at the cluster redshift provided secondary targets for multi-slit spectroscopy. We obtained spectra for a total of 138 objects and measured redshifts for 95 of them. Six masks were used, four with the 300R&sol;SO58 grism and filter and two with the 300B&sol;SY47 grism and filter. The total integration time is approximately 2 hr for each mask. Arcs B, C, and D were observed with the former setup, resulting in spectra that start at 5800 &Aring;, while arc E was observed with the latter, resulting in spectra that cover a bluer wavelength range. Arc A was observed with both setups. The slit width is 0&farcs;8, corresponding to a spectral resolution of <italic>R</italic> &sim; 500. Further details can be found in Morokuma et al. (<xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjl327453r29a\">2009</xref>).</p>\n<p>Redshifts for cluster members were obtained by fitting a series of galaxy spectral eigenfunctions to the spectra weighted by the error spectra. For the majority of the spectra, the 3933 &Aring; and 3968 &Aring; Ca&thinsp;<sc>ii</sc> H and K absorption lines were the dominant feature detected, along with the 4000 &Aring; break and the [O&thinsp;<sc>ii</sc>] emission line. Each redshift was assigned a quality flag <italic>Q</italic>, with <italic>Q</italic> &equals; 3 for those with multiple strong features, <italic>Q</italic> &equals; 2 for those with a single strong feature, and <italic>Q</italic> &equals; 1 for those with only weakly detected features. The redshift of the cluster is determined to be 1.026 from the average of the cluster member redshifts listed in the table. We calculate the cluster velocity dispersion from the 21 galaxies with <italic>Q</italic> &gt; 1. Using the biweight scale estimator of Beers et al. (<xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjl327453r3\">1990</xref>), we estimate &sigma;<sub>v</sub> &equals; 807 &plusmn; 185&thinsp;km&thinsp;s<sup>&minus;1</sup> in the cluster rest frame. (Figure <xref ref-type=\"fig\" rid=\"apjl327453f1\">1</xref>). The consistency in the observed emission wavelengths, surface brightness, color, and morphology of these five objects, and their arrangement around a circle centered on the BCG all strongly suggest that this is a multiply imaged system at <italic>z</italic> &gt; 1.026. In this case, the line is almost certainly Ly&alpha; at rest frame 1215.67 &Aring;. The detection of discontinuity in flux for wavelengths bluer and redder than the emission feature in the spectra for arcs A and E lends support to the identification of this line with Ly&alpha;. Assuming this emission is due to Ly&alpha;, the redshifts of each arc are reported in Table <xref ref-type=\"table\" rid=\"apjl327453t1\">1</xref>. As indicated by the apparent discrepancy in redshifts, there is &sim;9 &Aring; (or 453&thinsp;km&thinsp;s<sup>&minus;1</sup> in the rest frame of the cluster, Figure <xref ref-type=\"fig\" rid=\"apjl327453f1\">1</xref>) difference between the observed wavelengths of Ly&alpha; in arc A and in the triplet (arcs B, C, and D). It is three times the statistical error, which takes into account the spectral resolution. This difference is likely due to the placement of the arcs B, C, and D near the edges of the slit and contamination from the lensing galaxy (cluster member no. 13).</p>\n<p>Preliminary spectroscopic results show that object F in Figure <xref ref-type=\"fig\" rid=\"apjl327453f1\">1</xref> is a possible counter arc of the same source galaxy. Object G in Figure <xref ref-type=\"fig\" rid=\"apjl327453f1\">1</xref> has the same color as arcs A&ndash;E and lies near the Einstein ring but spectroscopic data are inconclusive as to whether it has the same redshift as the confirmed arcs.</p>\n</sec>\n</sec>\n<sec id=\"apjl327453s3\">\n<label>3.</label><title>DISCUSSION</title>\n<p>The total mass inside the Einstein ring of <italic>r<sub>E</sub></italic> &equals; 57.5 &plusmn; 3.1 kpc is 1.96<sup>&plus;0.22</sup><sub>&minus;0.20</sub> &times; 10<sup>13</sup>&thinsp;<italic>M</italic><sub>&sun;</sub> (e.g., Narayan &amp; Bartelmann <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjl327453r30\">1996</xref>). If we assume a singular isothermal sphere (SIS) profile for the central region of the cluster, this mass corresponds to a rest-frame velocity dispersion of 686<sup>&plus;15</sup><sub>&minus;19</sub>&thinsp;km&thinsp;s<sup>&minus;1</sup>, consistent with the value estimated from cluster member redshifts. We can estimate the magnification factor for arc A by using &mu; &equals; &verbar;1&sol;(1 &minus; <italic>r<sub>E</sub></italic>&sol;<italic>r</italic>)&verbar; (e.g., Narayan &amp; Bartelmann <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjl327453r30\">1996</xref>) for the SIS profile, where <italic>r</italic> is the distance of the image from the lens center. If we take <italic>r<sub>E</sub></italic> &equals; 7&farcs;13 (or 57.5 kpc), &mu; &sim; 18 for arc A. This implies that the unmagnified <italic>i</italic><sub>775</sub> magnitude for arc A is &sim; 26.6.</p>\n<p>To get a qualitative estimate on the change in the derived strong lensing mass caused by relaxing our assumption of a spherically symmetric mass distribution, we construct elliptical Navarro&ndash;Frenk&ndash;White profile (NFW) models (Glose &amp; Kneib <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjl327453r19\">2002</xref>) with a critical curve that passes through at least part of arcs A and E and passes between arc C and the lensing galaxy (cluster member no. 13). The model with the largest ellipticity that still meets these requirements has an ellipticity of 0.1. The mass enclosed within its critical curve is 1.79 &times; 10<sup>13</sup>&thinsp;<italic>M</italic><sub>&sun;</sub>, within the errors of the spherical models.</p>\n<p>The dynamical mass is estimated from the redshifts of the 21 cluster members with <italic>Q</italic> &gt; 1 in Table <xref ref-type=\"table\" rid=\"apjl327453t1\">1</xref> using the virial scaling relation from the simulations of Evrard et al. (<xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjl327453r13\">2008</xref>),\n<disp-formula id=\"apjl327453fd1\"><label>1</label><tex-math><?CDATA\n\\begin{equation}\n\\sigma _{\\rm DM}(M, z)=\\sigma _{\\rm DM, 15} \\left({h(z)M_{200}\\over {10^{15}\\,M_{\\odot }}} \\right) ^{\\alpha },\n\\end{equation}\n?></tex-math>\n<graphic xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" xlink:href=\"apjl327453fd1.gif\"/></disp-formula>where &sigma;<sub>DM</sub>(<italic>M</italic>, <italic>z</italic>) is the dark matter velocity dispersion, <italic>h</italic>(<italic>z</italic>) = <italic>H</italic>(<italic>z</italic>)&sol;100&thinsp;km&thinsp;s<sup>&minus;1</sup>, and <italic>M</italic><sub>200</sub> is the mass within <italic>r</italic><sub>200</sub> (the radius of a sphere where the mean density is 200 times the critical density at the redshift of the cluster). The constants &sigma;<sub>DM,15</sub> &equals; 1082.9 &plusmn; 4.0&thinsp;km&thinsp;s<sup>&minus;1</sup> and &alpha; &equals; 0.3361 &plusmn; 0.0026 are determined from the simulations of Evrard et al. (<xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjl327453r13\">2008</xref>). Assuming that the velocity bias <italic>b<sub>v</sub></italic> &equals; &sigma;<sub>v,gal</sub>&sol;&sigma;<sub>DM</sub> &equals; 1, we find <italic>M</italic><sub>200</sub> &equals; 3.33<sup>&plus;2.83</sup><sub>&minus;1.80</sub> &times; 10<sup>14</sup>&thinsp;<italic>M</italic><sub>&sun;</sub> and <italic>r</italic><sub>200</sub> &equals; 0.97 &plusmn; 0.22 Mpc. Thus, the mass to light ratio is &sim;114&thinsp;<italic>M</italic><sub>&sun;</sub>&sol;<italic>L</italic><sub><italic>B</italic>&odot;</sub>. In Figure <xref ref-type=\"fig\" rid=\"apjl327453f2\">2</xref>, we fit an NFW profile (Navarro et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjl327453r31\">1997</xref>) to the total cluster mass using the strong lensing and dynamical mass estimates. The model has a concentration parameter <italic>c</italic> &equals; 4.96<sup>&plus;1.81</sup><sub>&minus;1.03</sub> and a core radius <italic>r<sub>s</sub></italic> &equals; 0.179<sup>&plus;0.078</sup><sub>&minus;0.055</sub> Mpc, where uncertainties are computed via Monte Carlo simulation.</p>\n<fig id=\"apjl327453f2\" position=\"float\">\n<label>Figure 2.</label>\n<caption><p>Comparison of different mass estimates for WARPS J1415+36. The strong lensing, weak lensing, and dynamical mass estimates presented in this work are labeled above. <italic>M</italic><sub>2500(<italic>z</italic>)</sub> from the X-ray (Maughan et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjl327453r28\">2006</xref>) and SZ data (Muchovej et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjl327453r29\">2007</xref>) are also shown. The dashed line is an NFW profile fit to the strong lensing mass at <italic>r<sub>E</sub></italic> = 0.0575 Mpc and the dynamical mass at <italic>r</italic><sub>200</sub> = 0.97 Mpc (<italic>c</italic> &equals; 4.96<sup>&plus;1.81</sup><sub>&minus;1.03</sub> and <italic>r<sub>s</sub></italic> &equals; 0.179<sup>&plus;0.078</sup><sub>&minus;0.055</sub> Mpc).</p></caption>\n<graphic id=\"apjl327453f2_eps\" content-type=\"print\" alt-version=\"yes\" xlink:href=\"apjl327453f2_pr.eps\"/>\n<graphic id=\"apjl327453f2_tb\" content-type=\"thumb\" alt-version=\"yes\" xlink:href=\"apjl327453f2_tb.gif\"/>\n<graphic id=\"apjl327453f2_lr\" content-type=\"low\" alt-version=\"yes\" xlink:href=\"apjl327453f2_lr.gif\"/>\n<graphic id=\"apjl327453f2_hr\" content-type=\"high\" alt-version=\"yes\" xlink:href=\"apjl327453f2_hr.gif\"/>\n<graphic id=\"apjl327453f2_au\" content-type=\"author\" xlink:href=\"apjl327453f2_au.ps\"/>\n</fig>\n<p>For a simple test of the mass-observable relation in WARPS J1415.1+36, we compare our strong lensing and dynamical estimates and a model of the mass profile to an estimate of weak lensing mass, X-ray mass, and SZ mass in Figure <xref ref-type=\"fig\" rid=\"apjl327453f2\">2</xref>. Using the same deep ACS images, M. J. Jee et al. (2009, in preparation) derive the weak lensing mass assuming an NFW profile with a mass&ndash;concentration relation from Bullock et al. (<xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjl327453r8\">2001</xref>). The X-ray mass <italic>M</italic><sub>2500(<italic>z</italic>)</sub> (Maughan et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjl327453r28\">2006</xref>) is determined from a best-fit two-dimensional elliptical &beta;-model analyzed at <italic>r</italic><sub>2500(<italic>z</italic>)</sub>. The SZ value of <italic>M</italic><sub>2500(<italic>z</italic>)</sub> is derived from a spherical, isothermal &beta;-model fit to 30 GHz data from the SZ Array (Muchovej et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjl327453r29\">2007</xref>). In the X-ray and the SZ mass estimate, &Delta;(<italic>z</italic>) is a redshift-dependent density contrast as described in, e.g., Maughan et al. (<xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjl327453r28\">2006</xref>). Both the weak lensing mass and the X-ray mass are consistent with our results. However, the SZ mass is lower than the prediction of our model by roughly a factor of 2. In Muchovej et al. (<xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjl327453r29\">2007</xref>), it is noted that the SZ mass estimate is marginally lower than the X-ray value, likely due to low signal-to-noise ratio (S&sol;N) in SZ measurements, uncertainties in the absolute calibration, and possible systematic errors from the model assumptions. Additional observations with the SZA at 30 GHz and 90 GHz should improve the constraints on the SZ mass estimate and shed light on the apparent discrepancy.</p>\n<p>Using numerical simulations, Dolag et al. (<xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjl327453r11\">2004</xref>) predict the dependence of the average halo concentration parameter on the cluster redshift and virial mass\n<disp-formula id=\"apjl327453fd2\"><label>2</label><tex-math><?CDATA\n\\begin{equation}\nc(M, z) = {c_{0}\\over {1+z}} \\left({M\\over {M_{*}}}\\right)^{\\gamma }.\n\\end{equation}\n?></tex-math>\n<graphic xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" xlink:href=\"apjl327453fd2.gif\"/></disp-formula>Shaw et al. (<xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjl327453r34\">2006</xref>) find the best-fit parameters to be <italic>c</italic><sub>0</sub> &equals; 6.47 &plusmn; 0.03 and &gamma; &equals; &minus;0.12 &plusmn; 0.03 from <italic>N</italic>-body simulations. Here, <italic>M</italic> is the virial mass and <italic>M</italic><sub>&ast;</sub> &equals; 11.0 &times; 10<sup>14</sup>&thinsp;<italic>M</italic><sub>&sun;</sub>(Shaw et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjl327453r34\">2006</xref>). If we assume the dynamical mass (<italic>M</italic><sub>200</sub> &equals; 3.33 &times; 10<sup>14</sup>&thinsp;<italic>M</italic><sub>&sun;</sub>) to be the virial mass, Equation (<xref ref-type=\"disp-formula\" rid=\"apjl327453fd2\">2</xref>) predicts the average concentration to be <italic>c</italic> &equals; 3.70<sup>&plus;0.62&plus;1.9</sup><sub>&minus;0.34 &minus; 1.3</sub>, where the first set of errors is from the errors in the best-fit parameters (<italic>c</italic><sub>0</sub> and &gamma;) and the second set is from the scatter about the average (Bullock et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjl327453r8\">2001</xref>). Our concentration of <italic>c</italic> = 4.96 is higher than the predicted value of <italic>c</italic> but is well within its errors.</p>\n<p>We now examine the strong lensing arcs B, C, and D around cluster member no. 13. The radius of the Einstein ring for the lensing galaxy is estimated by the distance between the galaxy and arc C, 0&farcs;65<sup>&plus;0&farcs;35</sup><sub>&minus;0&farcs;30</sub>. The upper and lower bounds are set by the distances from the lensing galaxy to arcs D and B, respectively. Assuming that the deflection angle due to the cluster potential does not vary appreciably for arcs B, C, and D, the mass enclosed within the Einstein radius is estimated to be 1.65<sup>&plus;2.25</sup><sub>&minus;1.17</sub> &times; 10<sup>11</sup> <italic>M</italic><sub>&sun;</sub>.</p>\n</sec>\n<sec id=\"apjl327453s4\">\n<label>4.</label><title>SUMMARY</title>\n<p>The cluster WARPS J1415+36 at <italic>z</italic><sub>lens</sub> &equals; 1.026 is one of the most distant strong lensing clusters reported (Thompson et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjl327453r36\">2001</xref>; Gladders et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjl327453r17\">2003</xref>; Gilbank et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjl327453r15\">2008</xref>). Although the SZ mass estimate is not consistent with the rest of the data for this cluster, this work suggests that the mass-observable relations derived from numerical simulations and observations of strong lensing, weak lensing, dynamical mass, and X-ray mass still hold for clusters at redshifts at <italic>z</italic> &sim; 1.</p>\n<p>Recently Gilbank et al. (<xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjl327453r15\">2008</xref>) report multi-probe mass estimates for the strong lensing cluster, RCS2319, at <italic>z</italic> &equals; 0.91. Future surveys will soon reveal many new high-redshift, strong lensing systems and provide deep, multiwavelength images of these clusters. With the corresponding improvements in mass-observable relations in the decelerating regime, galaxy clusters will soon become an even more effective tool for cosmology.</p>\n</sec>\n</body>\n<back>\n<ack>\n<p>Financial support for this work was provided by NASA through program GO-10496 from the Space Telescope Science Institute, which is operated by AURA, Inc., under NASA contract NAS 5-26555.
