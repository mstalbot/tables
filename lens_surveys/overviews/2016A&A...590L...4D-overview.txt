Introduction</h2>\n<p>In the three decades since the first discoveries of strongly gravitationally lensed arcs (<a name=\"InR29\"></a><a href=\"/articles/aa/full_html/2016/06/aa28297-16/aa28297-16.html#R29\">Soucail et al. 1987</a>; <a name=\"InR13\"></a><a href=\"/articles/aa/full_html/2016/06/aa28297-16/aa28297-16.html#R13\">Lynds &amp; Petrosian 1989</a>), strong gravitational lensing has emerged as a highly important tool for studies of distant galaxies. The magnification and associated amplification caused by lensing has provided a rich source of information about source galaxies at spatial and spectral resolutions that would otherwise be unobtainable. The typical blue arcs found in ground-based imaging surveys are vigorously star-forming galaxies in the redshift interval <span class=\"simple-math\">1 &#8818; <i>z</i> &#8818; 3</span> (<a name=\"InR3\"></a><a href=\"/articles/aa/full_html/2016/06/aa28297-16/aa28297-16.html#R3\">Bayliss et al. 2011</a>), where the strong rest-frame UV flux has been redshifted into optical bands. A handful of very bright arcs have integrated magnitudes of <span class=\"simple-math\"><i>r</i> ~ 19</span>&#8211;20 (<a name=\"InR11\"></a><a href=\"/articles/aa/full_html/2016/06/aa28297-16/aa28297-16.html#R11\">Hammer et al. 1989</a>; <a name=\"InR35\"></a><a href=\"/articles/aa/full_html/2016/06/aa28297-16/aa28297-16.html#R35\">Yee et al. 1996</a>; <a name=\"InR2\"></a><a href=\"/articles/aa/full_html/2016/06/aa28297-16/aa28297-16.html#R2\">Allam et al. 2007</a>; <a name=\"InR28\"></a><a href=\"/articles/aa/full_html/2016/06/aa28297-16/aa28297-16.html#R28\">Smail et al. 2007</a>; <a name=\"InR5\"></a><a href=\"/articles/aa/full_html/2016/06/aa28297-16/aa28297-16.html#R5\">Belokurov et al. 2007</a>; <a name=\"InR12\"></a><a href=\"/articles/aa/full_html/2016/06/aa28297-16/aa28297-16.html#R12\">Koester et al. 2010</a>); the current record holder is RCS&#8201;GA032727<span class=\"simple-math\">&#8722;</span>132609 with an integrated (AB) magnitude <span class=\"simple-math\"><i>r</i> = 19.1</span> (<a name=\"InR34\"></a><a href=\"/articles/aa/full_html/2016/06/aa28297-16/aa28297-16.html#R34\">Wuyts et al. 2010</a>). </p>\n<p>We report here on the discovery of an exceptionally bright arc, spanning a total length along its path of <span class=\"simple-math\">55&#8242;&#8242;</span>. The arc was identified in ESO New Technology Telescope (NTT) follow-up imaging of the galaxy cluster PSZ1&#8201;G311.65<span class=\"simple-math\">&#8722;</span>18.48&#8201;, which was detected through its Sunyaev-Zeldovich (SZ) effect (<a name=\"InR30\"></a><a href=\"/articles/aa/full_html/2016/06/aa28297-16/aa28297-16.html#R30\">Sunyaev &amp; Zeldovich 1972</a>) in the ESA <i>Planck</i> survey (<a name=\"InR17\"></a><a href=\"/articles/aa/full_html/2016/06/aa28297-16/aa28297-16.html#R17\">Planck Collaboration XXIX 2014</a>). </p>\n<p>Throughout this letter, a flat <span class=\"simple-math\">&#923;</span>CDM cosmology is assumed with <span class=\"simple-math\">&#937;<sub>M</sub> = 0.3</span> and <span class=\"simple-math\"><i>H</i><sub>0</sub> = 70 km s<sup>-1</sup> Mpc<sup>-1</sup></span>. Photometric measurements are given in the Vega magnitude system throughout the paper, unless otherwise noted. </p>\n<h2 class=\"sec\">\n<a name=\"S2\"></a>2. ESO follow-up programme of <i>Planck</i> SZ clusters</h2>\n<p>The <i>Planck</i> all-sky survey has provided a significant number of sources detected by means of their SZ decrements over the entire extragalactic sky (<a name=\"InR15\"></a><a href=\"/articles/aa/full_html/2016/06/aa28297-16/aa28297-16.html#R15\">Planck Collaboration VIII 2011</a>; <a href=\"/articles/aa/full_html/2016/06/aa28297-16/aa28297-16.html#R17\">Planck Collaboration XXIX 2014</a>; <a name=\"InR20\"></a><a href=\"/articles/aa/full_html/2016/06/aa28297-16/aa28297-16.html#R20\">Planck Collaboration XXVII 2015</a>). To confirm their detection as galaxy clusters, extensive follow-up programmes of the cluster candidates were undertaken using optical telescopes (<a name=\"InR18\"></a><a href=\"/articles/aa/full_html/2016/06/aa28297-16/aa28297-16.html#R18\">Planck Collaboration Int. XXVI 2015</a>; <a name=\"InR19\"></a><a href=\"/articles/aa/full_html/2016/06/aa28297-16/aa28297-16.html#R19\">Planck Collaboration Int. XXXVI 2016</a>; <a name=\"InR31\"></a><a href=\"/articles/aa/full_html/2016/06/aa28297-16/aa28297-16.html#R31\">van der Burg et al. 2016</a>), with the aim of achieving full redshift determination for the <a href=\"/articles/aa/full_html/2016/06/aa28297-16/aa28297-16.html#R17\">Planck Collaboration XXIX (2014)</a> sample (hereafter PSZ1), in particular over the 65% of the sky that is least affected by Galactic foregrounds. In the southern hemisphere, we are conducting an ongoing ESO Large Programme (PI: N. Aghanim, &#8220;A <i>Planck</i>/ESO legacy sample of the most massive clusters&#8221;) for which imaging of 120 PSZ1 cluster candidates is performed using the EFOSC2 instrument at NTT (Aghanim et al., in prep.). Using the <span class=\"simple-math\"><i>R</i>&#8722;<i>z</i></span> colour of the red sequence of passively evolving galaxies (<a name=\"InR9\"></a><a href=\"/articles/aa/full_html/2016/06/aa28297-16/aa28297-16.html#R9\">Gladders &amp; Yee 2000</a>), we identify the subset of SZ cluster candidates that are confirmed to be real clusters at <span class=\"simple-math\"><i>z</i>&gt; 0.5</span>. This subsample is subsequently targeted for Very Large Telescope (VLT) multi-object spectroscopy to obtain spectroscopic redshifts and velocity dispersion for the clusters. </p>\n<p>The SZ cluster candidate PSZ1&#8201;G311.65<span class=\"simple-math\">&#8722;</span>18.48 was detected at <span class=\"simple-math\"><i>S</i>/<i>N</i> = 4.64</span> as part of the PSZ1 catalogue (<a href=\"/articles/aa/full_html/2016/06/aa28297-16/aa28297-16.html#R17\">Planck Collaboration XXIX 2014</a>). This candidate is noted as a CLASS2 detection, i.e. it is one of 170 PSZ1 detections that fulfill good or high-quality criteria either in the <i>Planck</i> SZ data, the ROSAT All-Sky Survey (RASS), or in data from the Wide-Field Infrared Survey Explorer (WISE), without simultaneously fulfilling all of these criteria. This candidate is also noted as having possible contamination by a radio source. The low significance of the PSZ1&#8201;G311.65<span class=\"simple-math\">&#8722;</span>18.48 detection, which is close to the PSZ1 threshold (<span class=\"simple-math\"><i>S</i>/<i>N</i> = 4.5</span>), has resulted in the downwards fluctuation in the <i>Planck</i> data, as the cluster is not included in the SZ catalogue based on the 29-month, full-mission <i>Planck</i> data (<a href=\"/articles/aa/full_html/2016/06/aa28297-16/aa28297-16.html#R20\">Planck Collaboration XXVII 2015</a>). PSZ1&#8201;G311.65<span class=\"simple-math\">&#8722;</span>18.48 is located in a region of moderate Galactic extinction (<span class=\"simple-math\"><i>E</i>(<i>B</i>&#8722;<i>V</i>) = 0.083</span>; <a name=\"InR25\"></a><a href=\"/articles/aa/full_html/2016/06/aa28297-16/aa28297-16.html#R25\">Schlafly &amp; Finkbeiner 2011</a>) and relatively high foreground star density (Fig. <a href=\"/articles/aa/full_html/2016/06/aa28297-16/aa28297-16.html#F1\">1</a>), which makes the cluster difficult to identify in archival images from the Digitized Sky Survey. </p>\n<p>\n        \n        <a name=\"F1\"></a><div class=\"inset\"><table>\n<tr>\n<td valign=\"middle\"><a href=\"/articles/aa/full_html/2016/06/aa28297-16/F1.html\" target=\"_blank\"><img alt=\"thumbnail\" src=\"/articles/aa/full_html/2016/06/aa28297-16/aa28297-16-fig1_small.jpg\"></a></td>\n<td class=\"img-txt\">\n<a href=\"/articles/aa/full_html/2016/06/aa28297-16/F1.html\" target=\"_blank\"><span class=\"bold\">Fig. 1</span></a><p>Colour image of the cluster lens and arc, generated from the NTT/EFOCS2 <span class=\"simple-math\"><i>R</i></span>-and <span class=\"simple-math\"><i>z</i></span>-band images. Observations</h2>\n<h3 class=\"sec2\">\n<a name=\"S4\"></a>3.1. Optical imaging</h3>\n<p>The <i>Planck</i> SZ cluster candidate PSZ1&#8201;G311.65<span class=\"simple-math\">&#8722;</span>18.48 was observed using EFOSC2 at ESO NTT in imaging mode on 2014 March 28 UT, using the Bessel <span class=\"simple-math\"><i>R</i></span> and Gunn <span class=\"simple-math\"><i>z</i></span> filter, with <span class=\"simple-math\">3 &#215; 400</span>&#8201;s exposure time in each band. The imaging data were de-biased, flat-fielded and combined using standard methodology. The seeing values measured in the combined <span class=\"simple-math\"><i>R</i></span>- and <span class=\"simple-math\"><i>z</i></span>-band images are <span class=\"img-inline\"><img src=\"/articles/aa/full_html/2016/06/aa28297-16/aa28297-16-eq25.png\"></span> and <span class=\"img-inline\"><img src=\"/articles/aa/full_html/2016/06/aa28297-16/aa28297-16-eq26.png\"></span>, respectively. </p>\n<p>The NTT images reveal a galaxy cluster with a central brightest cluster galaxy (BCG) offset by 36<span class=\"simple-math\">&#8242;&#8242;</span> from the <i>Planck</i> SZ detection (see Fig. <a href=\"/articles/aa/full_html/2016/06/aa28297-16/aa28297-16.html#F1\">1</a>). This is well within the median angular separation of <span class=\"img-inline\"><img src=\"/articles/aa/full_html/2016/06/aa28297-16/aa28297-16-eq28.png\"></span> between the real and estimated positions of PSZ1 clusters (<a href=\"/articles/aa/full_html/2016/06/aa28297-16/aa28297-16.html#R17\">Planck Collaboration XXIX 2014</a>). An X-ray source 1RXS&#8201;J155007.1<span class=\"simple-math\">&#8722;</span>781134, which is listed in the RASS Faint Source Catalogue (<a name=\"InR33\"></a><a href=\"/articles/aa/full_html/2016/06/aa28297-16/aa28297-16.html#R33\">Voges et al. 2000</a>), is located 4<span class=\"simple-math\">&#8242;&#8242;</span> from the BCG (cf. Fig. <a href=\"/articles/aa/full_html/2016/06/aa28297-16/aa28297-16.html#F1\">1</a>) and is likely associated with the cluster. </p>\n<p>The most striking feature is a long thin arc, visible to the northwest of the BCG and separated from it by 25<span class=\"simple-math\">&#8242;&#8242;</span> and 34<span class=\"simple-math\">&#8242;&#8242;</span>, respectively, at its two end points. We designate the arc PSZ1-ARC&#8201;G311.6602<span class=\"simple-math\">&#8722;</span>18.4624, which is derived from the position in Galactic coordinates of its maximum <span class=\"simple-math\"><i>R</i></span>-band surface brightness peak. This is similar to the nomenclature used for <i>Planck</i> SZ sources, but has two extra digits to reflect the higher positional accuracy. Relative to the position of the BCG, the total azimuthal extent of the arc is 108<span class=\"simple-math\">&#176;</span>. The upper left panel of Fig. <a href=\"/articles/aa/full_html/2016/06/aa28297-16/aa28297-16.html#F2\">2</a> shows a close-up of the NTT <span class=\"simple-math\"><i>R</i></span>-band image in the region of the arc. The arc is unresolved or barely resolved, with a full width at half maximum (FWHM) along the radial direction of <span class=\"simple-math\">~</span><span class=\"img-inline\"><img src=\"/articles/aa/full_html/2016/06/aa28297-16/aa28297-16-eq31.png\"></span>. PSZ1-ARC&#8201;G311.6602<span class=\"simple-math\">&#8722;</span>18.4624 has three brighter segments (S1-S3, listed in Table <a href=\"/articles/aa/full_html/2016/06/aa28297-16/aa28297-16.html#T1\">1</a>). The <span class=\"simple-math\"><i>R</i></span>-band image, in which the signal-to-noise of the arc is highest, reveals the arc to be contiguous, or nearly so: An azimuthally averaged (i.e. along the direction of the arc) flux measurement in the 8<span class=\"simple-math\">&#8242;&#8242;</span> gap between S2 and S3, while simultaneously averaging in bins of 3 pixels (<span class=\"img-inline\"><img src=\"/articles/aa/full_html/2016/06/aa28297-16/aa28297-16-eq32.png\"></span>) in width in the radial direction, yields a 5.8<span class=\"simple-math\"><i>&#963;</i></span> excess in flux, compared to the adjacent blank sky regions outside and inside the radius of the arc. This corresponds to a surface brightness of <span class=\"simple-math\">~</span>1% of the peak value of the arc. Faint flux in the 3<span class=\"simple-math\">&#8242;&#8242;</span> gap between S1 and S2 cannot unambiguously be associated with the arc owing to neighbouring foreground objects. Each of the brighter segments consists of multiple bright knots, which we interpret as unresolved star-forming clumps within the source galaxy. </p>\n<p>To measure the flux of PSZ1-ARC&#8201;G311.6602<span class=\"simple-math\">&#8722;</span>18.4624, we run SExtractor (<a name=\"InR6\"></a><a href=\"/articles/aa/full_html/2016/06/aa28297-16/aa28297-16.html#R6\">Bertin &amp; Arnouts 1996</a>) in dual-image mode on the stacked <span class=\"simple-math\"><i>R</i></span>- and <span class=\"simple-math\"><i>z</i></span>-band images (where the <span class=\"simple-math\"><i>z</i></span>-band image was smoothed to match the resolution of the <span class=\"simple-math\"><i>R</i></span>-band image) with the <span class=\"simple-math\"><i>R</i></span>-band image as a reference for object detection. A total of nine clumps are detected along the arc with a total combined flux that yields observed magnitudes <span class=\"simple-math\"><i>R</i> = 17.82</span> and <span class=\"simple-math\"><i>z</i> = 17.38</span> (corresponding to <span class=\"simple-math\"><i>R</i> = 17.62</span> and <span class=\"simple-math\"><i>z</i> = 17.26</span>, respectively, after correcting for Galactic extinction). </p>\n<p>\n          <a name=\"T1\"></a><div class=\"inset\"><div class=\"ligne\">\n<a href=\"/articles/aa/full_html/2016/06/aa28297-16/T1.html\" target=\"_blank\"><span class=\"bold\">Table 1</span></a><p>Astrometric and photometric properties of the three bright segments of PSZ1-ARC&#8201;G311.6602<span class=\"simple-math\">&#8722;</span>18.4624.</p>\n</div></div>\n        </p>\n<p>We also note that because of the extreme brightness of the arc, seven clumps along the arc are detected as individual objects in the USNO-B1.0 catalogue (<a name=\"InR14\"></a><a href=\"/articles/aa/full_html/2016/06/aa28297-16/aa28297-16.html#R14\">Monet et al. 2003</a>) with combined magnitudes <span class=\"simple-math\"><i>R</i>2 = 17.2</span> and <span class=\"simple-math\"><i>B</i>2 = 16.9</span>. </p>\n<h3 class=\"sec2\">\n<a name=\"S5\"></a>3.2. Infrared imaging</h3>\n<p>To further characterize PSZ1-ARC&#8201;G311.6602<span class=\"simple-math\">&#8722;</span>18.4624, imaging in the <span class=\"simple-math\"><i>J</i></span> and <span class=\"simple-math\"><i>K</i><sub>s</sub></span>-bands was performed using the FourStar instrument on the <i>Magellan-I Baade </i>Telescope, with total exposure times of 2100&#8201;s and 800&#8201;s, respectively. The seeing values measured in the combined images were <span class=\"img-inline\"><img src=\"/articles/aa/full_html/2016/06/aa28297-16/aa28297-16-eq55.png\"></span> (<span class=\"simple-math\"><i>J</i></span>-band) and <span class=\"img-inline\"><img src=\"/articles/aa/full_html/2016/06/aa28297-16/aa28297-16-eq56.png\"></span> (<span class=\"simple-math\"><i>K</i><sub>s</sub></span>-band). Close-up images of the arc are shown in the lower panels of Fig. <a href=\"/articles/aa/full_html/2016/06/aa28297-16/aa28297-16.html#F2\">2</a>. Photometry was performed by smoothing the <span class=\"simple-math\"><i>J</i></span>- and <span class=\"simple-math\"><i>K</i><sub>s</sub></span>-band images to the resolution of the <span class=\"simple-math\"><i>R</i></span>-band image and running SExtractor in dual detect mode with the <span class=\"simple-math\"><i>R</i></span>-band image as the reference for object detection. The photometric measurements were calibrated using unsaturated field stars with archival data from the Two Micron All Sky Survey (<a name=\"InR27\"></a><a href=\"/articles/aa/full_html/2016/06/aa28297-16/aa28297-16.html#R27\">Skrutskie et al. 2006</a>), yielding total magnitudes of <span class=\"simple-math\"><i>J</i> = 16.75</span> and <span class=\"simple-math\"><i>K</i><sub>s</sub> = 15.43</span> for the arc. The optical and infrared colours of each of the brighter arc segments S1-S3 are listed in Table <a href=\"/articles/aa/full_html/2016/06/aa28297-16/aa28297-16.html#T1\">1</a>. </p>\n<h3 class=\"sec2\">\n<a name=\"S6\"></a>3.3. Spectroscopy</h3>\n<p>In order to measure the redshift of PSZ1-ARC G311.6602<span class=\"simple-math\">&#8722;</span>18.4624 and the cluster lens PSZ1 G311.65<span class=\"simple-math\">&#8722;</span>18.48 we obtained a long-slit spectrum (spectral resolution <span class=\"simple-math\"><i>R</i> &#8771; 700</span>, slit width <span class=\"img-inline\"><img src=\"/articles/aa/full_html/2016/06/aa28297-16/aa28297-16-eq60.png\"></span>) using the IMACS instrument on the <i>Magellan-I Baade </i>Telescope with an exposure time of <span class=\"simple-math\">2 &#215; 750</span>&#8201;s. The slit intersected the arc at the location of the knot with the highest <span class=\"simple-math\"><i>R</i></span>-band surface brightness (see Fig. <a href=\"/articles/aa/full_html/2016/06/aa28297-16/aa28297-16.html#F2\">2</a>), while simultaneously covering the BCG. Part of the resulting spectrum is shown in Fig. <a href=\"/articles/aa/full_html/2016/06/aa28297-16/aa28297-16.html#F3\">3</a>, spanning the range <span class=\"simple-math\">~</span><span class=\"simple-math\">4000 &#197;&#8722;6500 &#197;</span>. The spectrum of PSZ1-ARC&#8201;G311.6602<span class=\"simple-math\">&#8722;</span>18.4624 reveals Ly&#8201;<span class=\"simple-math\"><i>&#945;</i></span> emission and numerous other lines seen in emission or absorption in the rest-frame UV, such as Si&#8201;II <span class=\"simple-math\"><i>&#955;&#955;</i></span>&#8201;1259,1294&#8201;&#197;, C&#8201;IV <span class=\"simple-math\"><i>&#955;&#955;</i></span>&#8201;1548, 1550&#8201;&#197;; the spectrum also includes nebular semi-forbidden transitions of O&#8201;III] <span class=\"simple-math\"><i>&#955;</i></span>&#8201;1666&#197;, Si&#8201;III] <span class=\"simple-math\"><i>&#955;</i></span>1892&#8201;&#197;, and C&#8201;III] <span class=\"simple-math\"><i>&#955;&#955;</i></span>1907, 1909&#8201;&#197;. There is no significant observed flux bluewards of Ly&#8201;<span class=\"simple-math\"><i>&#945;</i></span>. The spectrum does not show any indications of broad emission lines associated with typical quasar activity. </p>\n<p>The redshift of PSZ1-ARC&#8201;G311.6602<span class=\"simple-math\">&#8722;</span>18.4624 is <span class=\"simple-math\"><i>z</i> = 2.3686 &#177; 0.0006</span>, determined from the nebular emission lines. The observed Ly&#8201;<span class=\"simple-math\"><i>&#945;</i></span> emission line is redshifted with respect to the nebular lines, yielding <span class=\"simple-math\"><i>z</i> = 2.3708 &#177; 0.0004</span>. From the strength of the measured rest-frame equivalent width of <span class=\"simple-math\"><i>W</i><sub><i>r</i></sub>(Ly <i>&#945;</i>) = &#8722;24 &#177; 8 &#197;</span>, it can be properly classified as a <span class=\"simple-math\">Ly <i>&#945;</i></span> emitter (LAE). By modelling the observed <span class=\"simple-math\">Ly <i>&#945;</i></span>-line shape by radiative transfer through a dusty outflowing shell (<a name=\"InR1\"></a><a href=\"/articles/aa/full_html/2016/06/aa28297-16/aa28297-16.html#R1\">Ahn 2004</a>; <a name=\"InR32\"></a><a href=\"/articles/aa/full_html/2016/06/aa28297-16/aa28297-16.html#R32\">Verhamme et al. 2006</a>; <a name=\"InR24\"></a><a href=\"/articles/aa/full_html/2016/06/aa28297-16/aa28297-16.html#R24\">Schaerer et al. 2011</a>; <a name=\"InR10\"></a><a href=\"/articles/aa/full_html/2016/06/aa28297-16/aa28297-16.html#R10\">Gronke et al. 2015</a>) we obtain estimates for the intrinsic equivalent width of <span class=\"img-inline\"><img src=\"/articles/aa/full_html/2016/06/aa28297-16/aa28297-16-eq70.png\"></span>, the outflow velocity <span class=\"img-inline\"><img src=\"/articles/aa/full_html/2016/06/aa28297-16/aa28297-16-eq71.png\"></span>, and neutral hydrogen column density <span class=\"img-inline\"><img src=\"/articles/aa/full_html/2016/06/aa28297-16/aa28297-16-eq72.png\"></span>. We note that the estimate of <span class=\"simple-math\"><i>W</i><sub><i>r,i</i></sub>(Ly <i>&#945;</i>)</span> was derived independently of the <span class=\"simple-math\"><i>W</i><sub><i>r</i></sub>(Ly <i>&#945;</i>)</span> measurement and has smaller fractional errors as a result of simultaneously fitting the line and surrounding continuum, while forcing the best-fit line profile shape to a particular physically motivated theoretical model. The observed spectrum is typical of active starbursts with strong outflows in the interstellar medium. As illustrated in Fig. <a href=\"/articles/aa/full_html/2016/06/aa28297-16/aa28297-16.html#F3\">3</a>, the spectral features are similar to those seen in composite spectra of <span class=\"simple-math\"><i>z</i> ~ 3</span> Lyman break galaxies (LBGs; <a name=\"InR26\"></a><a href=\"/articles/aa/full_html/2016/06/aa28297-16/aa28297-16.html#R26\">Shapley et al. 2003</a>). The spectrum shows strong asymmetric, redshifted <span class=\"simple-math\">Ly <i>&#945;</i></span> emission and a mix of low-ionization (e.g. Si&#8201;II) absorption lines from neutral outflowing gas and high-ionization features such as a strong P Cygni stellar wind profile of C&#8201;IV, which is indicative of stellar winds from very young and massive stars. The observed C&#8201;III] <span class=\"simple-math\"><i>&#955;&#955;</i></span>1907, 1909&#8201;&#197; emission, <span class=\"simple-math\"><i>W</i><sub><i>r</i></sub>(C III] ) = &#8722;6 &#177; 4 &#197;</span>, is also indicative of an intense burst of star formation that is coupled with low metallicity (<a name=\"InR4\"></a><a href=\"/articles/aa/full_html/2016/06/aa28297-16/aa28297-16.html#R4\">Bayliss et al. 2014</a>; <a name=\"InR22\"></a><a href=\"/articles/aa/full_html/2016/06/aa28297-16/aa28297-16.html#R22\">Rigby et al. 2015</a>). </p>\n<p>The observed spectrum also covers the BCG, for which we see strong emission from the [O&#8201;II] <span class=\"simple-math\"><i>&#955;&#955;</i></span>&#8201;3727, 3729&#8201;&#197; doublet, in addition to typical absorption lines from early-type galaxies. These features yield a spectroscopic redshift <span class=\"simple-math\"><i>z</i> = 0.44316 &#177; 0.00035</span>, which we adopt as the value of the cluster redshift. </p>\n<h2 class=\"sec\">\n<a name=\"S7\"></a>4. Cluster mass estimates</h2>\n<h3 class=\"sec2\">\n<a name=\"S8\"></a>4.1. SZ mass estimate</h3>\n<p>The size&#8211;flux degeneracy observed in <i>Planck</i> SZ data can be broken with the intersection of the <span class=\"simple-math\"><i>M</i><sub>500</sub></span>&#8211;<span class=\"img-inline\"><img src=\"/articles/aa/full_html/2016/06/aa28297-16/aa28297-16-eq79.png\"></span> relation when the cluster redshift is known (<a href=\"/articles/aa/full_html/2016/06/aa28297-16/aa28297-16.html#R17\">Planck Collaboration XXIX 2014</a>). The derived <span class=\"simple-math\"><i>Y</i><sub>500</sub></span> parameter is the SZ mass proxy that is equivalent to the X-ray mass proxy <span class=\"simple-math\"><i>Y</i><sub>X</sub></span>. Under the two hypotheses of cosmology and scaling relation (<a name=\"InR16\"></a><a href=\"/articles/aa/full_html/2016/06/aa28297-16/aa28297-16.html#R16\">Planck Collaboration XX 2014</a>), it provides the best estimate of <span class=\"simple-math\"><i>Y</i><sub>500</sub></span> for the <i>Planck</i> SZ clusters and conversely an estimate of the SZ-mass that is X-ray calibrated. </p>\n<p>We use the 2D degeneracy plots measured from the <i>Planck</i> data in the direction of PSZ1&#8201;G311.65<span class=\"simple-math\">&#8722;</span>18.48 and the measured redshift. We derive an SZ mass <span class=\"img-inline\"><img src=\"/articles/aa/full_html/2016/06/aa28297-16/aa28297-16-eq83.png\"></span>, above the median mass, <span class=\"simple-math\"><i>M</i><sub>med</sub> = 5.7 &#215; 10<sup>14</sup><i>M</i><sub>&#8857;</sub></span>, of the newly discovered <i>Planck</i> clusters. We note that the <span class=\"simple-math\"><i>M</i><sub>500SZ</sub></span> value, and its uncertainty, does not take into account the hydrostatic mass bias (<a href=\"/articles/aa/full_html/2016/06/aa28297-16/aa28297-16.html#R16\">Planck Collaboration XX 2014</a>) and uncertainties in the value of this bias. The corresponding radius containing this mass is <span class=\"img-inline\"><img src=\"/articles/aa/full_html/2016/06/aa28297-16/aa28297-16-eq86.png\"></span>. </p>\n<p>\n          \n          <a name=\"F3\"></a><div class=\"inset\"><table>\n<tr>\n<td valign=\"middle\"><a href=\"/articles/aa/full_html/2016/06/aa28297-16/F3.html\" target=\"_blank\"><img alt=\"thumbnail\" src=\"/articles/aa/full_html/2016/06/aa28297-16/aa28297-16-fig3_small.jpg\"></a></td>\n<td class=\"img-txt\">\n<a href=\"/articles/aa/full_html/2016/06/aa28297-16/F3.html\" target=\"_blank\"><span class=\"bold\">Fig. 3</span></a><p>Observed spectrum of PSZ1-ARC&#8201;G311.6602<span class=\"simple-math\">&#8722;</span>18.4624 (black solid curve). Also plotted (orange curve) is a composite spectrum of <span class=\"simple-math\">~</span>1000 Lyman break galaxies at <span class=\"simple-math\"><i>z</i> ~ 3</span> from <a href=\"/articles/aa/full_html/2016/06/aa28297-16/aa28297-16.html#R26\">Shapley et al. Lensing mass estimate</h3>\n<p>The large azimuthal extent of the arc (<span class=\"simple-math\">108&#176;</span>) indicates a lens with a high degree of circular symmetry and a nearly perfect alignment of the cluster lens and PSZ1-ARC&#8201;G311.6602<span class=\"simple-math\">&#8722;</span> 18.4624 source galaxy along our line of sight. We derive a first estimate of the lensing mass by assuming a projected lens mass distribution that is circularly symmetric with an Einstein radius corresponding to the angular distance <span class=\"simple-math\"><i>&#952;</i><sub>arc</sub></span> between the BCG and the arc. </p>\n<p>Hence, we estimate the Einstein radius <span class=\"img-inline\"><img src=\"/articles/aa/full_html/2016/06/aa28297-16/aa28297-16-eq89.png\"></span> (the quoted uncertainty derived from the observed range in angular distance between the BCG and arc), corresponding to a physical radius in the lens plane of <span class=\"simple-math\"><i>r</i><sub>E</sub> = 0.169 &#177; 0.025</span> Mpc. The estimated projected 2D mass within this radius is <span class=\"img-inline\"><img src=\"/articles/aa/full_html/2016/06/aa28297-16/aa28297-16-eq91.png\"></span>, where <span class=\"simple-math\">&#931;<sub>crit</sub> = (<i>c</i><sup>2</sup>/ 4<i>&#960;G</i>)(<i>D</i><sub>s</sub>/<i>D</i><sub>d</sub><i>D</i><sub>ds</sub>)</span> is the critical surface mass density and <span class=\"simple-math\"><i>D</i><sub>s</sub></span>, <span class=\"simple-math\"><i>D</i><sub>d</sub></span>, and <span class=\"simple-math\"><i>D</i><sub>ds</sub></span> are angular diameter distances to the source, to the lens (deflector), and between the lens and source, respectively. </p>\n<p>Comparing the strong lensing mass estimate to the SZ mass estimate quoted above requires a significant extrapolation by a factor of <span class=\"simple-math\">~</span>6 in radius. A crude estimate can still be made using the fact that at radii of <span class=\"simple-math\">~</span>0.1&#8211;1 Mpc in a massive cluster the drop in matter density with radius is close to the <span class=\"simple-math\"><i>&#961;</i>(<i>r</i>) &#8733; <i>r</i><sup>-2</sup></span> behaviour of an isothermal sphere; this yields a 3D mass estimate of <span class=\"simple-math\"><i>M</i><sub>500</sub> = 7 &#177; 2 &#215; 10<sup>14</sup><i>M</i><sub>&#8857;</sub></span>, which is consistent with the SZ-based estimate. We caution, however, that numerical <span class=\"simple-math\"><i>N</i></span>-body simulations predict that the locations of tangential arcs such as PSZ1-ARC&#8201;G311.6602<span class=\"simple-math\">&#8722;</span>18.4624 show a wide scatter with respect to the true Einstein radii of clusters (<a name=\"InR21\"></a><a href=\"/articles/aa/full_html/2016/06/aa28297-16/aa28297-16.html#R21\">Puchwein &amp; Hilbert 2009</a>). Deriving an accurate strong lensing mass for PSZ1&#8201;G311.65<span class=\"simple-math\">&#8722;</span>18.48 will require detailed modelling of the cluster lens based on further spectroscopy and future high-resolution imaging of lensed sources behind the cluster. </p>\n<h2 class=\"sec\">\n<a name=\"S10\"></a>5. Summary and discussion</h2>\n<p>In this letter, we presented the discovery of the strongly lensed arc PSZ1-ARC&#8201;G311.6602<span class=\"simple-math\">&#8722;</span>18.4624 and made an initial characterization of its photometric, morphological and spectroscopic properties. The arc corresponds to a highly magnified, vigorously star-forming LAE at <span class=\"simple-math\"><i>z</i> = 2.369</span> that is lensed by a foreground galaxy cluster at <span class=\"simple-math\"><i>z</i> = 0.443</span>. </p>\n<p>The observed properties of PSZ1-ARC&#8201;G311.6602<span class=\"simple-math\">&#8722;</span>18.4624 outlined above make it a highly interesting target for follow-up studies. Given the typical compact morphologies revealed by <i>Hubble </i>Space Telescope (HST) images of <span class=\"simple-math\"><i>z</i> ~ 2</span>&#8211;3 LBGs (<a name=\"InR8\"></a><a href=\"/articles/aa/full_html/2016/06/aa28297-16/aa28297-16.html#R8\">Giavalisco 1998</a>), with half-light radii <span class=\"img-inline\"><img src=\"/articles/aa/full_html/2016/06/aa28297-16/aa28297-16-eq99.png\"></span>, it is not surprising that the arc is unresolved in the radial direction in <span class=\"img-inline\"><img src=\"/articles/aa/full_html/2016/06/aa28297-16/aa28297-16-eq100.png\"></span> seeing. However, as such strongly lensed images are typically stretched by a factor close to unity in the radial direction; the fact that the arc has a length along its path of <span class=\"simple-math\">55&#8243;</span> indicates a total lensing magnification factor of <span class=\"simple-math\"><i>&#956;</i> &#8805; 50</span>, which is extremely rare. On the other hand, even assuming magnifications as high as <span class=\"simple-math\"><i>&#956;</i> = 50 (100)</span> yields an un-lensed, Galactic extinction corrected <span class=\"simple-math\"><i>R</i></span>-band (AB) magnitude of <span class=\"simple-math\">22.0 (22.8)</span>, which is <span class=\"simple-math\">&#8819;</span>1.5 mag brighter than <span class=\"simple-math\"><i>L</i><sup><i>&#8902;</i></sup></span> LBGs at this redshift (<a name=\"InR7\"></a><a href=\"/articles/aa/full_html/2016/06/aa28297-16/aa28297-16.html#R7\">Bian et al. 2013</a>). This would classify the galaxy as one of the intrinsically most ultraviolet luminous &#8220;normal&#8221; (i.e., non-AGN) galaxies known at <span class=\"simple-math\"><i>z</i> ~ 2</span>&#8211;3. Such estimates are affected by the intrinsic ellipticity of the source galaxy: If the galaxy is intrinsically aligned with the direction of the shear, the magnification needed to account for the observed axis ratio would be smaller, but the intrinsic luminosity would be higher. If, on the other hand, it is aligned in the direction perpendicular to the shear direction, the magnification would be even higher. In any case, both the lensing magnification and intrinsic luminosity of PSZ1-ARC&#8201;G311.6602<span class=\"simple-math\">&#8722;</span>18.4624 are exceptional, and assuming more normal values for one only pushes the other towards even more extreme values. </p>\n<p>Using the Galactic dust-corrected total AB magnitude of the arc and assuming a magnification factor of 50, following <a name=\"InR23\"></a><a href=\"/articles/aa/full_html/2016/06/aa28297-16/aa28297-16.html#R23\">Sawicki &amp; Thompson  (2006)</a> we converted the unlensed arc magnitude into an absolute magnitude at 1700&#8201;&#197; and, hence, a star formation rate (SFR) was found to be of order 100 <span class=\"simple-math\"><i>M</i><sub>&#8857;</sub> yr<sup>-1</sup></span>. This estimate would be an underestimate if there were significant dust extinction in the galaxy. If the magnification is higher than 50, then the actual SFR is lower. </p>\n<p>Magnification factors <span class=\"simple-math\"><i>&#956;</i> &gt; 50</span> imply that sub-100 parsec details in the source galaxy can be resolved by HST or using adaptive optics on 8-m class ground-based telescopes. Such high-resolution imaging will allow identification of multiply imaged, star-forming clumps along the arc, which can be used as input for detailed modelling of the mass distribution of the cluster lens and yield constraints on the local magnification in each point along the arc, aiding in the physical interpretation of the observed properties of the arc. </p>\n<p>The measured redshift of the source also implies that a wide spectral range from <span class=\"simple-math\">Ly <i>&#945;</i></span> to <span class=\"simple-math\">H <i>&#945;</i></span> will be readily accessible for ground-based spectroscopy. The impressive amount of detail visible in the relatively shallow spectrum (with total exposure time of 25 min) shown in Fig. <a href=\"/articles/aa/full_html/2016/06/aa28297-16/aa28297-16.html#F3\">3</a>, covering only <span class=\"simple-math\">~</span>3% of its total length, gives an indication of the richness of information on the physical conditions obtainable from future multi-wavelength studies of this source, yielding detailed insights into the physical properties of its star formation activity and interstellar medium at an epoch when the universe was a quarter of its present age. </p>\n    <hr width=\"30%\" align=\"left\">\n<div>\n<a name=\"FN3\"></a>\n         \n        <p> Based on observations collected at the European Organisation for Astronomical Research in the Southern Hemisphere under ESO programme 192.A-0762.
