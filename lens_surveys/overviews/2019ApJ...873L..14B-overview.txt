itemprop=\"description\">\n             <P>We report the study of an \"Einstein Cross\" configuration first identified in a set of <I>HST</I> images by Cerny et al. Deep spectroscopic observations obtained at the Spanish 10.4 m Gran Telescopio Canarias telescope, allowed us to demonstrate the lens nature of the system, that consists of a Lyman-break galaxy (LBG), not a quasi-stellar object as is usually the case, at <I>z</I>\u00a0=\u00a03.03 lensed by a galaxy at <I>z</I>\u00a0=\u00a00.556. Combining the new spectroscopy with the archival <I>HST</I> data, it turns out that the lens is an elliptical galaxy with <I>M</I> <SUB> <I>V</I> </SUB>\u00a0=\u00a0\u221221.0, effective radius 2.8 kpc, and stellar velocity dispersion <I>\u03c3</I>\u00a0=\u00a0208\u00a0\u00b1\u00a039 km s<SUP>\u22121</SUP>. The source is an LBG with Ly<I>\u03b1</I> luminosity ~<I>L</I>* at that redshift. From the modeling of the system, performed by assuming a singular isothermal ellipsoid (SIE) with external shear, we estimate that the flux source is magnified about 4.5 times, and the velocity dispersion of the lens is <IMG ALIGN=\"MIDDLE\" alt=\"${\\sigma }_{\\mathrm{SIE}}={197.9}_{-1.3}^{+2.6}$\" src=\"https://static.iopscience.com/2.44.0/img/lazy-loading-placeholder.gif\" data-src=\"https://cdn.iopscience.com/images/2041-8205/873/2/L14/apjlab0aebieqn1.gif\"></IMG> km s<SUP>\u22121</SUP>, in good agreement with the value derived spectroscopically. This is the second case known of an Einstein cross of an LBG.</P> </div>\n    <!-- End abstract -->\n<!-- Start Export citation and abstract tools -->\n\t<p>\n\t\t<small>Export citation and itemprop=\"articleBody\" class=\"wd-jnl-art-full-text article-text\">\n<h2 class=\"header-anchor\" id=\"apjlab0aebs1\" name=\"apjlab0aebs1\">1.\u00a0Introduction</h2><div class=\"article-text\" data-mobile-collapse=\"\"><p>Gravitational lenses represent one of the most powerful tools to probe the properties of distant galaxies and the cosmological parameters. Strong gravitational lensing produces multiple images of distant sources that have their lines of sight very close to foreground massive objects (see Treu <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjlab0aebbib22\" id=\"fnref-apjlab0aebbib22\">2010</a>, for a review). In addition, the special case of image splitting of distant quasars can provide the direct measurement of the Hubble constant from correlated flux variability (Treu &amp; Koopmans <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjlab0aebbib24\" id=\"fnref-apjlab0aebbib24\">2002</a>; Suyu et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjlab0aebbib21\" id=\"fnref-apjlab0aebbib21\">2014</a>).</p><p>Accurate lens modeling can precisely probe the density profile of galaxies at cosmological distances, specifically the mass enclosed within the Einstein radius and the mean local density slope within it. Combining lensing and dynamics allows the central dark matter profile to be robustly inferred (e.g., Treu &amp; Koopmans <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjlab0aebbib23\" id=\"fnref-apjlab0aebbib23\">2004</a>). All these facts make this kind of configuration a fantastic laboratory for the study of the universe.</p><p>Of particular interest is the detection of quadruple images of lensed quasi-stellar object (QSO) in the shape of an Einstein Cross (see e.g., Wisotzki et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjlab0aebbib29\" id=\"fnref-apjlab0aebbib29\">2002</a>; Morgan et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjlab0aebbib10\" id=\"fnref-apjlab0aebbib10\">2004</a> for first discoveries). However, these optical structures are rare on the sky (Oguri &amp; Marshall <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjlab0aebbib12\" id=\"fnref-apjlab0aebbib12\">2010</a>) as they require a very close alignment of quasars with foreground massive galaxies. Various large-area sky surveys are planned in the near future to increase the number of such systems (e.g., Schechter et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjlab0aebbib17\" id=\"fnref-apjlab0aebbib17\">2017</a>; Williams et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjlab0aebbib27\" id=\"fnref-apjlab0aebbib27\">2017</a>, <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjlab0aebbib28\" id=\"fnref-apjlab0aebbib28\">2018</a>; Agnello et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjlab0aebbib1\" id=\"fnref-apjlab0aebbib1\">2018</a>), and recently the <em>H</em><sub><em>o</em></sub> Lenses in COSMO-GRAILs Wellspring (H0LiCOW) program (Bonvin et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjlab0aebbib3\" id=\"fnref-apjlab0aebbib3\">2017</a>; Suyu et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjlab0aebbib19\" id=\"fnref-apjlab0aebbib19\">2017</a>) listed the five best lensed quasars discovered to date showing an Einstein Cross structure.</p><p>During a search for high-<em>z</em> galaxies from the Reionization Lensing Cluster Survey (RELICS) of the Hubble Treasury Program (Salmon et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjlab0aebbib15\" id=\"fnref-apjlab0aebbib15\">2017</a>), a possible new Einstein Cross configuration around a galaxy located at <em>\u03b1</em>\u00a0=\u00a022:11:41.99, <em>\u03b4</em>\u00a0=\u00a003:50:52.3 was discovered by Cerny et al. (<a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjlab0aebbib6\" id=\"fnref-apjlab0aebbib6\">2018</a>; see Figure <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" href=\"#apjlab0aebf1\">1</a>). Later on, during a search for stripped galaxies in the <em>Hubble Space Telescope</em> (<em>HST</em>) images from the RELICS project, the serendipitous re-discovery of this object by one of us (A. O.) led to the observations presented in this Letter. The system, hereafter called J2211\u20130350, is sitting ~90 arcsec southwest from the core of the cluster RXC J2211.7\u22120349 (<em>z</em>\u00a0=\u00a00.397) and, based on the redder color of the alleged lens galaxy, it was argued that the lens had to be well behind the nearby cluster. The system is composed by an early-type red galaxy surrounded by four blue objects that are arranged in the shape of a \"Latin Cross\" around the central galaxy. In Table <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"tabref\" href=\"#apjlab0aebt1\">1</a> we report the magnitudes and the relative positions of the image components. We used the optical spectrograph OSIRIS (Cepa et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjlab0aebbib5\" id=\"fnref-apjlab0aebbib5\">2003</a>) with the grism R1000B covering the spectral range 4100\u20137500 \u00c5, and a slit width of 1.0 arcsec. This configuration yields an effective spectral resolution of <em>R</em>\u00a0~\u00a0600.</p><p>Figure <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" href=\"#apjlab0aebf1\">1</a> shows an <em>HST</em> image of the target with superimposed the position of the slit, located so to observe both the lensing galaxy and the three brighter images of the source at once. Only source D is outside the slit. Three independent exposures of 1800 s were obtained under photometric conditions and good atmospheric seeing (0.8 arcsec).</p><p>Standard IRAF<sup xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"fnref\"><a class=\"fnref\" href=\"#apjlab0aebfn1\">6</a></sup>\n tools were adopted for the data reduction. Bias subtraction, flat field correction, image alignment, and combination were performed. Cosmic rays were cleaned by combining the three independent exposures and using the <em>crreject</em> algorithm. The spectra were then calibrated in wavelength with ~0.2 \u00c5 accuracy. Data of a spectrophotometric standard star observed on the same night were used to perform a relative flux calibration of the spectrum. <em>HST</em> photometry on filter F606W was then used to achieve absolute flux calibration of the spectrum (see Table <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"tabref\" href=\"#apjlab0aebt1\">1</a>).</p><p>The final 2D spectrum is characterized by the strong emission from three blobs coincident with the spatial location in the slit of the three lensed sources (see Figures <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" href=\"#apjlab0aebf2\">2</a> and <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" href=\"#apjlab0aebf3\">3</a>). We used the library of single stellar population spectra from Vazdekis et al. (<a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjlab0aebbib25\" id=\"fnref-apjlab0aebbib25\">2012</a>) properly convolved with the instrumental resolution to fit the lines' profile. The best fit corresponds to a velocity dispersion <em>\u03c3</em>\u00a0=\u00a0208\u00a0\u00b1\u00a039 km s<sup>\u22121</sup>. These results confirm the photometric redshift found by Cerny et al. (<a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjlab0aebbib6\" id=\"fnref-apjlab0aebbib6\">2018</a>) that the galaxy is not a member of the cluster RXC J2211.7\u22120349.</p><p>The ACS+F814W image was used to derive the properties of the lensing galaxy. After properly masking the four images of the source, we modeled the lens galaxy with a S\u00e9rsic law. The data were deconvolved using as point-spread function (PSF) an unsaturated and isolated star present in the field. We found that the lens galaxy is well fitted by a model with <em>n</em>\u00a0=\u00a05 and effective radius <em>r</em><sub><em>e</em></sub>\u00a0=\u00a00.44 arcsec corresponding to <em>R</em><sub><em>e</em></sub>\u00a0~\u00a02.8 kpc. The absolute magnitude of the lens galaxy (<em>z</em>\u00a0=\u00a00.556) corresponds to <em>M</em><sub><em>V</em></sub>\u00a0~\u00a0\u221221.0 (taking into account <em>k</em>-correction). The mass of the lens galaxy estimated from the measured <em>\u03c3</em> and <em>R</em><sub><em>e</em></sub> is <em>M</em><sub>gal</sub>\u00a0=\u00a04.5\u00a0<b>&#x00d7;</b>\u00a010<sup>10</sup> <em>M</em><sub>\u2609</sub>.</p><h3 id=\"apjlab0aebs3-1\" name=\"apjlab0aebs3-1\">3.1.\u00a0Lens Modeling</h3><div class=\"article-text\"><p>We performed the lens modeling of the system by using the code by Enia et al. (<a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjlab0aebbib7\" id=\"fnref-apjlab0aebbib7\">2018</a>), which implements the regularized semi-linear inversion formalism with adaptive pixel scale (Warren &amp; Dye <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjlab0aebbib26\" id=\"fnref-apjlab0aebbib26\">2003</a>; Suyu et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjlab0aebbib20\" id=\"fnref-apjlab0aebbib20\">2006</a>; Nightingale &amp; Dye <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjlab0aebbib11\" id=\"fnref-apjlab0aebbib11\">2015</a>, and references therein).</p><p>The source plane (SP; i.e., the plane orthogonal to the line of sight of the observer to the deflector, containing the background source) is gridded into pixels whose values represent the source surface brightness counts and are treated as free parameters. This approach avoids any a priori analytic assumption on the surface brightness of the background source. The size of the pixels adapts to the magnification pattern, being smaller closer to the regions of increasing magnification, to ensure a uniform signal-to-noise ratio (S/N) across the reconstructed source and to fully exploit the increase in spatial resolution in highly magnified regions provided by gravitational lensing. For a fixed mass model of the deflector (or lens), the SP is mapped into the image plane (IP; i.e., the plane containing the deflector and orthogonal to the line of sight of the observer to the deflector), then convolved with the PSF, and finally compared to the observed image. In order to avoid unphysical solutions, in with the reconstructed source presenting severe discontinuities and pixel-to-pixel variations, a <em>regularization term</em> is added to the merit function. The weight of the regularization term is calculated via Bayesian analysis, according to Suyu et al. (<a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjlab0aebbib20\" id=\"fnref-apjlab0aebbib20\">2006</a>).</p><p>The mass distribution of the lens is modeled as a singular isothermal ellipsoid (SIE), described by the following parameters: the Einstein radius (<em>\u03b8</em><sub>E</sub>), the position of the lens centroid (<em>x</em><sub>L</sub>, <em>y</em><sub>L</sub>), the minor-to-major axis ratio (<em>q</em><sub>L</sub>), and the orientation angle (<em>\u03b8</em><sub>L</sub>; counter-clockwise from west). Although the object is at the edge of the nearby cluster, to model the lens an external shear is included in the model. It is described by the shear strength (<em>\u03b3</em>) and the shear angle (<em>\u03b8</em><sub><em>\u03b3</em></sub>; counter-clockwise from west). The search for the best-fitting parameters of the lens is done using the <span class=\"small-caps\">emcee</span> code (Foreman-Mackey et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjlab0aebbib30\" id=\"fnref-apjlab0aebbib30\">2013</a>), which implements the Markov Chain Monte Carlo technique to sample the posterior probability density function of the model parameters.</p><p>The magnification factor, <em>\u03bc</em>, is calculated as the ratio between the total flux density of the sources, as measured in the SP within the region of S/N\u00a0\u2265\u00a03, and the flux density of the corresponding image in the IP. The uncertainty on the magnification factor is derived by computing <em>\u03bc</em> 1000 times, perturbing each time the lens model parameters around their best-fitting values.</p><p>The modeling is carried out on the reduced ACS + F606w image, with a pixel scale of 0<img src=\"https://cdn.iopscience.com/icons/Entities/farcs.gif\" alt=\"farcs\" align=\"bottom\" />06. A noise map is constructed from the provided weight map and the PSF is obtained by median combining three unsaturated stars in the vicinity of the target. The lens is subtracted from the image after fitting its light profile with GALFIT (Peng et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjlab0aebbib14\" id=\"fnref-apjlab0aebbib14\">2002</a>).</p><p>The best-fitting SIE model has <span xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"inline-eqn\"><span class=\"tex\"><span class=\"texImage\"><img The lens is an elliptical galaxy (<em>M</em><sub><em>V</em></sub>\u00a0~\u00a0\u221221) at <em>z</em>\u00a0=\u00a00.556, while the lensed source is an LBG at <em>z</em>\u00a0=\u00a03.03. Modeling of the lens shows that the Einstein Cross is well reproduced by an SIE model with an Einstein ring of 0.76 arcsec and that includes the shear effect due to the foreground massive low-redshift cluster of galaxies. The lensed source is magnified by a factor 4.5. This gravitational lens is similar to the case reported by Bolton et al. (<a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjlab0aebbib2\" id=\"fnref-apjlab0aebbib2\">2006</a>) for J1011+0143. This is the second case of an Einstein Cross gravitational lens produced by a distant LBG. The intrinsic Ly<em>\u03b1</em> luminosity, taking into account the magnification factor, is <em>L</em>(Ly<em>\u03b1</em>)\u00a0=\u00a05\u00a0<b>&#x00d7;</b>\u00a010<sup>42</sup> erg s<sup>\u22121</sup>, a factor ~2 higher than that found by Bolton et al. (<a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjlab0aebbib2\" id=\"fnref-apjlab0aebbib2\">2006</a>) for J1011+0143. The Ly<em>\u03b1</em> luminosity is close to <em>L</em>* of the luminosity function of high-redshift Ly<em>\u03b1</em> emitters (see e.g., Sobral et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjlab0aebbib18\" id=\"fnref-apjlab0aebbib18\">2018</a>).</p></div> <div class=\"article-text\" data-mobile-collapse=\"\"><p>We thank Mario Radovich, Simona Paiano, and Aldo Treves for useful discussions. itemprop=\"description\">\n             <P>We report the study of an \"Einstein Cross\" configuration first identified in a set of <I>HST</I> images by Cerny et al. Deep spectroscopic observations obtained at the Spanish 10.4 m Gran Telescopio Canarias telescope, allowed us to demonstrate the lens nature of the system, that consists of a Lyman-break galaxy (LBG), not a quasi-stellar object as is usually the case, at <I>z</I>\u00a0=\u00a03.03 lensed by a galaxy at <I>z</I>\u00a0=\u00a00.556. Combining the new spectroscopy with the archival <I>HST</I> data, it turns out that the lens is an elliptical galaxy with <I>M</I> <SUB> <I>V</I> </SUB>\u00a0=\u00a0\u221221.0, effective radius 2.8 kpc, and stellar velocity dispersion <I>\u03c3</I>\u00a0=\u00a0208\u00a0\u00b1\u00a039 km s<SUP>\u22121</SUP>. The source is an LBG with Ly<I>\u03b1</I> luminosity ~<I>L</I>* at that redshift. From the modeling of the system, performed by assuming a singular isothermal ellipsoid (SIE) with external shear, we estimate that the flux source is magnified about 4.5 times, and the velocity dispersion of the lens is <IMG ALIGN=\"MIDDLE\" alt=\"${\\sigma }_{\\mathrm{SIE}}={197.9}_{-1.3}^{+2.6}$\" src=\"https://static.iopscience.com/2.44.0/img/lazy-loading-placeholder.gif\" data-src=\"https://cdn.iopscience.com/images/2041-8205/873/2/L14/apjlab0aebieqn1.gif\"></IMG> km s<SUP>\u22121</SUP>, in good agreement with the value derived spectroscopically. This is the second case known of an Einstein cross of an LBG.</P> </div>\n    <!-- End abstract -->\n<!-- Start Export citation and abstract tools -->\n\t<p>\n\t\t<small>Export citation and itemprop=\"articleBody\" class=\"wd-jnl-art-full-text article-text\">\n<h2 class=\"header-anchor\" id=\"apjlab0aebs1\" name=\"apjlab0aebs1\">1.\u00a0Introduction</h2><div class=\"article-text\" data-mobile-collapse=\"\"><p>Gravitational lenses represent one of the most powerful tools to probe the properties of distant galaxies and the cosmological parameters. Strong gravitational lensing produces multiple images of distant sources that have their lines of sight very close to foreground massive objects (see Treu <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjlab0aebbib22\" id=\"fnref-apjlab0aebbib22\">2010</a>, for a review). In addition, the special case of image splitting of distant quasars can provide the direct measurement of the Hubble constant from correlated flux variability (Treu &amp; Koopmans <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjlab0aebbib24\" id=\"fnref-apjlab0aebbib24\">2002</a>; Suyu et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjlab0aebbib21\" id=\"fnref-apjlab0aebbib21\">2014</a>).</p><p>Accurate lens modeling can precisely probe the density profile of galaxies at cosmological distances, specifically the mass enclosed within the Einstein radius and the mean local density slope within it. Combining lensing and dynamics allows the central dark matter profile to be robustly inferred (e.g., Treu &amp; Koopmans <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjlab0aebbib23\" id=\"fnref-apjlab0aebbib23\">2004</a>). All these facts make this kind of configuration a fantastic laboratory for the study of the universe.</p><p>Of particular interest is the detection of quadruple images of lensed quasi-stellar object (QSO) in the shape of an Einstein Cross (see e.g., Wisotzki et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjlab0aebbib29\" id=\"fnref-apjlab0aebbib29\">2002</a>; Morgan et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjlab0aebbib10\" id=\"fnref-apjlab0aebbib10\">2004</a> for first discoveries). However, these optical structures are rare on the sky (Oguri &amp; Marshall <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjlab0aebbib12\" id=\"fnref-apjlab0aebbib12\">2010</a>) as they require a very close alignment of quasars with foreground massive galaxies. Various large-area sky surveys are planned in the near future to increase the number of such systems (e.g., Schechter et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjlab0aebbib17\" id=\"fnref-apjlab0aebbib17\">2017</a>; Williams et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjlab0aebbib27\" id=\"fnref-apjlab0aebbib27\">2017</a>, <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjlab0aebbib28\" id=\"fnref-apjlab0aebbib28\">2018</a>; Agnello et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjlab0aebbib1\" id=\"fnref-apjlab0aebbib1\">2018</a>), and recently the <em>H</em><sub><em>o</em></sub> Lenses in COSMO-GRAILs Wellspring (H0LiCOW) program (Bonvin et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjlab0aebbib3\" id=\"fnref-apjlab0aebbib3\">2017</a>; Suyu et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjlab0aebbib19\" id=\"fnref-apjlab0aebbib19\">2017</a>) listed the five best lensed quasars discovered to date showing an Einstein Cross structure.</p><p>During a search for high-<em>z</em> galaxies from the Reionization Lensing Cluster Survey (RELICS) of the Hubble Treasury Program (Salmon et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjlab0aebbib15\" id=\"fnref-apjlab0aebbib15\">2017</a>), a possible new Einstein Cross configuration around a galaxy located at <em>\u03b1</em>\u00a0=\u00a022:11:41.99, <em>\u03b4</em>\u00a0=\u00a003:50:52.3 was discovered by Cerny et al. (<a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjlab0aebbib6\" id=\"fnref-apjlab0aebbib6\">2018</a>; see Figure <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" href=\"#apjlab0aebf1\">1</a>). Later on, during a search for stripped galaxies in the <em>Hubble Space Telescope</em> (<em>HST</em>) images from the RELICS project, the serendipitous re-discovery of this object by one of us (A. O.) led to the observations presented in this Letter. The system, hereafter called J2211\u20130350, is sitting ~90 arcsec southwest from the core of the cluster RXC J2211.7\u22120349 (<em>z</em>\u00a0=\u00a00.397) and, based on the redder color of the alleged lens galaxy, it was argued that the lens had to be well behind the nearby cluster. The system is composed by an early-type red galaxy surrounded by four blue objects that are arranged in the shape of a \"Latin Cross\" around the central galaxy. In Table <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"tabref\" href=\"#apjlab0aebt1\">1</a> we report the magnitudes and the relative positions of the image components. We used the optical spectrograph OSIRIS (Cepa et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjlab0aebbib5\" id=\"fnref-apjlab0aebbib5\">2003</a>) with the grism R1000B covering the spectral range 4100\u20137500 \u00c5, and a slit width of 1.0 arcsec. This configuration yields an effective spectral resolution of <em>R</em>\u00a0~\u00a0600.</p><p>Figure <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" href=\"#apjlab0aebf1\">1</a> shows an <em>HST</em> image of the target with superimposed the position of the slit, located so to observe both the lensing galaxy and the three brighter images of the source at once. Only source D is outside the slit. Three independent exposures of 1800 s were obtained under photometric conditions and good atmospheric seeing (0.8 arcsec).</p><p>Standard IRAF<sup xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"fnref\"><a class=\"fnref\" href=\"#apjlab0aebfn1\">6</a></sup>\n tools were adopted for the data reduction. Bias subtraction, flat field correction, image alignment, and combination were performed. Cosmic rays were cleaned by combining the three independent exposures and using the <em>crreject</em> algorithm. The spectra were then calibrated in wavelength with ~0.2 \u00c5 accuracy. Data of a spectrophotometric standard star observed on the same night were used to perform a relative flux calibration of the spectrum. <em>HST</em> photometry on filter F606W was then used to achieve absolute flux calibration of the spectrum (see Table <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"tabref\" href=\"#apjlab0aebt1\">1</a>).</p><p>The final 2D spectrum is characterized by the strong emission from three blobs coincident with the spatial location in the slit of the three lensed sources (see Figures <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" href=\"#apjlab0aebf2\">2</a> and <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" href=\"#apjlab0aebf3\">3</a>). Including the contribution of source D (estimated from the <em>HST</em> photometry), we derive a total observed line luminosity of <em>L</em>(Ly<em>\u03b1</em>)\u00a0=\u00a02.5\u00a0<b>&#x00d7;</b>\u00a010<sup>43</sup> erg s<sup>\u22121</sup>.</p><p>The red part of the spectrum is dominated by the signal from the lens galaxy. Using the pPXF (Cappellari &amp; Emsellem <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjlab0aebbib4\" id=\"fnref-apjlab0aebbib4\">2004</a>) IDL routines we measured the stellar velocity dispersion. The Ca <span class=\"small-caps\">ii</span> H and K absorption lines were fitted at <em>z</em>\u00a0=\u00a00.556 to estimate the galaxy velocity dispersion. We used the library of single stellar population spectra from Vazdekis et al. (<a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjlab0aebbib25\" id=\"fnref-apjlab0aebbib25\">2012</a>) properly convolved with the instrumental resolution to fit the lines' profile. The best fit corresponds to a velocity dispersion <em>\u03c3</em>\u00a0=\u00a0208\u00a0\u00b1\u00a039 km s<sup>\u22121</sup>. These results confirm the photometric redshift found by Cerny et al. (<a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjlab0aebbib6\" id=\"fnref-apjlab0aebbib6\">2018</a>) that the galaxy is not a member of the cluster RXC J2211.7\u22120349.</p><p>The ACS+F814W image was used to derive the properties of the lensing galaxy. After properly masking the four images of the source, we modeled the lens galaxy with a S\u00e9rsic law. The data were deconvolved using as point-spread function (PSF) an unsaturated and isolated star present in the field. We found that the lens galaxy is well fitted by a model with <em>n</em>\u00a0=\u00a05 and effective radius <em>r</em><sub><em>e</em></sub>\u00a0=\u00a00.44 arcsec corresponding to <em>R</em><sub><em>e</em></sub>\u00a0~\u00a02.8 kpc. The absolute magnitude of the lens galaxy (<em>z</em>\u00a0=\u00a00.556) corresponds to <em>M</em><sub><em>V</em></sub>\u00a0~\u00a0\u221221.0 (taking into account <em>k</em>-correction). The mass of the lens galaxy estimated from the measured <em>\u03c3</em> and <em>R</em><sub><em>e</em></sub> is <em>M</em><sub>gal</sub>\u00a0=\u00a04.5\u00a0<b>&#x00d7;</b>\u00a010<sup>10</sup> <em>M</em><sub>\u2609</sub>.</p><h3 id=\"apjlab0aebs3-1\" name=\"apjlab0aebs3-1\">3.1.\u00a0Lens Modeling</h3><div class=\"article-text\"><p>We performed the lens modeling of the system by using the code by Enia et al. (<a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjlab0aebbib7\" id=\"fnref-apjlab0aebbib7\">2018</a>), which implements the regularized semi-linear inversion formalism with adaptive pixel scale (Warren &amp; Dye <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjlab0aebbib26\" id=\"fnref-apjlab0aebbib26\">2003</a>; Suyu et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjlab0aebbib20\" id=\"fnref-apjlab0aebbib20\">2006</a>; Nightingale &amp; Dye <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjlab0aebbib11\" id=\"fnref-apjlab0aebbib11\">2015</a>, and references therein).</p><p>The source plane (SP; i.e., the plane orthogonal to the line of sight of the observer to the deflector, containing the background source) is gridded into pixels whose values represent the source surface brightness counts and are treated as free parameters. This approach avoids any a priori analytic assumption on the surface brightness of the background source. The size of the pixels adapts to the magnification pattern, being smaller closer to the regions of increasing magnification, to ensure a uniform signal-to-noise ratio (S/N) across the reconstructed source and to fully exploit the increase in spatial resolution in highly magnified regions provided by gravitational lensing. For a fixed mass model of the deflector (or lens), the SP is mapped into the image plane (IP; i.e., the plane containing the deflector and orthogonal to the line of sight of the observer to the deflector), then convolved with the PSF, and finally compared to the observed image. In order to avoid unphysical solutions, in with the reconstructed source presenting severe discontinuities and pixel-to-pixel variations, a <em>regularization term</em> is added to the merit function. The weight of the regularization term is calculated via Bayesian analysis, according to Suyu et al. (<a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjlab0aebbib20\" id=\"fnref-apjlab0aebbib20\">2006</a>).</p><p>The mass distribution of the lens is modeled as a singular isothermal ellipsoid (SIE), described by the following parameters: the Einstein radius (<em>\u03b8</em><sub>E</sub>), the position of the lens centroid (<em>x</em><sub>L</sub>, <em>y</em><sub>L</sub>), the minor-to-major axis ratio (<em>q</em><sub>L</sub>), and the orientation angle (<em>\u03b8</em><sub>L</sub>; counter-clockwise from west). Although the object is at the edge of the nearby cluster, to model the lens an external shear is included in the model. It is described by the shear strength (<em>\u03b3</em>) and the shear angle (<em>\u03b8</em><sub><em>\u03b3</em></sub>; counter-clockwise from west). The search for the best-fitting parameters of the lens is done using the <span class=\"small-caps\">emcee</span> code (Foreman-Mackey et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjlab0aebbib30\" id=\"fnref-apjlab0aebbib30\">2013</a>), which implements the Markov Chain Monte Carlo technique to sample the posterior probability density function of the model parameters.</p><p>The magnification factor, <em>\u03bc</em>, is calculated as the ratio between the total flux density of the sources, as measured in the SP within the region of S/N\u00a0\u2265\u00a03, and the flux density of the corresponding image in the IP. The uncertainty on the magnification factor is derived by computing <em>\u03bc</em> 1000 times, perturbing each time the lens model parameters around their best-fitting values.</p><p>The modeling is carried out on the reduced ACS + F606w image, with a pixel scale of 0<img src=\"https://cdn.iopscience.com/icons/Entities/farcs.gif\" alt=\"farcs\" align=\"bottom\" />06. A noise map is constructed from the provided weight map and the PSF is obtained by median combining three unsaturated stars in the vicinity of the target. The lens is subtracted from the image after fitting its light profile with GALFIT (Peng et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjlab0aebbib14\" id=\"fnref-apjlab0aebbib14\">2002</a>).</p><p>The best-fitting SIE model has <span xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"inline-eqn\"><span class=\"tex\"><span class=\"texImage\"><img The lens is an elliptical galaxy (<em>M</em><sub><em>V</em></sub>\u00a0~\u00a0\u221221) at <em>z</em>\u00a0=\u00a00.556, while the lensed source is an LBG at <em>z</em>\u00a0=\u00a03.03. Modeling of the lens shows that the Einstein Cross is well reproduced by an SIE model with an Einstein ring of 0.76 arcsec and that includes the shear effect due to the foreground massive low-redshift cluster of galaxies. The lensed source is magnified by a factor 4.5. This gravitational lens is similar to the case reported by Bolton et al. (<a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjlab0aebbib2\" id=\"fnref-apjlab0aebbib2\">2006</a>) for J1011+0143. This is the second case of an Einstein Cross gravitational lens produced by a distant LBG. The intrinsic Ly<em>\u03b1</em> luminosity, taking into account the magnification factor, is <em>L</em>(Ly<em>\u03b1</em>)\u00a0=\u00a05\u00a0<b>&#x00d7;</b>\u00a010<sup>42</sup> erg s<sup>\u22121</sup>, a factor ~2 higher than that found by Bolton et al. (<a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjlab0aebbib2\" id=\"fnref-apjlab0aebbib2\">2006</a>) for J1011+0143. The Ly<em>\u03b1</em> luminosity is close to <em>L</em>* of the luminosity function of high-redshift Ly<em>\u03b1</em> emitters (see e.g., Sobral et al. <a xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" class=\"cite\" href=\"#apjlab0aebbib18\" id=\"fnref-apjlab0aebbib18\">2018</a>).</p></div> <div class=\"article-text\" data-mobile-collapse=\"\"><p>We thank Mario Radovich, Simona Paiano, and Aldo Treves for useful discussions.
