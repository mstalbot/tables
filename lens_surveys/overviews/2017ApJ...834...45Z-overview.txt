The first SGs were noted some 50&ndash;60 years ago (Arp <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa4d1abib2\">1966</xref>, see also Zwicky <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa4d1abib54\">1956</xref>), and then in earnest around the early 1980s (Malin &amp; Carter <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa4d1abib32\">1980</xref>), and have been then studied observationally, analytically, and by numerical simulations (e.g., Malin &amp; Carter <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa4d1abib33\">1983</xref>; Quinn <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa4d1abib41\">1984</xref>; Athanassoula &amp; Bosma <xref rid=\"apjaa4d1abib3\" ref-type=\"bibr\">1985</xref>; Schweizer &amp; Ford <xref rid=\"apjaa4d1abib43\" ref-type=\"bibr\">1985</xref>; Dupraz &amp; Combes <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa4d1abib16\">1986</xref>; Hernquist &amp; Quinn <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa4d1abib25\">1988</xref>, and references therein). Significantly improved computer power in recent years has become particularly useful for simulating such galaxies with greater detail (e.g., Cooper et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa4d1abib14\">2011</xref>; Ebrov&aacute; et al. <xref rid=\"apjaa4d1abib18\" ref-type=\"bibr\">2012</xref>; Ebrova <xref rid=\"apjaa4d1abib19\" ref-type=\"bibr\">2013</xref>), generating renewed interest in these systems (see also Canalizo et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa4d1abib11\">2007</xref>; Sikkema et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa4d1abib46\">2007</xref>; Bennert et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa4d1abib5\">2008</xref>; Foster et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa4d1abib22\">2014</xref>).</p>\n<p>The shells are a particular tidal feature that forms as a result of an interaction between two galaxies (for a recent review of SGs, see Ebrova <xref rid=\"apjaa4d1abib19\" ref-type=\"bibr\">2013</xref> and references therein), in particular a highly radial, minor merger (Quinn <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa4d1abib41\">1984</xref>, but see also Hernquist &amp; Spergel <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa4d1abib26\">1992</xref>). The shells consist of stars stripped by the interaction, oscillating in the system&rsquo;s potential well and forming faint envelopes near the turnaround radii (e.g., Dupraz &amp; Combes <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa4d1abib16\">1986</xref>; Hernquist &amp; Quinn <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa4d1abib25\">1988</xref>). Shells are relatively common around elliptical galaxies (at least 10&percnt; show shells, e.g., Malin &amp; Carter <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa4d1abib33\">1983</xref>; Athanassoula &amp; Bosma <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa4d1abib3\">1985</xref>; Ebrova <xref rid=\"apjaa4d1abib19\" ref-type=\"bibr\">2013</xref>), but are quite rare around spiral or disk galaxies (cf. Schweizer &amp; Seitzer <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa4d1abib44\">1988</xref>; Fardal et al. <xref rid=\"apjaa4d1abib21\" ref-type=\"bibr\">2007</xref>; Foster et al. <xref rid=\"apjaa4d1abib22\" ref-type=\"bibr\">2014</xref>). Despite being seen mostly around elliptical galaxies, most shells have been observed in the field rather than in clusters of galaxies (e.g., Malin &amp; Carter <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa4d1abib33\">1983</xref>; Athanassoula &amp; Bosma <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa4d1abib3\">1985</xref>). This is likely a result of various factors, primarily the low cross-section for small impact parameter galaxy encounters within the cluster (<inline-formula>\n<tex-math>\n<?CDATA $\\pi {r}_{\\mathrm{core}}^{2}$?>\n</tex-math>\n<!-- <mml:math>\n<mml:mi>&pi;</mml:mi>\n<mml:msubsup>\n<mml:mrow>\n<mml:mi>r</mml:mi>\n</mml:mrow>\n<mml:mrow>\n<mml:mi>core</mml:mi>\n</mml:mrow>\n<mml:mrow>\n<mml:mn>2</mml:mn>\n</mml:mrow>\n</mml:msubsup>\n</mml:math> -->\n<inline-graphic xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" xlink:href=\"apjaa4d1aieqn5.gif\"/>\n</inline-formula>, where <italic>r</italic><sub>core</sub> is the typical galaxy&rsquo;s core size), the collisionlessness of dark matter and stars, combined with high encounter velocities which lower the chances for merger within the cluster. In addition, it is conceivable the intra-cluster light may also play a role in smoothing the shell structure in clusters so it becomes harder to observe due to lack of contrast.</p>\n<p>The number of shells and distance between them can shed light on the interaction or merger history of the two galaxies, as in each passage of the smaller galaxy at the host&rsquo;s center (Gu et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa4d1abib23\">2013</xref>), more material is stripped to form an expanding front (e.g., Quinn <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa4d1abib41\">1984</xref>; Ebrova <xref rid=\"apjaa4d1abib19\" ref-type=\"bibr\">2013</xref>). The shape of the shell, especially in the case of narrow cones, adds useful information that can be then used to tighten the constraints on the initial configuration, relative masses, and velocities (Hernquist &amp; Quinn <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa4d1abib25\">1988</xref>; Ebrov&aacute; et al. <xref rid=\"apjaa4d1abib18\" ref-type=\"bibr\">2012</xref>), although significant degeneracies exist. Also, color information and gradients, if seen, might add information relevant for a population synthesis of the shell stars and the system&rsquo;s history (e.g., Sikkema et al. <xref rid=\"apjaa4d1abib46\" ref-type=\"bibr\">2007</xref>; B&iacute;lek et al. <xref rid=\"apjaa4d1abib7\" ref-type=\"bibr\">2016</xref>).</p>\n<p>Here, we present an SG system caught relatively early on, so that only one highly symmetric shell is seen on each side of the system, where the distance of the shell on one side is half the distance on the other side, and the two interacting galaxies are both still observed (see also B&iacute;lek et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa4d1abib7\">2016</xref>). The SG is formed in a massive galaxy cluster, MACS J1319.9&plus;7003 (hereafter M1319, <inline-formula>\n<tex-math>\n<?CDATA $z=0.33;$?>\n</tex-math>\n<!-- <mml:math>\n<mml:mi>z</mml:mi>\n<mml:mo>&equals;</mml:mo>\n<mml:mn>0.33</mml:mn>\n<mml:mo>;</mml:mo>\n</mml:math> -->\n<inline-graphic xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" xlink:href=\"apjaa4d1aieqn6.gif\"/>\n</inline-formula> Ebeling et al. <xref rid=\"apjaa4d1abib17\" ref-type=\"bibr\">2010</xref>; Mantz et al. <xref rid=\"apjaa4d1abib34\" ref-type=\"bibr\">2010</xref>; also known as Abell 1722; Abell et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa4d1abib1\">1989</xref>), where, as mentioned, SGs are generally considered less common.</p>\n<p>The system was identified in the framework of our ongoing effort (e.g., Zitrin &amp; Broadhurst <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa4d1abib49\">2016</xref>) to lens-model massive clusters with available <italic>Hubble Space Telescope </italic>(<italic>HST</italic>) imaging, toward the launch of the <italic>James Webb Space Telescope</italic> (<italic>JWST</italic>). Since one of the main goals of <italic>JWST</italic> is to target galaxies in the era of reionization, strong lensing (SL) by galaxy clusters will be of increasing importance for detecting the faintest, highest-redshift galaxies. In addition, M1319 has another interesting aspect due to its high ecliptic latitude, where both the infrared (IR) background is &sim;1.3 mag fainter (Windhorst et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa4d1abib48\">2011</xref>) and dust extinction from our Galaxy is minimized, so it may become a good candidate for high-redshift searches in the future. Here we map the projected mass distribution of M1319, from measurements of two strongly lensed galaxies we identify and measure below. The model is made publicly available for future studies of matter distributions in clusters (Meneghetti et al. <xref rid=\"apjaa4d1abib37\" ref-type=\"bibr\">2014</xref>; Donahue et al. <xref rid=\"apjaa4d1abib15\" ref-type=\"bibr\">2016</xref>; Umetsu et al. <xref rid=\"apjaa4d1abib47\" ref-type=\"bibr\">2016</xref>), lensed background sources or lensing-efficiency measurements (Coe et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa4d1abib13\">2015</xref>; Lotz et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa4d1abib31\">2016</xref>), but also, if so desired, it can be used to numerically simulate the details and environment of the SG with greater detail.</p>\n<p>The presented SG, although rare, supplies an interesting example of how galaxies can merge, evolve, and lose their material to the intra-cluster medium (e.g Edwards et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa4d1abib20\">2016</xref>), warranting further study (see also Gu et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa4d1abib23\">2013</xref>). However, despite the interesting case of the SG, note that we will only present and give its basic characteristics, and the paper mainly concentrates on the SL properties of the cluster, so that we leave a detailed examination (and possibly numerical simulation) of the SG system to other dedicated work.</p>\n<p>The paper is organized as follows. In Section <xref ref-type=\"sec\" rid=\"apjaa4d1as2\">2</xref> we review the different observations, and show the spectra of the identified multiply imaged galaxies. In Section <xref ref-type=\"sec\" rid=\"apjaa4d1as3\">3</xref> we construct a mass model for the cluster and summarize its properties. In Section <xref ref-type=\"sec\" rid=\"apjaa4d1as4\">4</xref> we summarize and discuss the results, including an estimate of the occurrence of SGs in galaxy clusters. (<xref rid=\"apjaa4d1abib50\" ref-type=\"bibr\">2009</xref>; see also Broadhurst et al. <xref rid=\"apjaa4d1abib9\" ref-type=\"bibr\">2005</xref>; Zitrin et al. <xref rid=\"apjaa4d1abib52\" ref-type=\"bibr\">2015b</xref>). Full details can be found in these papers. Here we describe the method with brevity.</p>\n<p>We start with the cluster galaxies, chosen by following the red-sequence in a color&ndash;magnitude diagram. Each member galaxy is parametrized as a power-law mass density distribution, with a weight in proportion to its luminosity, and the superposition of all galaxies makes the total galaxy component of the model. The power-law exponent is the same for all galaxies and is a free parameter of the model. This mass density map is then smoothed with a 2D Gaussian, whose width is also a free parameter of the model, to obtain the smooth dark matter component (this is why this method is referred to as LTM&mdash;both the galaxy and dark matter component follow the light). The two components are then combined with a relative weight&mdash;the third free parameter of the method, which along with the overall normalization, brings the number of free parameters to four. A two-parameter external shear is usually also added to allow further flexibility, and to improve the fit further we sometimes allow single bright galaxies to be freely weighted in the minimization and deviate from the nominal mass-to-light ratio adopted (in our case, only the brightest cluster galaxy (BCG) is left to be freely weighted). We also leave the ellipticity of the BCG a free parameter.</p>\n<p>We first ran a model fixing the redshift of system 1 to <italic>z</italic>&nbsp;=&nbsp;1.55 as indicated by our MOSFIRE data (Figure <xref ref-type=\"fig\" rid=\"apjaa4d1af2\">2</xref>), but allowing the redshift of system 2 to vary given the line identification in this system was ambiguous. Nonetheless, while larger lenses may be more efficient, also somewhat smaller lensing clusters such as M1319 are worth observing, and can usefully magnify faint background sources with&mdash;in this case&mdash;the advantage that most moderately magnified regions lie well within <italic>HST</italic>&rsquo;s (and <italic>JWST</italic>&rsquo;s) near-infrared cameras.</p>\n<p>Another main motivation to studying this massive cluster followed the detection of the SG. We now estimate approximately the chances of seeing such a system forming in a galaxy cluster. To form such a symmetric well-aligned SG, the encounter should occur with an impact parameter of the scale of the host&rsquo;s core. This renders the cross section (<inline-formula>\n<tex-math>\n<?CDATA ${\\sigma }_{\\mathrm{SG}}=\\pi {r}_{\\mathrm{core}}^{2}$?>\n</tex-math>\n<!-- <mml:math>\n<mml:msub>\n<mml:mrow>\n<mml:mi>&sgr;</mml:mi>\n</mml:mrow>\n<mml:mrow>\n<mml:mi>SG</mml:mi>\n</mml:mrow>\n</mml:msub>\n<mml:mo>&equals;</mml:mo>\n<mml:mi>&pi;</mml:mi>\n<mml:msubsup>\n<mml:mrow>\n<mml:mi>r</mml:mi>\n</mml:mrow>\n<mml:mrow>\n<mml:mi>core</mml:mi>\n</mml:mrow>\n<mml:mrow>\n<mml:mn>2</mml:mn>\n</mml:mrow>\n</mml:msubsup>\n</mml:math> -->\n<inline-graphic xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" xlink:href=\"apjaa4d1aieqn40.gif\"/>\n</inline-formula>) for such a configuration of order kpc<sup>2</sup> (adopting a galaxy core radius of <inline-formula>\n<tex-math>\n<?CDATA $\\sim 0.5\\mbox{--}1$?>\n</tex-math>\n<!-- <mml:math>\n<mml:mo>&sim;</mml:mo>\n<mml:mn>0.5</mml:mn>\n<mml:mo>&ndash;</mml:mo>\n<mml:mn>1</mml:mn>\n</mml:math> -->\n<inline-graphic xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" xlink:href=\"apjaa4d1aieqn41.gif\"/>\n</inline-formula> kpc). However, we only need to account for the fraction of galaxy pairs with low enough relative velocities. We assume a (radial) velocity dispersion of 1000 km s<sup>&minus;1</sup> and account only for velocities&mdash;with respect to the mean velocity&mdash;lower than the escape velocity from the SG host which we take as 200 km s<sup>&minus;1</sup>. This yields to first-order approximation <inline-formula>\n<tex-math>\n<?CDATA ${(200/1000)}^{3}\\sim 1 \\% $?>\n</tex-math>\n<!-- <mml:math>\n<mml:msup>\n<mml:mrow>\n<mml:mo stretchy=\"true\">(</mml:mo>\n<mml:mn>200</mml:mn>\n<mml:mrow>\n<mml:mo stretchy=\"true\">/</mml:mo>\n</mml:mrow>\n<mml:mn>1000</mml:mn>\n<mml:mo stretchy=\"true\">)</mml:mo>\n</mml:mrow>\n<mml:mrow>\n<mml:mn>3</mml:mn>\n</mml:mrow>\n</mml:msup>\n<mml:mo>&sim;</mml:mo>\n<mml:mn>1</mml:mn>\n<mml:mo>&percnt;</mml:mo>\n</mml:math> -->\n<inline-graphic xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" xlink:href=\"apjaa4d1aieqn44.gif\"/>\n</inline-formula> of the galaxies (or an order of magnitude less, if actually counting only the possible pairs rather then approximating as above). Assuming the dissipation timescale of the shells, i.e., the timeframe in which the shells can be observed after having formed, is of order Gyr, in total we get that the chance of seeing an SG is of order one in a few dozen to one in a few hundred massive clusters. Note that we neglected the mass distribution of cluster galaxies and did not demand certain mass ratios. Note also that the estimate is susceptible to different assumptions, especially the galaxy core radius (affecting the cross section per galaxy) or escape velocity, that within a reasonable value range can easily change the estimate by an order of magnitude. Overall, this calculation shows why SGs are rare in clusters (note however that we do not refer to the BCG in our estimate here, for which other assumptions may apply). A search for SGs in archival <italic>HST</italic> imaging of other massive clusters would be interesting, to confront and reassess this estimate.</p>\n<p>We can exploit our mass model&rsquo;s best-fit <italic>M/L</italic> scaling relation for cluster galaxies to estimate the masses of the SG. Our mass model suggests a total mass of <inline-formula>\n<tex-math>\n<?CDATA $\\sim 1.3\\times {10}^{11}$?>\n</tex-math>\n<!-- <mml:math>\n<mml:mo>&sim;</mml:mo>\n<mml:mn>1.3</mml:mn>\n<mml:mo>&times;</mml:mo>\n<mml:msup>\n<mml:mrow>\n<mml:mn>10</mml:mn>\n</mml:mrow>\n<mml:mrow>\n<mml:mn>11</mml:mn>\n</mml:mrow>\n</mml:msup>\n</mml:math> -->\n<inline-graphic xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" xlink:href=\"apjaa4d1aieqn45.gif\"/>\n</inline-formula> <inline-formula>\n<tex-math>\n<?CDATA ${M}_{\\odot }$?>\n</tex-math>\n<!-- <mml:math>\n<mml:msub>\n<mml:mrow>\n<mml:mi>M</mml:mi>\n</mml:mrow>\n<mml:mrow>\n<mml:mo>&CircleDot;</mml:mo>\n</mml:mrow>\n</mml:msub>\n</mml:math> -->\n<inline-graphic xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" xlink:href=\"apjaa4d1aieqn46.gif\"/>\n</inline-formula> for the SG system, yielding a mass-to-light ratio of <inline-formula>\n<tex-math>\n<?CDATA $M/{L}_{B}\\sim 15;$?>\n</tex-math>\n<!-- <mml:math>\n<mml:mi>M</mml:mi>\n<mml:mrow>\n<mml:mo stretchy=\"true\">/</mml:mo>\n</mml:mrow>\n<mml:msub>\n<mml:mrow>\n<mml:mi>L</mml:mi>\n</mml:mrow>\n<mml:mrow>\n<mml:mi>B</mml:mi>\n</mml:mrow>\n</mml:msub>\n<mml:mo>&sim;</mml:mo>\n<mml:mn>15</mml:mn>\n<mml:mo>;</mml:mo>\n</mml:math> -->\n<inline-graphic xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" xlink:href=\"apjaa4d1aieqn47.gif\"/>\n</inline-formula> a typical value for cluster galaxies. The luminosities, or magnitudes, of the SG host (F814W&nbsp;=&nbsp;20.61 AB) and companion (F814W&nbsp;=&nbsp;22.50 AB) suggest a minor merger of mass ratio of roughly 10:1. Clearly, this is an upper limit as some stars of the companion are already distributed to the shells, so it was somewhat more massive to begin with than its current luminosity suggests. While we leave detailed modeling of this system to future work, from the mere fact that both the host and progenitor are still observed, and that only two shells are seen, one of them half the distance of the other from the center, it is immediately implied that this a relatively young system compared to the expected merger timescale for this mass ratio (typically of order several Gyr, see for example Boylan-Kolchin et al. <xref rid=\"apjaa4d1abib8\" ref-type=\"bibr\">2008</xref>; Jiang et al. <xref rid=\"apjaa4d1abib28\" ref-type=\"bibr\">2008</xref>; Lotz et al. <xref rid=\"apjaa4d1abib30\" ref-type=\"bibr\">2008</xref>; Ebrova <xref rid=\"apjaa4d1abib19\" ref-type=\"bibr\">2013</xref>). Indeed, new generations of numerical simulations are now capable of simulating complex SG systems with high resolution (Cooper et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa4d1abib14\">2011</xref>; Ebrov&aacute; et al. <xref rid=\"apjaa4d1abib18\" ref-type=\"bibr\">2012</xref>). Given the rarity of SGs in massive clusters, and our public mass model, it might be interesting to dedicatedly simulate this system in future work.</p>\n</sec> </body>\n<back>\n<ack>\n<p>AZ thanks the reviewer of this work for useful comments. Our analysis reveals a modest lens, with an effective Einstein radius of <inline-formula>\n<tex-math>\n<?CDATA ${\\theta }_{e}(z=2)=12\\pm 1^{\\prime\\prime} $?>\n</tex-math>\n<!-- <mml:math>\n<mml:msub>\n<mml:mrow>\n<mml:mi>&theta;</mml:mi>\n</mml:mrow>\n<mml:mrow>\n<mml:mi>e</mml:mi>\n</mml:mrow>\n</mml:msub>\n<mml:mo stretchy=\"true\">(</mml:mo>\n<mml:mi>z</mml:mi>\n<mml:mo>&equals;</mml:mo>\n<mml:mn>2</mml:mn>\n<mml:mo stretchy=\"true\">)</mml:mo>\n<mml:mo>&equals;</mml:mo>\n<mml:mn>12</mml:mn>\n<mml:mo>&PlusMinus;</mml:mo>\n<mml:mn>1</mml:mn>\n<mml:mo accent=\"false\">&Prime;</mml:mo>\n</mml:math> -->\n<inline-graphic xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" xlink:href=\"apjaa4d1aieqn1.gif\"/>\n</inline-formula>, enclosing <inline-formula>\n<tex-math>\n<?CDATA $2.1\\pm 0.3\\times {10}^{13}$?>\n</tex-math>\n<!-- <mml:math>\n<mml:mn>2.1</mml:mn>\n<mml:mo>&PlusMinus;</mml:mo>\n<mml:mn>0.3</mml:mn>\n<mml:mo>&times;</mml:mo>\n<mml:msup>\n<mml:mrow>\n<mml:mn>10</mml:mn>\n</mml:mrow>\n<mml:mrow>\n<mml:mn>13</mml:mn>\n</mml:mrow>\n</mml:msup>\n</mml:math> -->\n<inline-graphic xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" xlink:href=\"apjaa4d1aieqn2.gif\"/>\n</inline-formula> <italic>M</italic><sub>&CircleDot;</sub>. We briefly discuss the SL properties of the cluster, using two different modeling techniques (see the text for details), and make the mass models publicly available (<ext-link ext-link-type=\"uri\" xlink:href=\"ftp://wise-ftp.tau.ac.il/pub/adiz/MACS1319/\">ftp://wise-ftp.tau.ac.il/pub/adiz/MACS1319/</ext-link>). The first SGs were noted some 50&ndash;60 years ago (Arp <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa4d1abib2\">1966</xref>, see also Zwicky <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa4d1abib54\">1956</xref>), and then in earnest around the early 1980s (Malin &amp; Carter <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa4d1abib32\">1980</xref>), and have been then studied observationally, analytically, and by numerical simulations (e.g., Malin &amp; Carter <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa4d1abib33\">1983</xref>; Quinn <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa4d1abib41\">1984</xref>; Athanassoula &amp; Bosma <xref rid=\"apjaa4d1abib3\" ref-type=\"bibr\">1985</xref>; Schweizer &amp; Ford <xref rid=\"apjaa4d1abib43\" ref-type=\"bibr\">1985</xref>; Dupraz &amp; Combes <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa4d1abib16\">1986</xref>; Hernquist &amp; Quinn <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa4d1abib25\">1988</xref>, and references therein). Significantly improved computer power in recent years has become particularly useful for simulating such galaxies with greater detail (e.g., Cooper et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa4d1abib14\">2011</xref>; Ebrov&aacute; et al. <xref rid=\"apjaa4d1abib18\" ref-type=\"bibr\">2012</xref>; Ebrova <xref rid=\"apjaa4d1abib19\" ref-type=\"bibr\">2013</xref>), generating renewed interest in these systems (see also Canalizo et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa4d1abib11\">2007</xref>; Sikkema et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa4d1abib46\">2007</xref>; Bennert et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa4d1abib5\">2008</xref>; Foster et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa4d1abib22\">2014</xref>).</p>\n<p>The shells are a particular tidal feature that forms as a result of an interaction between two galaxies (for a recent review of SGs, see Ebrova <xref rid=\"apjaa4d1abib19\" ref-type=\"bibr\">2013</xref> and references therein), in particular a highly radial, minor merger (Quinn <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa4d1abib41\">1984</xref>, but see also Hernquist &amp; Spergel <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa4d1abib26\">1992</xref>). The shells consist of stars stripped by the interaction, oscillating in the system&rsquo;s potential well and forming faint envelopes near the turnaround radii (e.g., Dupraz &amp; Combes <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa4d1abib16\">1986</xref>; Hernquist &amp; Quinn <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa4d1abib25\">1988</xref>). Shells are relatively common around elliptical galaxies (at least 10&percnt; show shells, e.g., Malin &amp; Carter <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa4d1abib33\">1983</xref>; Athanassoula &amp; Bosma <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa4d1abib3\">1985</xref>; Ebrova <xref rid=\"apjaa4d1abib19\" ref-type=\"bibr\">2013</xref>), but are quite rare around spiral or disk galaxies (cf. Schweizer &amp; Seitzer <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa4d1abib44\">1988</xref>; Fardal et al. <xref rid=\"apjaa4d1abib21\" ref-type=\"bibr\">2007</xref>; Foster et al. <xref rid=\"apjaa4d1abib22\" ref-type=\"bibr\">2014</xref>). Despite being seen mostly around elliptical galaxies, most shells have been observed in the field rather than in clusters of galaxies (e.g., Malin &amp; Carter <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa4d1abib33\">1983</xref>; Athanassoula &amp; Bosma <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa4d1abib3\">1985</xref>). This is likely a result of various factors, primarily the low cross-section for small impact parameter galaxy encounters within the cluster (<inline-formula>\n<tex-math>\n<?CDATA $\\pi {r}_{\\mathrm{core}}^{2}$?>\n</tex-math>\n<!-- <mml:math>\n<mml:mi>&pi;</mml:mi>\n<mml:msubsup>\n<mml:mrow>\n<mml:mi>r</mml:mi>\n</mml:mrow>\n<mml:mrow>\n<mml:mi>core</mml:mi>\n</mml:mrow>\n<mml:mrow>\n<mml:mn>2</mml:mn>\n</mml:mrow>\n</mml:msubsup>\n</mml:math> -->\n<inline-graphic xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" xlink:href=\"apjaa4d1aieqn5.gif\"/>\n</inline-formula>, where <italic>r</italic><sub>core</sub> is the typical galaxy&rsquo;s core size), the collisionlessness of dark matter and stars, combined with high encounter velocities which lower the chances for merger within the cluster. In addition, it is conceivable the intra-cluster light may also play a role in smoothing the shell structure in clusters so it becomes harder to observe due to lack of contrast.</p>\n<p>The number of shells and distance between them can shed light on the interaction or merger history of the two galaxies, as in each passage of the smaller galaxy at the host&rsquo;s center (Gu et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa4d1abib23\">2013</xref>), more material is stripped to form an expanding front (e.g., Quinn <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa4d1abib41\">1984</xref>; Ebrova <xref rid=\"apjaa4d1abib19\" ref-type=\"bibr\">2013</xref>). The shape of the shell, especially in the case of narrow cones, adds useful information that can be then used to tighten the constraints on the initial configuration, relative masses, and velocities (Hernquist &amp; Quinn <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa4d1abib25\">1988</xref>; Ebrov&aacute; et al. <xref rid=\"apjaa4d1abib18\" ref-type=\"bibr\">2012</xref>), although significant degeneracies exist. Also, color information and gradients, if seen, might add information relevant for a population synthesis of the shell stars and the system&rsquo;s history (e.g., Sikkema et al. <xref rid=\"apjaa4d1abib46\" ref-type=\"bibr\">2007</xref>; B&iacute;lek et al. <xref rid=\"apjaa4d1abib7\" ref-type=\"bibr\">2016</xref>).</p>\n<p>Here, we present an SG system caught relatively early on, so that only one highly symmetric shell is seen on each side of the system, where the distance of the shell on one side is half the distance on the other side, and the two interacting galaxies are both still observed (see also B&iacute;lek et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa4d1abib7\">2016</xref>). The SG is formed in a massive galaxy cluster, MACS J1319.9&plus;7003 (hereafter M1319, <inline-formula>\n<tex-math>\n<?CDATA $z=0.33;$?>\n</tex-math>\n<!-- <mml:math>\n<mml:mi>z</mml:mi>\n<mml:mo>&equals;</mml:mo>\n<mml:mn>0.33</mml:mn>\n<mml:mo>;</mml:mo>\n</mml:math> -->\n<inline-graphic xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" xlink:href=\"apjaa4d1aieqn6.gif\"/>\n</inline-formula> Ebeling et al. <xref rid=\"apjaa4d1abib17\" ref-type=\"bibr\">2010</xref>; Mantz et al. <xref rid=\"apjaa4d1abib34\" ref-type=\"bibr\">2010</xref>; also known as Abell 1722; Abell et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa4d1abib1\">1989</xref>), where, as mentioned, SGs are generally considered less common.</p>\n<p>The system was identified in the framework of our ongoing effort (e.g., Zitrin &amp; Broadhurst <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa4d1abib49\">2016</xref>) to lens-model massive clusters with available <italic>Hubble Space Telescope </italic>(<italic>HST</italic>) imaging, toward the launch of the <italic>James Webb Space Telescope</italic> (<italic>JWST</italic>). Since one of the main goals of <italic>JWST</italic> is to target galaxies in the era of reionization, strong lensing (SL) by galaxy clusters will be of increasing importance for detecting the faintest, highest-redshift galaxies. In addition, M1319 has another interesting aspect due to its high ecliptic latitude, where both the infrared (IR) background is &sim;1.3 mag fainter (Windhorst et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa4d1abib48\">2011</xref>) and dust extinction from our Galaxy is minimized, so it may become a good candidate for high-redshift searches in the future. Here we map the projected mass distribution of M1319, from measurements of two strongly lensed galaxies we identify and measure below. The model is made publicly available for future studies of matter distributions in clusters (Meneghetti et al. <xref rid=\"apjaa4d1abib37\" ref-type=\"bibr\">2014</xref>; Donahue et al. <xref rid=\"apjaa4d1abib15\" ref-type=\"bibr\">2016</xref>; Umetsu et al. <xref rid=\"apjaa4d1abib47\" ref-type=\"bibr\">2016</xref>), lensed background sources or lensing-efficiency measurements (Coe et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa4d1abib13\">2015</xref>; Lotz et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa4d1abib31\">2016</xref>), but also, if so desired, it can be used to numerically simulate the details and environment of the SG with greater detail.</p>\n<p>The presented SG, although rare, supplies an interesting example of how galaxies can merge, evolve, and lose their material to the intra-cluster medium (e.g Edwards et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa4d1abib20\">2016</xref>), warranting further study (see also Gu et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa4d1abib23\">2013</xref>). However, despite the interesting case of the SG, note that we will only present and give its basic characteristics, and the paper mainly concentrates on the SL properties of the cluster, so that we leave a detailed examination (and possibly numerical simulation) of the SG system to other dedicated work.</p>\n<p>The paper is organized as follows. In Section <xref ref-type=\"sec\" rid=\"apjaa4d1as2\">2</xref> we review the different observations, and show the spectra of the identified multiply imaged galaxies. In Section <xref ref-type=\"sec\" rid=\"apjaa4d1as3\">3</xref> we construct a mass model for the cluster and summarize its properties. In Section <xref ref-type=\"sec\" rid=\"apjaa4d1as4\">4</xref> we summarize and discuss the results, including an estimate of the occurrence of SGs in galaxy clusters. These data include imaging in four bands from <italic>HST</italic> programs 10266 and 10491 (PI: Ebeling) available through the Hubble Legacy Archive: a F606W image (total exposure time 1200 s), taken on 2005 November 04, and a F814W image (total exposure time 1440 s), taken on 2011 January 22, with the ACS/WFC; and F110W and F140W, 705.88 s each, taken on 2011 July 17 with the WFC3/IR.</p>\n<p>We ran SExtractor (Bertin &amp; Arnouts <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa4d1abib6\">1996</xref>) in dual-mode to obtain the photometry of objects in the cluster field, useful for identifying multiply imaged galaxies as well as red-sequence cluster members (Section <xref ref-type=\"sec\" rid=\"apjaa4d1as3\">3</xref>). We then used the resulting catalogs as input and run the Bayesian Photometric Redshift program (Ben&iacute;tez <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa4d1abib4\">2000</xref>; Coe et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa4d1abib12\">2006</xref>) to derive photometric redshifts, especially examining multiple-image candidates.</p>\n<p>We observed the cluster field with the Multi-Object Spectrometer For Infra-Red Exploration (MOSFIRE; McLean et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa4d1abib35\">2012</xref>) on the Keck I telescope, for approximately half an hour, consisting of sets of 120 s exposures, on 2015 June 10, placing a slit along the SG system, and on multiple-images 1.1 and 2.2 seen in Figure <xref ref-type=\"fig\" rid=\"apjaa4d1af1\">1</xref>. (<xref rid=\"apjaa4d1abib50\" ref-type=\"bibr\">2009</xref>; see also Broadhurst et al. <xref rid=\"apjaa4d1abib9\" ref-type=\"bibr\">2005</xref>; Zitrin et al. <xref rid=\"apjaa4d1abib52\" ref-type=\"bibr\">2015b</xref>). Full details can be found in these papers. Here we describe the method with brevity.</p>\n<p>We start with the cluster galaxies, chosen by following the red-sequence in a color&ndash;magnitude diagram. Each member galaxy is parametrized as a power-law mass density distribution, with a weight in proportion to its luminosity, and the superposition of all galaxies makes the total galaxy component of the model. The power-law exponent is the same for all galaxies and is a free parameter of the model. This mass density map is then smoothed with a 2D Gaussian, whose width is also a free parameter of the model, to obtain the smooth dark matter component (this is why this method is referred to as LTM&mdash;both the galaxy and dark matter component follow the light). The two components are then combined with a relative weight&mdash;the third free parameter of the method, which along with the overall normalization, brings the number of free parameters to four. A two-parameter external shear is usually also added to allow further flexibility, and to improve the fit further we sometimes allow single bright galaxies to be freely weighted in the minimization and deviate from the nominal mass-to-light ratio adopted (in our case, only the brightest cluster galaxy (BCG) is left to be freely weighted). We also leave the ellipticity of the BCG a free parameter.</p>\n<p>We first ran a model fixing the redshift of system 1 to <italic>z</italic>&nbsp;=&nbsp;1.55 as indicated by our MOSFIRE data (Figure <xref ref-type=\"fig\" rid=\"apjaa4d1af2\">2</xref>), but allowing the redshift of system 2 to vary given the line identification in this system was ambiguous. (<xref rid=\"apjaa4d1abib36\" ref-type=\"bibr\">2016</xref>).</p>\n</caption>\n<graphic id=\"apjaa4d1af3_eps\" content-type=\"print\" xlink:href=\"apjaa4d1af3.eps\"/>\n<graphic id=\"apjaa4d1af3_lr\" content-type=\"low\" xlink:href=\"apjaa4d1af3_lr.jpg\"/>\n<graphic id=\"apjaa4d1af3_hr\" content-type=\"high\" xlink:href=\"apjaa4d1af3_hr.jpg\"/>\n</fig>\n<fig id=\"apjaa4d1af4\" position=\"float\">\n<label>Figure 4.</label>\n<caption id=\"apjaa4d1afc4\">\n<p>The SG in color&ndash;magnitude space. The figure shows four different color&ndash;magnitude diagrams from photometric catalogs generated for the central field of M1319. We plot all objects (blue open circles) cross-matched between the different bands in the central 1&farcm;5&nbsp;&times;&nbsp;1&farcm;5 field. The SG (filled red) lies exactly on the top of the easily identifiable cluster-member red sequence, leaving little doubt it is indeed a cluster member. Future spectroscopic redshifts will help to confirm this assumption.</p>\n</caption>\n<graphic id=\"apjaa4d1af4_eps\" content-type=\"print\" xlink:href=\"apjaa4d1af4.eps\"/>\n<graphic id=\"apjaa4d1af4_lr\" content-type=\"low\" xlink:href=\"apjaa4d1af4_lr.jpg\"/>\n<graphic id=\"apjaa4d1af4_hr\" content-type=\"high\" xlink:href=\"apjaa4d1af4_hr.jpg\"/>\n</fig>\n<p>Note that the final rms of our pipeline is often somewhat higher than in other schemes: the LTM model, and for self-consistency purposes also the fully parametric PIEMDeNFW model, are in practice constructed on a grid, whose resolution is, for speed-up purposes, comparable to or somewhat lower than that of <italic>HST</italic>. In significant magnification regions the round-up of the average source position to the grid&rsquo;s lower resolution pixel scale introduces a finite, non-negligible rms error of order 0&farcs;1 per system, contributing quite significantly to the global, quoted imprecision of the model (but, importantly, without harming its reliability nor prediction power). These points have been recently emphasized in more length in a community effort to compare lens-modeling techniques to simulated clusters (Meneghetti et al. <xref rid=\"apjaa4d1abib36\" ref-type=\"bibr\">2016</xref>), and we refer the interested reader to that work for more discussion to this end.<xref ref-type=\"fn\" rid=\"apjaa4d1afn2\"><sup>5</sup></xref>\n<fn id=\"apjaa4d1afn2\">\n<label><sup>5</sup></label>\n<p>Also note we aim to improve this numerically in the near future.</p>\n</fn>\n</p>\n</sec>\n<sec id=\"apjaa4d1as4\">\n<label>4.</label>\n<title>DISCUSSION AND SUMMARY</title>\n<p>M1319 is a massive galaxy cluster, with an X-ray inferred mass of <inline-formula>\n<tex-math>\n<?CDATA ${M}_{500}=4.8\\pm 0.9\\times {10}^{14}$?>\n</tex-math>\n<!-- <mml:math>\n<mml:msub>\n<mml:mrow>\n<mml:mi>M</mml:mi>\n</mml:mrow>\n<mml:mrow>\n<mml:mn>500</mml:mn>\n</mml:mrow>\n</mml:msub>\n<mml:mo>&equals;</mml:mo>\n<mml:mn>4.8</mml:mn>\n<mml:mo>&PlusMinus;</mml:mo>\n<mml:mn>0.9</mml:mn>\n<mml:mo>&times;</mml:mo>\n<mml:msup>\n<mml:mrow>\n<mml:mn>10</mml:mn>\n</mml:mrow>\n<mml:mrow>\n<mml:mn>14</mml:mn>\n</mml:mrow>\n</mml:msup>\n</mml:math> -->\n<inline-graphic xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" xlink:href=\"apjaa4d1aieqn34.gif\"/>\n</inline-formula> <inline-formula>\n<tex-math>\n<?CDATA ${M}_{\\odot }$?>\n</tex-math>\n<!-- dispersion of &sim;1000 km s<sup>&minus;1</sup>, in good agreement with its weak-lensing (WL) measurement, suggesting <inline-formula>\n<tex-math>\n<?CDATA ${\\sigma }_{\\mathrm{WL}}=1160\\pm 140$?>\n</tex-math>\n<!-- <mml:math>\n<mml:msub>\n<mml:mrow>\n<mml:mi>&sgr;</mml:mi>\n</mml:mrow>\n<mml:mrow>\n<mml:mi>WL</mml:mi>\n</mml:mrow>\n</mml:msub>\n<mml:mo>&equals;</mml:mo>\n<mml:mn>1160</mml:mn>\n<mml:mo>&PlusMinus;</mml:mo>\n<mml:mn>140</mml:mn>\n</mml:math> rid=\"apjaa4d1abib27\">2002</xref>). Naturally, not all massive clusters have SL regions in proportion to their overall mass. To maximize the SL properties, great importance lies in how the matter is distributed within the cluster, for example, its concentration (Broadhurst et al. <xref rid=\"apjaa4d1abib10\" ref-type=\"bibr\">2008</xref>) and elongation along the line of sight (e.g., Hennawi et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa4d1abib24\">2007</xref>; Sereno et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa4d1abib45\">2010</xref>; Merten et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa4d1abib38\">2015</xref>), or alternatively, if there are substantial mass clumps and/or effective ellipticity boosting the critical area and lensing cross section (e.g., Redlich et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa4d1abib42\">2012</xref>; Zitrin et al. <xref rid=\"apjaa4d1abib53\" ref-type=\"bibr\">2013</xref>).</p>\n<p>Part of the motivation for our work here is to systematically map cluster lenses with archival <italic>HST</italic> imaging, so that the best cosmic telescopes can be designated before the launch of <italic>JWST</italic>. M1319 lies at high ecliptic latitude where the zodiacal IR background is low, which might be beneficial for <italic>JWST</italic> studies of high-redshift galaxies. For <inline-formula>\n<tex-math>\n<?CDATA ${z}_{s}=15$?>\n</tex-math>\n<!-- <mml:math>\n<mml:msub>\n<mml:mrow>\n<mml:mi>z</mml:mi>\n</mml:mrow>\n<mml:mrow>\n<mml:mi>s</mml:mi>\n</mml:mrow>\n</mml:msub>\n<mml:mo>&equals;</mml:mo>\n<mml:mn>15</mml:mn>\n</mml:math> -->\n<inline-graphic xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" xlink:href=\"apjaa4d1aieqn37.gif\"/>\n</inline-formula>, for example, we find an effective Einstein radius of <inline-formula>\n<tex-math>\n<?CDATA ${\\theta }_{e}(z=15)\\simeq 16^{\\prime\\prime} $?>\n</tex-math>\n<!-- <mml:math>\n<mml:msub>\n<mml:mrow>\n<mml:mi>&theta;</mml:mi>\n</mml:mrow>\n<mml:mrow>\n<mml:mi>e</mml:mi>\n</mml:mrow>\n</mml:msub>\n<mml:mo stretchy=\"true\">(</mml:mo>\n<mml:mi>z</mml:mi>\n<mml:mo>&equals;</mml:mo>\n<mml:mn>15</mml:mn>\n<mml:mo stretchy=\"true\">)</mml:mo>\n<mml:mo>&simeq;</mml:mo>\n<mml:mn>16</mml:mn>\n<mml:mo accent=\"false\">&Prime;</mml:mo>\n</mml:math> -->\n<inline-graphic xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" xlink:href=\"apjaa4d1aieqn38.gif\"/>\n</inline-formula>, enclosing <inline-formula>\n<tex-math>\n<?CDATA $3.1\\times {10}^{13}$?>\n</tex-math>\n<!-- <mml:math>\n<mml:mn>3.1</mml:mn>\n<mml:mo>&times;</mml:mo>\n<mml:msup>\n<mml:mrow>\n<mml:mn>10</mml:mn>\n</mml:mrow>\n<mml:mrow>\n<mml:mn>13</mml:mn>\n</mml:mrow>\n</mml:msup>\n</mml:math> -->\n<inline-graphic xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" xlink:href=\"apjaa4d1aieqn39.gif\"/>\n</inline-formula> <italic>M</italic><sub>&CircleDot;</sub>. This is a relatively small lens size compared to other massive clusters (MACS clusters in particular, e.g., Zitrin &amp; Broadhurst <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa4d1abib49\">2016</xref>, or those selected for the Hubble Frontier Fields program, see Lotz et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa4d1abib31\">2016</xref>), so that our analysis reveals M1319 is perhaps not in the top class of lensing clusters. Nonetheless, while larger lenses may be more efficient, also somewhat smaller lensing clusters such as M1319 are worth observing, and can usefully magnify faint background sources with&mdash;in this case&mdash;the advantage that most moderately magnified regions lie well within <italic>HST</italic>&rsquo;s (and <italic>JWST</italic>&rsquo;s) near-infrared cameras.</p>\n<p>Another main motivation to studying this massive cluster followed the detection of the SG. We now estimate approximately the chances of seeing such a system forming in a galaxy cluster. To form such a symmetric well-aligned SG, the encounter should occur with an impact parameter of the scale of the host&rsquo;s core. This renders the cross section (<inline-formula>\n<tex-math>\n<?CDATA ${\\sigma }_{\\mathrm{SG}}=\\pi {r}_{\\mathrm{core}}^{2}$?>\n</tex-math>\n<!-- <mml:math>\n<mml:msub>\n<mml:mrow>\n<mml:mi>&sgr;</mml:mi>\n</mml:mrow>\n<mml:mrow>\n<mml:mi>SG</mml:mi>\n</mml:mrow>\n</mml:msub>\n<mml:mo>&equals;</mml:mo>\n<mml:mi>&pi;</mml:mi>\n<mml:msubsup>\n<mml:mrow>\n<mml:mi>r</mml:mi>\n</mml:mrow>\n<mml:mrow>\n<mml:mi>core</mml:mi>\n</mml:mrow>\n<mml:mrow>\n<mml:mn>2</mml:mn>\n</mml:mrow>\n</mml:msubsup>\n</mml:math> -->\n<inline-graphic xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" xlink:href=\"apjaa4d1aieqn40.gif\"/>\n</inline-formula>) for such a configuration of order kpc<sup>2</sup> (adopting a galaxy core radius of <inline-formula>\n<tex-math>\n<?CDATA $\\sim 0.5\\mbox{--}1$?>\n</tex-math>\n<!-- <mml:math>\n<mml:mo>&sim;</mml:mo>\n<mml:mn>0.5</mml:mn>\n<mml:mo>&ndash;</mml:mo>\n<mml:mn>1</mml:mn>\n</mml:math> -->\n<inline-graphic xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" xlink:href=\"apjaa4d1aieqn41.gif\"/>\n</inline-formula> kpc). The resulting mean free path before such an event, <inline-formula>\n<tex-math>\n<?CDATA $l=1/(n{\\sigma }_{\\mathrm{SG}})$?>\n</tex-math>\n<!-- <mml:math>\n<mml:mi>l</mml:mi>\n<mml:mo>&equals;</mml:mo>\n<mml:mn>1</mml:mn>\n<mml:mrow>\n<mml:mo stretchy=\"true\">/</mml:mo>\n</mml:mrow>\n<mml:mo stretchy=\"true\">(</mml:mo>\n<mml:mi>n</mml:mi>\n<mml:msub>\n<mml:mrow>\n<mml:mi>&sgr;</mml:mi>\n</mml:mrow>\n<mml:mrow>\n<mml:mi>SG</mml:mi>\n</mml:mrow>\n</mml:msub>\n<mml:mo stretchy=\"true\">)</mml:mo>\n</mml:math> -->\n<inline-graphic xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" xlink:href=\"apjaa4d1aieqn42.gif\"/>\n</inline-formula>, where <italic>n</italic> is the number density of galaxies which we take as &sim;1000 Mpc<sup>&minus;3</sup>, a typical thumb number for massive clusters, comes out to be of order <inline-formula>\n<tex-math>\n<?CDATA $l\\sim 1/({10}^{-2})\\sim 1000$?>\n</tex-math>\n<!-- <mml:math>\n<mml:mi>l</mml:mi>\n<mml:mo>&sim;</mml:mo>\n<mml:mn>1</mml:mn>\n<mml:mrow>\n<mml:mo stretchy=\"true\">/</mml:mo>\n</mml:mrow>\n<mml:mo stretchy=\"true\">(</mml:mo>\n<mml:msup>\n<mml:mrow>\n<mml:mn>10</mml:mn>\n</mml:mrow>\n<mml:mrow>\n<mml:mo>&minus;</mml:mo>\n<mml:mn>2</mml:mn>\n</mml:mrow>\n</mml:msup>\n<mml:mo stretchy=\"true\">)</mml:mo>\n<mml:mo>&sim;</mml:mo>\n<mml:mn>1000</mml:mn>\n</mml:math> -->\n<inline-graphic xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" xlink:href=\"apjaa4d1aieqn43.gif\"/>\n</inline-formula> Mpc. In contrast, the radius of massive clusters is of order &sim;1 Mpc, including that of M1319 (Mantz et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa4d1abib34\">2010</xref>), which means each galaxy has an order of a tenth of a percent to become an SG, in each crossing of the cluster (in general the crossing time is of order Gyr so that only a few crossings per galaxy are expected). To obtain the chance a cluster would produce an SG we need to multiply by the number of galaxies in the cluster for which we take a nominal 1000 galaxies per cluster. However, we only need to account for the fraction of galaxy pairs with low enough relative velocities. We assume a (radial) velocity dispersion of 1000 km s<sup>&minus;1</sup> and account only for velocities&mdash;with respect to the mean velocity&mdash;lower than the escape velocity from the SG host which we take as 200 km s<sup>&minus;1</sup>. This yields to first-order approximation <inline-formula>\n<tex-math>\n<?CDATA ${(200/1000)}^{3}\\sim 1 \\% $?>\n</tex-math>\n<!-- <mml:math>\n<mml:msup>\n<mml:mrow>\n<mml:mo stretchy=\"true\">(</mml:mo>\n<mml:mn>200</mml:mn>\n<mml:mrow>\n<mml:mo stretchy=\"true\">/</mml:mo>\n</mml:mrow>\n<mml:mn>1000</mml:mn>\n<mml:mo stretchy=\"true\">)</mml:mo>\n</mml:mrow>\n<mml:mrow>\n<mml:mn>3</mml:mn>\n</mml:mrow>\n</mml:msup>\n<mml:mo>&sim;</mml:mo>\n<mml:mn>1</mml:mn>\n<mml:mo>&percnt;</mml:mo>\n</mml:math> -->\n<inline-graphic xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" xlink:href=\"apjaa4d1aieqn44.gif\"/>\n</inline-formula> of the galaxies (or an order of magnitude less, if actually counting only the possible pairs rather then approximating as above). Assuming the dissipation timescale of the shells, i.e., the timeframe in which the shells can be observed after having formed, is of order Gyr, in total we get that the chance of seeing an SG is of order one in a few dozen to one in a few hundred massive clusters. Note that we neglected the mass distribution of cluster galaxies and did not demand certain mass ratios. Note also that the estimate is susceptible to different assumptions, especially the galaxy core radius (affecting the cross section per galaxy) or escape velocity, that within a reasonable value range can easily change the estimate by an order of magnitude. Overall, this calculation shows why SGs are rare in clusters (note however that we do not refer to the BCG in our estimate here, for which other assumptions may apply). A search for SGs in archival <italic>HST</italic> imaging of other massive clusters would be interesting, to confront and reassess this estimate.</p>\n<p>We can exploit our mass model&rsquo;s best-fit <italic>M/L</italic> scaling relation for cluster galaxies to estimate the masses of the SG. Our mass model suggests a total mass of <inline-formula>\n<tex-math>\n<?CDATA $\\sim 1.3\\times {10}^{11}$?>\n</tex-math>\n<!-- <mml:math>\n<mml:mo>&sim;</mml:mo>\n<mml:mn>1.3</mml:mn>\n<mml:mo>&times;</mml:mo>\n<mml:msup>\n<mml:mrow>\n<mml:mn>10</mml:mn>\n</mml:mrow>\n<mml:mrow>\n<mml:mn>11</mml:mn>\n</mml:mrow>\n</mml:msup>\n</mml:math> -->\n<inline-graphic xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" xlink:href=\"apjaa4d1aieqn45.gif\"/>\n</inline-formula> <inline-formula>\n<tex-math>\n<?CDATA ${M}_{\\odot }$?>\n</tex-math>\n<!-- <mml:math>\n<mml:msub>\n<mml:mrow>\n<mml:mi>M</mml:mi>\n</mml:mrow>\n<mml:mrow>\n<mml:mo>&CircleDot;</mml:mo>\n</mml:mrow>\n</mml:msub>\n</mml:math> -->\n<inline-graphic xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" xlink:href=\"apjaa4d1aieqn46.gif\"/>\n</inline-formula> for the SG system, yielding a mass-to-light ratio of <inline-formula>\n<tex-math>\n<?CDATA $M/{L}_{B}\\sim 15;$?>\n</tex-math>\n<!-- <mml:math>\n<mml:mi>M</mml:mi>\n<mml:mrow>\n<mml:mo stretchy=\"true\">/</mml:mo>\n</mml:mrow>\n<mml:msub>\n<mml:mrow>\n<mml:mi>L</mml:mi>\n</mml:mrow>\n<mml:mrow>\n<mml:mi>B</mml:mi>\n</mml:mrow>\n</mml:msub>\n<mml:mo>&sim;</mml:mo>\n<mml:mn>15</mml:mn>\n<mml:mo>;</mml:mo>\n</mml:math> -->\n<inline-graphic xmlns:xlink=\"http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink\" xlink:href=\"apjaa4d1aieqn47.gif\"/>\n</inline-formula> a typical value for cluster galaxies. The luminosities, or magnitudes, of the SG host (F814W&nbsp;=&nbsp;20.61 AB) and companion (F814W&nbsp;=&nbsp;22.50 AB) suggest a minor merger of mass ratio of roughly 10:1. Clearly, this is an upper limit as some stars of the companion are already distributed to the shells, so it was somewhat more massive to begin with than its current luminosity suggests. While we leave detailed modeling of this system to future work, from the mere fact that both the host and progenitor are still observed, and that only two shells are seen, one of them half the distance of the other from the center, it is immediately implied that this a relatively young system compared to the expected merger timescale for this mass ratio (typically of order several Gyr, see for example Boylan-Kolchin et al. <xref rid=\"apjaa4d1abib8\" ref-type=\"bibr\">2008</xref>; Jiang et al. <xref rid=\"apjaa4d1abib28\" ref-type=\"bibr\">2008</xref>; Lotz et al. <xref rid=\"apjaa4d1abib30\" ref-type=\"bibr\">2008</xref>; Ebrova <xref rid=\"apjaa4d1abib19\" ref-type=\"bibr\">2013</xref>). Indeed, new generations of numerical simulations are now capable of simulating complex SG systems with high resolution (Cooper et al. <xref ref-type=\"bibr\" rid=\"apjaa4d1abib14\">2011</xref>; Ebrov&aacute; et al. <xref rid=\"apjaa4d1abib18\" ref-type=\"bibr\">2012</xref>). Given the rarity of SGs in massive clusters, and our public mass model, it might be interesting to dedicatedly simulate this system in future work.</p>\n</sec> </body>\n<back>\n<ack>\n<p>AZ thanks the reviewer of this work for useful comments.
